Numerals

Unverified Content

The RFC 2119 normative framing (MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, MAY) is not present in the source material. The source uses informal language throughout. The RECOMMENDED and MUST claims in this introduction go beyond what the source states; the source simply says "it deserves recommendation to write digits instead of number words."

This section specifies the forms, inflection, agreement rules, and usage conventions for all numeral categories in Interslavic. All normative statements use MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, and MAY as defined in RFC 2119.

Because numeral forms differ considerably across the individual Slavic languages — including lexically unique forms such as Russian sorok "forty" and devjanosto "ninety" — it is RECOMMENDED to write numerals as Arabic digits in written Interslavic wherever possible, so as to avoid misunderstanding. Nevertheless, the spoken and inflected forms remain necessary, and speakers MUST know how to combine numerals with the nouns they govern.

Cardinal Numerals​

Unverified Content

The structural classification into "simple," "compound," and "composite" numerals, and the further division by meaning into "definite cardinals," "fractional numerals," "collective numerals," and "indefinite cardinals," is not found in the source material.

Cardinal numerals in Interslavic are divided by structure into simple (osm "eight", sto "hundred"), compound (petdeset "fifty", devetdeset "ninety"), and composite (devet sot trideset sedm "nine hundred thirty-seven"). By meaning they are further divided into definite cardinals (treated in the relevant section), fractional numerals , collective numerals , and indefinite cardinals .

Composite numbers and word order​

Composite numbers are always formed from high to low: thousands — hundreds — tens — units. The word i "and" MAY be inserted between tens and units: tri-tyseč četyristo petdeset (i) šest "3 456".

For agreement of composite numbers with their dependent nouns, the rightmost component governs the agreement (see the relevant section).

Numeral–Noun Agreement​

The case and number form of a noun following a cardinal numeral depends on the value of the numeral. Agreement patterns differ sharply between the numerals 1–4 and all higher numerals.

1: full agreement with noun​

The numeral jedin "one" agrees with its noun in gender, number, and case, exactly as an adjective does. Examples:

  • jedin dobry prijatelj "one good friend" (masc. sg.)
  • jedna mlada žena "one young woman" (fem. sg.)
  • jedno malo děte "one small child" (neut. sg.)
  • jedne sinje džinsy "one pair of jeans" (plurale tantum)

The numeral jedin is not restricted to its purely numerical sense; it is also used in the meanings "a certain", "lone", "individual", "single", making it even more adjective-like in behaviour.

2–4: nominative plural of noun​

The numerals dva, tri, četyri, oba, and obydva govern the nominative plural of the following noun (and its modifying adjective) in the nominative and accusative cases. In oblique cases, these numerals agree with the noun in case. Examples:

  • dva věrni drugi "two faithful friends" (nom.)
  • tri lětnje měsěce "three summer months" (nom.)
  • četyri dolge dni "four long days" (nom.)

In the genitive and other oblique cases:

  • dvoh věrnyh drugov "of two faithful friends"
  • trěh lětnjih měsěcev "of three summer months"

Ordinal Numerals​

Ordinal numerals indicate the position of an item in a sequence. Grammatically they are adjectives and MUST agree with the noun they modify in gender, number, and case.

All ordinals except trětji are declined as hard adjectives (dobry-type; see the relevant section). The ordinal trětji follows the soft adjectival declension (svěži-type; see the relevant section).

Collective numerals SHOULD be used instead of cardinal numerals in the following situations:

  1. When the group consists of both men and women: dva studenti "two male students", dvě studentky "two female students", dvoje studentov "two students (mixed)".
  2. With gender-neutral words like ljudi and děti: osmero ljudij "eight people", četvero dětij "four children".
  3. With neuter words denoting young animals (singular -e, plural -eta): troje telet "three calves".
  4. For objects that naturally come in pairs: dvoje rukavic "two gloves".
  5. With pluralia tantum (words existing only in the plural): dvoje dverij "two doors".

Differential (generic) numerals​

Differential numerals indicate qualitative differentiation — "of different types" or "of how many kinds". The older form uses the collective stem directly: dvoj, troj (declined like the possessive pronoun moj).

The more common form uses the suffix -aky:

When adverbialised, the meaning becomes "in X ways": trojako "in three ways".

Fractions​

For the word "half", Interslavic uses pol (indeclinable) or its full noun form polovina / polovica (declined as a feminine noun). Pol MAY also be used as a prefix: pol-mrtvy "half-dead".

All other fractions are formed by replacing the ordinal ending -y with -ina:

1/3tretjina
1/4četvrtina
1/5petina
1/6šestina

1/8osmina
1/9devetina
1/10desetina
1/16šestnadsetina
1/100sotina or stotina
Unverified Content

The source gives the form as sedm trideseti-osmin (with the genitive form trideseti-), not sedm trideset-osmin as written here.

These fraction nouns are feminine and are declined as second-declension nouns. Thus "7/38" is sedm trideset-osmin (the hyphen enhances clarity).

Ordinal-based fractions (e.g., dvě trětje "two thirds") are also used, reflecting the pattern common across most Slavic languages.

Substantivised numerals​

When a numeral is used as a noun (referring to a digit, a bus line, a school grade, a playing card, a banknote, etc.), the following forms apply:

For 2–4, the collective numeral stem plus -ka is used: dvojka "a two", trojka "a three", četverka "a four".

For all other numerals, -ka is appended to the cardinal form: jedinka "a one", petka "a five", šestka "a six", etc.

Unverified Content

The claim that "not all forms in -ica are equally established" and that "forms in -ka are preferred" is not stated in the source material. The source simply presents both suffixes as alternatives.

Alternatively, the suffix -ica MAY be used: jedinica, petica, šestica, sedmica, etc. Not all forms in -ica are equally established; forms in -ka are preferred.
