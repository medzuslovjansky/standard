Word Formation and Derivation

This section specifies the productive morphological processes by which new words are formed in Interslavic: suffixation, prefixation, zero derivation, and compounding. All normative rules use the terms MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, and MAY as defined in RFC 2119.

Productive Noun-Forming Suffixes​

Agent Nouns: -nik, -telj, -ec, -ač​

Interslavic possesses four productive agentive suffixes for deriving person nouns from verb and noun bases.

Suffix -telj derives agent nouns from verb stems, denoting the person who habitually or professionally performs the action:

BaseDerived formGloss
učiti "to teach"učitelj"teacher"
pisati "to write"pisatelj"writer"
vladati "to rule"vladatelj"ruler"

The feminine counterpart of -telj nouns is formed with -nica.

Suffix -ač derives agent nouns from verb stems, with a focus on trades and crafts:

BaseDerived formGloss
kovati "to forge"kovač"blacksmith"
krojiti "to cut (cloth)"krojač"tailor"

Suffix -ar similarly derives trade nouns from verb stems:

BaseDerived formGloss
pekti "to bake"pekar"baker"

Suffix -nik derives nouns denoting the person in charge of something, handler of something, or the object containing something. The base is a noun:

BaseDerived formGloss
glava "head"glavnik"boss, chief"
kabel "cable"kabelnik"cable technician"
kniga "book"knižnik"bookcase"

Suffix -ec derives agent nouns and nouns denoting persons characterised by an adjective:

BaseDerived formGloss
glupy "stupid"glupec"fool"
izgnany "banished"izgnanec"exile"

Phonological conditioning: When the noun stem ends in k, g, or h, the following alternations apply before -nik, -ka, -ko, -ny, -ok, and -sky:

Stem-final consonantChangeExample
k / c→ čruka → ručny
g→ žkniga → knižka
h→ šmuha → mušji

The soft consonant nj hardens before -nik and -ny.

Diminutives: -ok/-ek, -ik, -ka, -ko​

Diminutives are gender-sensitive and phonologically conditioned. The selection of suffix depends on both the grammatical gender of the base noun and the nature of the final stem consonant:

GenderConditionSuffixExample
MasculineHard stem-ok (gen. -ka)pes "dog" → pesok "doggy"
MasculineSoft stem-ik or -ekmuž "man" → mužik "little man"
FeminineAny-ka (appended to stem)kniga "book" → knižka "booklet"
NeuterAny-ko (appended to stem)polje "field" → poljko "small field"

Suffixes beginning with -o- change to -e- after a soft consonant:

  • byk + -ok → byček

Augmentatives: -išče, -ina​

Suffix -išče may carry augmentative force alongside its place-noun function; context and base determine the primary reading. The suffix -ina is used to form augmentatives and large-instance nouns, as well as abstract nouns of quality or extent.

Suffix -je derives place nouns from prepositional phrases or locative descriptions, often with a prefixal element:

Productive Adjective-Forming Suffixes​

Relational Adjectives: -ny, -sky, -ji​

Three primary suffixes derive relational adjectives from nouns. The choice among them is governed by the semantic class of the base noun:

SuffixConditionExample
-skyGeographical names, nationalities, professionsžena "woman" → žensky "female, feminine"; morje "sea" → morsky "maritime"
-jiAnimals, persons, divine entitiesčlověk "person" → člověčji "human"; konj "horse" → konji "horse's"
-nyAll other casesnoč "night" → nočny "nocturnal"; pismo "letter" → pisemny "written"

Suffixes beginning with -o- change to -e- after a soft consonant:

  • konec + -ovy → koncevy

Softness Preservation at the Root–Suffix Boundary​

When an adjective-forming suffix (especially -sky, -ny) is attached to a root whose final consonant is inherently soft, the softness of that root consonant should in principle be preserved through derivation. This follows the general morphophonological principle that root-internal softness is a property of the lexeme, not of the suffix.

Examples where root softness is preserved:

RootDerived adjectiveRationale
morje (root morj-, where rj = rʲ)morjskyThe rj is an iotated consonant inherent to the root; it persists before -sky (cf. Czech mořský, BCMS morski with soft r)
konj (from PS *konjь)konjskyThe final nj is iotation-derived softness, preserved before -sky (cf. Czech koňský, BCMS konjski)

In the etymological register (MS Plus), where yer-derived softness is explicitly marked, the soft mark persists before suffixes:

Root (MS Plus)Derived adjective (MS Plus)Standard form
svinь- (from svinja, PS *svinьja)svińskysvinsky
Unresolved: Pre-Suffix Softening of S and Z

In MS Plus, certain consonants undergo softening before derivational suffixes such as -sky, -ny, -no, -ba. This is already established for consonants like r (e.g., boŕba) and t (e.g., svat́ba). However, the extension of this pattern to s → ś and z → ź is not consistently applied.

Cases where pre-suffix softening of s and z is expected by analogy with the broader pattern:

RootExpected MS Plus formStandard formComparative evidence
Rus + -skyruśskyrusskyPolish ruski (historically soft s), Russian русский
bliz + -nopribliźnopribliznoCzech přibližně, Russian приблизительно (with soft z)
obraz + -nyobraźnyobraznyRussian образный (with soft z before derivational boundary)
groza + -bagroźbagrozbaAlready attested in the dictionary with soft ź

Note that t and d are partial exceptions: they generally do not soften before most suffixes in the living Slavic languages, except before -ba (e.g., svat́ba).

The trade-offs are:

  1. Extending softening to ś/ź would make the MS Plus system more internally consistent and align with the comparative Slavic evidence.
  2. Not extending it keeps the rule set smaller and avoids introducing distinctions that may not be salient to all Slavic speakers.

A systematic review of all derivational contexts involving s and z before suffixes is needed to determine the correct scope of this rule.

Productive Verb-Forming Suffixes and Processes​

Denominative Verbs: -iti, -ovati​

Suffix -ovati is the primary suffix for deriving verbs from nouns, expressing behaviour or activity associated with the noun:

BaseDerived formGloss
malpa "monkey"malpovati"to act like a monkey"
noč "night"nočevati"to spend the night"

Suffixes beginning with -o- change to -e- after a soft consonant:

  • noč + -ovati → nočevati

Suffix -iti (with prefix o-) is used to form verbs expressing the act of causing someone or something to undergo treatment with what the noun denotes:

BaseDerived formGloss
kamenj "stone"okameniti"to stone; to turn to stone"
světlo "light"osvětliti"to illuminate"
svoboda "freedom"osvoboditi"to set free"

The prefix obez- combined with -iti forms verbs meaning "to deprive of":

BaseDerived formGloss
glava "head"obezglaviti"to decapitate"
dom "house"obezdomiti"to render homeless"

Causative Verbs: -iti from Adjectives​

The prefix o- combined with -iti forms causative verbs from adjectives, expressing "to make something have the quality of the adjective":

BaseDerived formGloss
črny "black"očrniti"to blacken"
krugl "round"okrugliti"to make round"

The prefix u- combined with -iti forms causative verbs from comparatives, expressing "to make more":

BaseDerived formGloss
lěpši "better"ulěpšiti"to improve"
menši "less"umenšiti"to diminish, reduce"

The prefix od- combined with -iti forms verbs expressing removal of a quality:

BaseDerived formGloss
črny "black"odčrniti"to remove the black colour"

Iterative and Habitual Verbs: -yvati/-ivati, -vati​

Prefixed perfective verbs derive imperfective counterparts through systematic suffix replacement. The rule set is as follows:

| Perfective ending | Imperfective suffix | Example | |-------------------|--------------------|---------|| | -ati | -yvati | sodržati → sodrživati "to contain (ipfv)" | | -iti | -jati | obnoviti → obnavjati "to renew (ipfv)" | | -nuti | -ati | oddohnuti → oddyhati "to breathe (ipfv)" | | Monosyllabic + vowel | -vati | prodati → prodavati "to sell (ipfv)" |

Unverified Content

The source states that "the base form of a prefixed verb is always perfective" but does not use normative MUST language. The MUST requirement is an editorial addition.

Normative rule: Prefixation of a bare verb always creates a perfective verb. An imperfective counterpart MUST be derived by one of the suffix-replacement processes above.

Inchoative Verbs: -ěti​

The suffix -ěti forms inchoative verbs from adjectives, expressing the process of becoming:

BaseDerived formGloss
běly "white"bělěti"to turn white"
bogaty "rich"bogatěti"to grow rich"
hvory "sick"hvorěti"to become sick"

Aspectual-Only Prefixes​

Unverified Content

The source says po- is "often used as a perfective marker" but does not state a SHOULD preference for po- over other prefixes. The normative SHOULD language is an editorial addition.

The prefix po- functions as a default perfectivizer for a wide range of verbs, especially when no lexical directional meaning is needed. In this use, po- SHOULD be preferred when no other prefix adequately expresses the aspectual relationship.

The prefix na- (with reflexive se) signals satiation or sufficiency: naspati se "to sleep one's fill"; najesti se "to eat one's fill".

The prefixes prě- and u- are used specifically with adjectives as bases to produce perfective verbs expressing intensification (see the relevant section):

  • prě- + adjective: "very/too much" → prěumorjeny "exhausted"
  • ne- (negative prefix) + adjective: negation → nedobry "not good"

Zero Derivation​

Zero derivation (conversion) is a minor process in Interslavic by which a word changes word class without any overt affixal change.

The primary attested case is the deverbal noun directly from a verb root, where the noun denotes a vehicle, instrument, or container associated with the verbal action:

VerbZero-derived nounGloss
vezti "to carry, to transport"voz"cart, vehicle"

An adjective may also function as a substantivized noun without morphological change: běly "white (adj.)" → běly "a white one (noun)".

Zero derivation is not a productive process in Interslavic and its outputs SHOULD be treated as lexicalized items.

Compounding​

Compound Formation Rules​

Interslavic permits free compounding of two roots to express a new concept. The standard linking element is -o- after hard consonants and -e- after soft consonants:

First elementSecond elementCompoundGloss
voda "water"padati "to fall"vodopad"waterfall"
sam "self"kritika "criticism"samokritika"self-criticism"
myš "mouse"loviti "to catch"myšelovka"mousetrap"
zemja "earth"tresenje "trembling"zemjetresenje"earthquake"

Compound Orthography​

When the first element of a compound is a borrowing from English or another non-Slavic language, the elements MAY be separated by a hyphen (Interslavic: defis):

Latin scriptGloss
rok-muzika"rock music"
veb-stranica"web page"

Native Interslavic compounds are written as a single word without a hyphen.
