Adjectives

This section specifies the inflection, short forms, possessive formation, and degrees of comparison of adjectives in Interslavic. All normative rules use the terms MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, and MAY as defined in RFC 2119.

Adjectival Agreement​

Adjectives in Interslavic MUST agree with the head noun they modify in gender, number, and case. This requirement applies both in attributive position (adjective preceding or following the noun) and in predicative position (adjective linked to the noun by a copula).

Adjectival declension is entirely regular. Three systematic rules govern all paradigms:

  1. Hard vs. soft stem alternations. Adjective stems ending in š, ž, č, or j are soft stems. After soft stems, every -o- becomes -e- and every -y- becomes -i- in the endings (see the relevant section).

  2. Animate masculine accusative. The accusative of an adjective modifying an animate masculine noun MUST be identical to the genitive, both in the singular and in the plural. For inanimate masculines and for all other genders the accusative equals the nominative (singular and plural).

  3. Nominative–accusative syncretism in the plural. Except when modifying animate masculine nouns, the nominative plural and the accusative plural are identical across all genders.

Short (Predicative) Form​

Unresolved: Definite vs. Indefinite Adjective Declension

Lombino (2022) proposes a fully productive distinction between definite (determined) and indefinite (undetermined) adjective forms, modeled on Old Church Slavonic and modern Bulgarian. In this system:

  • Indefinite forms (short forms) are fully declined as nouns: mlad, gen. mlada, dat. mladu, etc., following the corresponding noun declension pattern. They are used predicatively and with indefinite nouns.
  • Definite forms (long forms) carry the standard pronominal endings: mladyj, mladaja, mladoje, etc. They are used when the noun is contextually definite.

Additionally, this system includes a postpositive definite article suffixed with -t- to the noun itself (e.g., bratrŭt "the brother", zemjata "the earth", poljeto "the field"), following the Bulgarian pattern. The article is used only when the noun has no other determiner (adjective, possessive, or demonstrative).

Standard Interslavic preserves only a vestige of this system: the short form in the masculine nominative singular. The question of whether to document the full definite/indefinite distinction as an optional literary feature remains open. The system would increase expressive precision but at a significant cost to learnability, and it is productive only in Bulgarian, Macedonian, and (in part) Old Church Slavonic among Slavic languages.

The East Slavic Long/Short Adjective Distinction​

Beyond the definite/indefinite question discussed above, a separate tension exists regarding the productive grammatical function of long vs. short adjective forms in East Slavic languages. In Russian, the distinction between long forms (добрый, добрая, доброе) and short forms (добр, добра, добро) is not merely stylistic — it is syntactically productive:

  • Short forms are obligatory in certain predicative constructions (e.g., Он болен "He is ill" vs. больной человек "a sick person").
  • Short forms can carry different semantic nuances from long forms (e.g., Он хороший "He is a good person [general quality]" vs. Он хорош "He looks good [at this moment]").
  • Ukrainian and Belarusian each preserve elements of this distinction, though with different distributions: Ukrainian tends to use short forms predicatively while Belarusian shows a mixed pattern.

Standard Interslavic uses long adjective endings (-y, -a, -o in the nominative) as the default in all syntactic positions, with the short form available only as a stylistic option in the masculine nominative singular. The full paradigm of short-form endings across all genders and numbers — which would be needed for a productive short/long distinction — is not part of the standard grammar.

Unresolved: Productive Short Adjective Forms for Eastern Flavorization

East Slavic languages maintain a grammatically productive distinction between long and short adjective forms across all genders, with the short forms obligatory in certain predicative constructions. Interslavic currently treats the short form as vestigial (masculine nominative singular only). Formalizing a full set of short-form predicative adjective endings (-Ø masc., -a fem., -o neut., -y pl.) as an Eastern flavorization option would bring the language closer to East Slavic grammatical expectations, but would add a layer of complexity that most West and South Slavic speakers would find unfamiliar. The scientific notation has explored marking this distinction with special vowel diacritics on adjective endings (long -yj/-ij/-aja/-oje vs. short -y/-i/-a/-o), but no consensus has been reached on whether this should enter any practical register of the language.

Distribution of Short Forms​

Interslavic possesses a vestigial short form of adjectives, a relic of the indefinite (non-pronominal) declension inherited from Common Slavic. The short form differs from the regular (long) form only in the masculine nominative singular, where the ending -y / -i is omitted, leaving a bare stem.

Short forms do not constitute a separate declension class; all oblique forms of short-form adjectives are identical to those of the corresponding long-form adjective. The short form MUST NOT be confused with adverbs, which are also derived from the bare stem (see the relevant section).

The short form occurs productively in two classes of adjective:

  1. Possessive adjectives in -ov and -in : the masculine nominative singular is zero-ended — bratov, mamin — while all other forms add regular long endings.
  2. A small number of other adjectives where tradition preserves the short masculine nominative, e.g. velik alongside veliky.

Short Form in Predicative Position​

The short form MAY be used when the adjective functions as a predicate nominal (i.e., after a copula such as jest "is"):

  • Petr jest ščestliv. — "Peter is happy."
  • Dom jest velik. — "The house is big."

Use of the long form in predicative position is equally acceptable:

  • Petr jest ščestlivy. — "Peter is happy."
  • Dom jest veliky. — "The house is big."

Neither form is obligatory in predicative position; the long form is more common in contemporary usage.

Scope Restriction: Single-Word Possessor Only​

Possessive adjectives in -ov / -ev and -in MUST be used only when the possessor is expressed as a single uninflected word (a simple noun without modifiers or additional words):

  • bratova kniga — "brother's book" (single-word possessor → possessive adjective)
  • kniga mojego brata — "my brother's book" (modified possessor → genitive phrase)
  • Puškinova poezija — "Pushkin's poetry" (single proper name → possessive adjective)
  • poezija Aleksandra Puškina — "Aleksandr Pushkin's poetry" (full name → genitive phrase)

When the possessor is a phrase, a multi-word name, or requires its own inflection, the genitive form of the possessor noun MUST be used instead.

Demonstratives as Quasi-Articles​

Toj / ta / to (Proximal and Distal Demonstratives)​

Interslavic has no grammaticalized definite or indefinite article. The demonstrative pronoun toj (masc.), ta (fem.), to (neut.) — "that; this" — serves as the closest functional equivalent to a definiteness marker.

Anaphoric and Deictic Uses​

Demonstratives in Interslavic serve two functions:

  1. Deictic use: pointing to a referent in the physical or discourse context — toj čelověk "that man (over there)".
  2. Anaphoric use: referring back to a previously mentioned or contextually established referent — Kupil jesm knigu. Ta kniga je interesna. "I bought a book. That/The book is interesting."

Because Interslavic lacks a definite article, the demonstrative in its anaphoric use is the standard means of marking definiteness when contextual clarity requires it. Its use in this function is OPTIONAL; definiteness need not be marked when the context is unambiguous.

Degrees of Comparison​

Adjectives (and the adverbs derived from them; see the relevant section) have three degrees of comparison: positive, comparative, and superlative.

Positive (Base) Form​

The positive degree is the uninflected base form of the adjective, expressing a quality without comparison: dobry "good", prosty "simple", svěži "fresh".

Synthetic Comparative (-ějši / -ejši)​

Literary Register: Alternative Comparative Formation

Lombino (2022) describes an alternative comparative formation used in the rich/literary register, based on iotation (consonant softening) or the suffix -ěj- rather than the standard -š- suffix:

  • Monosyllabic adjectives form the comparative by iotating the final consonant (the same consonant alternation used in verbal derivation: t > ć, d > đ, s > š, etc.): mlad > mlađ/mlađa/mlađe, lěp > lěpj/lěpša.
  • Polysyllabic adjectives add the suffix -ěj-: bogat > bogatěj/bogatěja/bogatěje, jasŭn > jasněj/jasněja/jasněje.
  • Adjectives with -k- or -ok- drop the suffix before comparison: širok > širj, dalek > dalj.

This system is closer to the inherited Common Slavic comparative (preserved in varying degrees across South and West Slavic languages) and avoids the -ějši suffix, which is a Interslavic innovation combining features from multiple language groups. The standard -ějši / -ši forms remain the default for Standard Interslavic.

Lombino also documents an absolute superlative (elative), formed with the standard naj- prefix but used without comparison to express a high degree: Najuvažimji prijateli! ("Most esteemed friends!" --- not the most esteemed of all, but very esteemed).

The primary synthetic comparative is formed by replacing the ending -y / -i with the suffix -ějši (after hard stems) or -ejši (after soft stems, i.e. after š, ž, č, j):

Analytic Comparative (vyše / bolje + positive)​

The analytic comparative is formed by placing vyše or bolje ("more") before the positive form of the adjective or adverb:

  • vyše prosty — "simpler, more simple"
  • bolje sintetičny — "more synthetic"
  • vyše smrdeči — "more stinking"
Unverified Content

The source says the analytic form "is most likely encountered in the case of very long words, foreign words, and participles." The doc upgrades this observation to a normative SHOULD recommendation and adds the criterion "difficult to pronounce or understand," which is not in the source.

The analytic form MAY be used for any adjective or adverb. It SHOULD be preferred for:

  • Long words and words of foreign origin;
  • Participles used as adjectives;
  • Contexts in which the synthetic form would be difficult to pronounce or understand.

The analytic and synthetic forms are equivalent in meaning. In most everyday adjectives, either form is acceptable.

Less-than comparative. The word menje ("less") functions as the analytic marker for the opposite direction:

  • menje prosty — "less simple"
  • menje prosto — "less simply"
Unverified Content

The rules distinguishing comparative adjective vs. adverb usage, the comparison examples with neželi, and the constructions with od and než/neželi are not discussed in the source material.

Adjective vs. adverb in comparisons. When comparing a property of a noun, the comparative adjective (agreeing with the noun) MUST be used. When comparing a property of an action or verb, the comparative adverb is used:

  • Aleksandr je bystrějši, neželi Dmitri. — "Alexander is faster than Dmitri." (adjective)
  • Aleksandr bystrěje běgaje, neželi Dmitri. — "Alexander runs faster than Dmitri." (adverb)

When introducing the standard of comparison, use the genitive with the preposition od "from" or the conjunction než / neželi "than":

  • Ljubov je silnějša od smŕti. — "Love is stronger than death."
  • Goręći čaj je lěpši, než hlådny. — "Hot tea is better than cold."

Superlative (naj- Prefix)​

The superlative is formed by adding the prefix naj- to the comparative form:

ComparativeSuperlative (adj.)Superlative (adv.)
novějšinajnovějšinajnověje
prostějšinajprostějšinajprostěje
vysšinajvysšinajvyše

The prefix naj- MAY also be applied to the analytic comparative:

  • najvyše sintetičny — "most synthetic"
  • najbolje prosty — "simplest, most simple"

As a simplification, naj- MAY be prefixed directly to the positive form of the adjective or adverb (following the pattern of Bulgarian and Macedonian):

  • najnovy — "newest" (adjective)
  • najnovo — "most newly" (adverb)

Least-superlative. The adverb najmenje expresses the opposite extreme ("least"):

  • najmenje prosty — "least simple"
  • najmenje prosto — "least simply"

Excessive degree. The prefix prě- attached to the positive expresses an extreme or excessive degree ("very", "too", "overly"):

  • prěprosty — "too simple, overly simple"
  • prěneprijatny — "extremely unpleasant"

Negative. The prefix ne- creates a negative adjective:

  • neprijatny — "unpleasant, not pleasant"
