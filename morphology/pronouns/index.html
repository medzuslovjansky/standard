Pronouns

This section specifies the forms, inflection, and usage rules for all pronominal categories in Interslavic. All normative statements use MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, and MAY as defined in RFC 2119.

Personal Pronouns​

Paradigm: 1st, 2nd, 3rd person singular and plural​

Personal pronouns in Interslavic inflect for six cases (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, instrumental, locative). There is no vocative for pronouns. The reflexive pronoun sebe inflects identically to the second-person singular but has no nominative form.

Third-person pronouns also distinguish gender (masculine, feminine, neuter in the singular) and animacy (masculine plural only: oni for groups containing male referents, one for all others). The third person is historically derived from demonstrative pronouns and therefore behaves differently from first- and second-person forms.

In the plural, Interslavic maintains two nominative forms: oni is used for groups that include at least one male person or male animal; one is used for all other groups (exclusively female, mixed-gender inanimate, neuter). This distinction is obligatory in the nominative.

Long vs. short (clitic) forms​

Several personal pronouns have two sets of forms: a long (full) form and a short (clitic) form. The clitic forms appear in parentheses in the paradigm above (e.g., mę, tę, go, mi, ti, mu).

The following rules govern their distribution:

  1. The long form MUST be used after a preposition: k mně "to me", za tebe "for you", pri njim "with him".
  2. The long form MUST be used in emphatic or contrastive contexts: Podavali sųt ne mně, a jemu "They served not me but him."
  3. The long form SHOULD be used in sentence-initial position: Mně ješče ne podali sųt obědų "They have not yet served me lunch."
  4. The short clitic form SHOULD be used in all other positions (unstressed, non-initial, not after a preposition): Prinesi mi tų knigų "Bring me that book"; Vidiš li mę? "Do you see me?"

Because not all verb endings are equally transparent to all speakers of Slavic languages, pro-drop (omitting personal pronouns when they are the subject of a clause) is NOT RECOMMENDED. Speakers SHOULD include the subject pronoun explicitly: ja čitaju is preferred over bare čitaju, though the latter is not ungrammatical.

The pronoun on refers to any noun of masculine grammatical gender, not only to male beings. The pronoun ona refers to any noun of feminine grammatical gender.

Optional dual personal pronouns​

The dual personal pronouns are entirely optional and correspond to the optional dual number in nouns and verbs (see the nouns chapter for the full discussion of dual number status). They are based on Roberto Lombino's reconstruction (2021) from Old Church Slavonic and Slovenian models.

Note on ně

The feminine 1du. form ně is an innovation created by analogy with the dva / dvě gender distinction. It is not attested in Old Church Slavonic or any natural Slavic language, where only na or vě occur. It was proposed on the Interslavic server as a logical extension of the system.

Reflexive Pronouns​

Sebe / sę paradigm​

The reflexive pronoun sebe has no nominative form. It does not inflect for person, number, or gender. Its case endings are identical to those of the second-person singular pronoun ty.

CaseLong formShort (clitic) form
Nom.——
Acc.sebesę
Gen.sebe—
Dat.soběsi
Ins.sobojų—
Loc.(o) sobě—

The short clitic sę is used with reflexive verbs: Ja myju sę "I am washing myself." When emphasis is required, the long form MUST be used: Ja myju jedino sebe "I wash nobody but myself." After a preposition, the long form MUST be used: on boji sę sebe.

The absence of number and person inflection in the reflexive pronoun means it can indicate that the action of a transitive verb is directed at the subject regardless of whether that subject is singular or plural.

The reflexive pronoun also serves as a reciprocal pronoun. Oni bijut sę may mean either "They are hitting themselves" or "They are hitting each other." To make the reciprocal reading explicit, the formula jedin drugogo SHOULD be added: Oni bijut sę jedin drugogo.

Reflexive possessive: svoj​

The reflexive possessive svoj "one's own" is used whenever the possessor is coreferential with the grammatical subject of the same clause, regardless of person or number.

Ja myju svoje avto "I am washing my (own) car." My ljubimo svoju zemlju "We love our (own) land." Pjotr dal Ivanu svoju knigu "Pjotr gave Ivan his [= Pjotr's] book." Pjotr dal Ivanu jegovu knigu "Pjotr gave Ivan his [= Ivan's] book."

The contrast above is normatively significant: when the subject in a third-person clause is also the possessor, svoj MUST be used to express that meaning; use of jego / jej / jih unambiguously refers to a possessor distinct from the subject.

Possessive Pronouns​

Possessive pronouns agree with the noun they modify in gender, number, and case. They are inflected like adjectives, with the exception of the masculine nominative singular (and inanimate accusative singular), where the zero ending is used rather than the adjectival -y / -i.

The core possessive pronouns are:

PronounMeaning
mojmy, mine
tvojyour, yours (singular familiar)
našour, ours
vašyour, yours (plural or formal)
svojone's own (reflexive possessive; see the relevant section)

Third-person possessives:

For third-person possession, Interslavic most commonly uses the genitive form of the corresponding personal pronoun, which is uninflected:

FormMeaning
jegohis, its (masc./neut.)
jejher, its (fem.)
jihtheir

These genitive-possessive forms are not declined and do not change to agree with the possessed noun.

Alternatively, the following adjectival possessive forms MAY be used. These are declined like hard adjectives (dobry-type):

FormMeaning
jegov, jegova, jegovohis
jejin, jejina, jejinoher
jihny, jihna, jihnotheir

The genitive forms (jego, jej, jih) are preferred in standard Interslavic. The adjectival forms (jegov, jejin, jihny) are fully acceptable and reflect patterns found in several Slavic literary norms.

Interrogative and indefinite possessives:

The interrogative possessive pronoun čij "whose" and its derived forms ničij "nobody's", něčij "somebody's", etc. are inflected like moj. For the full system of derived possessives see the relevant section (indefinite pronouns) and (correlative table).

Demonstrative Pronouns​

Primary demonstrative: toj / ten​

The primary demonstrative pronoun is toj (masculine), ta (feminine), to (neuter), meaning "this / that". This form SHOULD be used whenever there is no need to distinguish explicitly between proximal "this" and distal "that".

The variant form ten for the masculine nominative singular is used in western Slavic contexts; in other contexts toj is the preferred form.

The plural oblique forms těh, těm, těmi are found particularly in Russian, Bulgarian, and Czech; the variants tyh, tym, tymi are found in other Slavic languages. Both are acceptable in Interslavic.

Proximal vs. distal distinction: tutoj and tamtoj​

When a speaker needs to explicitly distinguish between "this one (here)" and "that one (over there)", Interslavic provides:

  • tutoj "this (near the speaker)"
  • tamtoj "that (over there, distant from both speaker and addressee)"

Both tutoj and tamtoj are declined exactly like toj (see table above). These forms SHOULD be used when spatial deixis needs to be made explicit.

Three-way deictic distinction (archaic/literary)​

A more precise three-way deictic system exists and MAY be used, particularly in literary or more archaic registers:

The pronoun sej has largely disappeared from most modern Slavic languages except in fossilized forms. Its use may therefore not be immediately transparent to all readers. Speakers choosing this system SHOULD ensure context makes meaning clear.

Interrogative and Relative Pronouns​

Kto (who) and čto / što (what)​

The primary interrogative pronouns are kto "who?" and čto (also written što) "what?". These pronouns do not inflect for number or gender.

Unverified Content

The source material mentions the inflection of kto and čto but does not provide the actual case forms in the available text. The specific forms in this table (e.g. kym / kěm, čim) cannot be verified against the source.

Casektočto

Acc.kogočto
Gen.kogočego
Dat.komučemu
Ins.kym / kěmčim
Loc.(o) kom(o) čem

Both kto and čto serve as interrogative pronouns in direct and indirect questions, and as relative pronouns in subordinate clauses. The same uninflected form functions in both roles:

  • Oni sųt besědovali o tom, čto bylo je pisano v gazetah. "They were talking about what had been written in the newspapers."
  • Ja zapytal sòm, u kogo oni sųt kupili ovų rybų. "I asked from whom they bought that fish."

Ktory / koj (which, relative)​

The pronoun ktory "which, that" is the standard relative pronoun. It inflects like a hard adjective (dobry-type) and agrees with its antecedent in gender and number; it takes its case from its function in the relative clause.

The South Slavic alternative koj is inflected like moj and is fully interchangeable with ktory in both relative and interrogative uses.

A third, more archaic option is iže. In the nominative it is invariable across genders and numbers. In oblique cases it is formed from the corresponding form of the personal pronoun on / ona / ono with the suffix -že: genitive masculine singular jegože, dative masculine singular jemuže, etc. This form MAY be used in literary or archaic-register texts.

Double negation requirement​

Unverified Content

The double negation requirement and the constructions equivalent to Russian "некого/нечего" described below are not discussed in the source material.

Negative pronouns MUST co-occur with the verbal negation particle ne. Double negation is grammatically obligatory in Interslavic and is not an error:

  • Nikto ne zna. "Nobody knows."
  • Ničto ne pomaga. "Nothing helps."
  • Nikdě ne byh. "I was nowhere."

Omitting ne when a negative pronoun is present is a grammatical error in Interslavic. This rule aligns with the universal Slavic pattern of negative concord.

Note on constructions equivalent to Russian "некого/нечего":

Interslavic does not have a separate series of pronouns corresponding to Russian некого, нечего, негде (meaning "there is no one to…, there is nothing to…, there is nowhere to…"). The equivalent construction in Interslavic uses a verbal copula in the present tense:

  • Ne jest kogo spytati. "There is no one to ask."
  • Ne jest o čem govoriti. "There is nothing to talk about."
  • Ne jest kdě sę zajmati. "There is nowhere to study."

Pronominal Adverbs and the Correlative System​

Interslavic has a systematic table of correlatives: a set of interrelated pronouns, determiners, and adverbs built on a regular grid of pronominal roots and semantic prefixes/suffixes. The system covers the semantic categories of person (kto / who), thing (čto / what), quality (kaky / what kind), quantity (koliko / how many), possession (čij / whose), selection (koj / which), manner (kak / how), location (kdě / where), direction-to (kamo / whither), path (kųdy / which way), origin (odkųdy / whence), and time (kogda / when).
