Conjunctions and Particles

Unverified Content

The RFC 2119 normative framing (MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, MAY) is an editorial addition. The source material does not use normative language or RFC 2119 terminology.

This section specifies the inventory and usage of conjunctions, particles, clitics, and other uninflected words in Interslavic. All normative rules use the terms MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, and MAY as defined in RFC 2119.

Coordinating Conjunctions​

A coordinating conjunction joins two or more words, phrases, or clauses of equal syntactic weight. Coordinating conjunctions are invariable and stand between the items they conjoin.

The source material lists i, a, and ale but does not elaborate on their semantic differences. The nuance description below is not sourced.

The conjunctions i and a differ in nuance: i introduces a simple additive relation, while a introduces a contrastive or adversative relation ("and yet", "but"). The conjunction ale is more exclusively adversative.

Final Clauses​

Final (purpose) clauses expressing purpose MAY be introduced by že or da followed by the conditional particle by:

  • Turisti posětet Niderlandiju, že by fotografovali tulipany. — "Tourists visit the Netherlands to photograph the tulips."
  • Ona prinosi jabloka, da by děti byli zdrave. — "She brings apples so that the children will be healthy."

The Question Particle li​

Unverified Content

The source material states that li is "placed right after the focus point of the question, usually the verb" but does not use normative MUST language. The MUST constraint is editorial.

The particle li is a sentential clitic used to form yes/no questions. It MUST be placed immediately after the word that carries the focus of the question, typically the finite verb:

  • Kupil li otec knigu? — "Has father bought a book?"

The particle li is unstressed and functions as an enclitic (see the relevant section). When used as a subordinating conjunction introducing an indirect yes/no question, li means "whether, if":

  • Ne věm, kupil li otec knigu. — "I don't know whether father bought a book."

The Question Particle či​

Unverified Content

The source describes či as preceding the sentence but does not specify normative rules about its position or its equivalence with other question constructions in the detail given below.

The particle či introduces a yes/no question by fronting the entire clause. When used to introduce a question, či appears at the very beginning of the sentence:

  • Či otec kupil knigu? — "Has father bought a book?"

As a subordinating conjunction , či introduces indirect yes/no questions meaning "whether":

  • Sprašal jesm go, či on prijde. — "I asked him whether he would come."

A yes/no question MAY alternatively be formed by intonation alone, without any question particle:

  • Otec kupil knigu? — "Father bought a book?"

All three constructions (intonation, či, li) are grammatically equivalent for direct yes/no questions.

The Negative Particle ne​

The particle ne is the primary negation marker. It MUST immediately precede the verb it negates:

  • On ne ide. — "He is not going."
  • Ja ne věm. — "I don't know."

When negative pronouns or adverbs are present in the clause, the negative particle ne before the verb is OBLIGATORY. Double (or multiple) negation is grammatically required in Interslavic:

  • Nikto ne prijde. — "No one will come." (lit. "No one not-will-come")
  • Ničto ne znaju. — "They know nothing."

Omission of ne before the verb when a negative word is present constitutes a grammatical error in Interslavic.

The Optative Particle nehaj​

The particle nehaj is used to form third-person imperatives (jussives). It MUST precede the third-person present-tense verb form:

  • Nehaj on govori! — "Let him speak!"
  • Nehaj ona pride! — "Let her come!"
  • Nehaj živut narodi! — "Let the peoples live!"

The particle nehaj is invariable and does not inflect for person, number, or gender.

Clitics and Clitic Placement​

Clitics are phonologically unstressed words that attach prosodically to a neighboring stressed host. In Interslavic, clitics cluster in second position in the clause (Wackernagel position): after the first stressed constituent of the clause.

Second-Position (Wackernagel) Constraint​

Clitics in Interslavic MUST cluster in second position in the clause: immediately after the first stressed constituent (which may be a word, a phrase, or a fronted subordinate clause). Placement of clitics at any other position in the clause is non-standard.

Example:

  • Ja mu go dal jesm. — "I gave it to him." (clitics mu go jesm in second position, after the subject ja)

Clitic Ordering within the Cluster​

When multiple clitics co-occur, they MUST appear in the following fixed order within the clitic cluster:

  1. Conditional / verbal auxiliary clitic (by, short forms of byti)
  2. Dative pronoun clitic (mi, ti, mu, jej, nam, vam, jim)
  3. Accusative pronoun clitic or reflexive sę (mę, tę, go, ju, nas, vas, jih, sę)

Example:

  • On by mu sę znal. — illustrating conditional > dative > reflexive ordering.

Reflexive sę: Position and Splitting​

The reflexive clitic sę follows the conjugated verb form when the verb is finite. In compound tenses (auxiliary + past active participle), sę MUST follow the auxiliary, not the participle:

  • On sę myje. — "He washes himself." (simple present)
  • On je sę myl. — "He has washed himself." (compound past; sę follows auxiliary je)

Placement of sę directly after the participle (e.g., *On je myl sę) is non-standard.
