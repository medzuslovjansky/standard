Transliteration

Transliteration is the systematic, rule-governed conversion of text from the Latin script to the Cyrillic script, or vice versa, without reference to pronunciation rules beyond what the orthography itself encodes. Because the two standard Interslavic orthographies were designed as mutual mirrors, transliteration between them is nearly always one-to-one at the grapheme level; the few cases requiring contextual rules are enumerated in the relevant section and

Digraph and Cluster Handling in Transliteration​

Three Latin digraphs function as single phoneme units and each maps to a single Cyrillic unit, or vice versa. These MUST be treated atomically — never split — during transliteration.

Latin dž → Cyrillic дж​

The digraph dž (Latin) represents the single affricate d͡ʒ and MUST be rendered as the digraph дж (Cyrillic) in its entirety.

LatinCyrillicGloss
džezджезjazz
džunglaджунглаjungle

Capitalization rule: when dž begins a sentence or proper noun, only the first letter is capitalized: Džez, not DŽez. In all-caps contexts the full digraph is capitalized: DŽEZ / ДЖЕЗ.

Latin lj → Cyrillic љ​

The digraph lj (Latin) represents the palatal lateral ʎ and MUST be rendered as the single Cyrillic letter љ. Conversely, љ (Cyrillic) MUST be rendered as lj (Latin).

LatinCyrillicGloss
ljubovљубовlove
poljeпољеfield

Capitalization rule: at word-initial position, only L is capitalized: Ljubljana / Љубљана. In all-caps: LJUBOV / ЉУБОВ.

Latin nj → Cyrillic њ​

The digraph nj (Latin) represents the palatal nasal ɲ and MUST be rendered as the single Cyrillic letter њ. Conversely, њ (Cyrillic) MUST be rendered as nj (Latin).

njuhatiњухатиto smell

Handling Ambiguous Sequences​

Distinguishing digraphs from letter sequences at morpheme boundaries​

A digraph sequence that spans a morpheme boundary MUST NOT be treated as a single digraph. The following method resolves such ambiguities:

Latin script: A sequence d+ž, l+j, or n+j that crosses a morpheme boundary — for example, the prefix nad- followed by a root beginning with ž — is not a digraph. In standard Latin orthography this ambiguity is rare and normally resolved by context; however, in etymological or careful-register writing a hyphen MAY be inserted at the morpheme boundary to make the split explicit: nad-žiti (to outlive), distinguishing it from any potential nadžiti.

Cyrillic script: Because лj and нj are already single letters Љ / Њ in Cyrillic, and the digraph дж is written as a two-letter sequence, ambiguous clusters involving д+ж across a morpheme boundary MUST be marked with a hyphen when the reading is otherwise unclear: под-жечи (to set fire to, cf. podjěči).

The letter j (Latin) / ј (Cyrillic) after vowels​

In informal Cyrillic writing, the letter ј is sometimes replaced by iotated vowel letters following a vowel:

Normative CyrillicInformal alternativeLatin equivalent
ајайaj
ејейej

ујуйuj
ыјыйyj

These informal alternatives do not affect Latin transliteration: all Cyrillic forms above transliterate uniformly to Latin j following the corresponding vowel. Conversely, when converting Latin to Cyrillic, the normative form with ј MUST be used; the Russian-style forms with й are permitted only as keyboard-fallback alternatives (see of the core orthography spec).

Iotated vowels in informal Cyrillic​

Some writers use iotated vowel letters (ја, је, ји, ју) or pre-iotated letters (я, є/е, и, ю) instead of the sequence ј + vowel. For transliteration purposes these sequences MUST be resolved as follows:

Cyrillic formLatin transliteration
ја or яja
је or є (when = je)je
јиji
ју or юju
јa after a consonant (as soft marker)ja

The normative Cyrillic form for all of the above is the sequence ј + plain vowel letter. Transliteration software SHOULD normalize informal Cyrillic iotated forms to the normative ј-sequence form before outputting Latin.

The Latin–Cyrillic Asymmetry: The lj/nj ↔ љ/њ Problem​

Problem Statement​

The single most debated orthographic issue in Interslavic is the asymmetry between the Latin digraphs lj and nj (two letters each) and the Cyrillic single letters љ and њ. In Latin orthography, the morphological structure of a word like pisanje is transparent: a reader can parse it as pisan- + -je (the stem plus the palatalizing suffix). In Cyrillic, the standard form писање absorbs the suffix-initial ј into the digraph letter њ, making the suffix boundary invisible.

This asymmetry matters because Interslavic has two etymologically distinct sources of nj and lj:

  1. Iotation-derived (Proto-Slavic *nj, *lj — consonant fused with *j): konj "horse," polje "field." Here the nj/lj is genuinely a single fused sound. The Cyrillic њ/љ is the appropriate representation.

  2. Yer-softened + suffix -je (Proto-Slavic *nь + *je, *lь + *je): pisanje "writing" (from *pisańьje), usilje "effort" (from *usiĺьje). Here nj/lj is actually a sequence of a soft consonant and the suffix-initial j — two distinct morphological units that happen to look like the iotation digraph.

In Latin script this ambiguity is manageable because context and the optional etymological diacritics (ń, ĺ) can disambiguate. In Cyrillic, however, both types map to њ/љ, with no way to indicate whether the letter represents a fused iotated sound or a soft consonant + j sequence.

The verbal nouns in -ńje constitute by far the largest open class. Any solution to the asymmetry problem must handle these ~600+ words correctly.

Consequences for Morphological Derivation​

The standard Cyrillic form знање (znanje) is morphologically opaque in a specific way: from it, a reader cannot reliably derive the genitive plural form. Two analyses compete:

  1. If знање is read as a fused њ + е (as in коње from konj), the genitive plural would be znanj (by analogy with konjev).
  2. If знање is read as znań + -je (soft ń + suffix), the genitive plural would be znanij (the -ij ending, parallel to čutij from čutje).

The correct form is znanij, because the underlying structure is znań-je. But this is recoverable only if the reader knows the etymological structure — the standard Cyrillic spelling provides no signal.

Compare: from čutje (standard чутје), the genitive plural čutij is straightforwardly derivable because the ј is visible. But znanje → znanij requires knowing that the њ conceals a morpheme boundary.

The Karadžić Principle and Its Limits​

The Cyrillic letters љ and њ were introduced by Vuk Karadžić as part of his phonemic reform of Serbian orthography, guided by the principle "one sound, one letter" (piši kao što govoriš — "write as you speak"). In Serbian, this principle is fully consistent: љ and њ always represent single fused palatal sounds.

In Interslavic, however, this principle conflicts with the Slovenian model for suffix -je (see the relevant section in Phonological Alternations): the Interslavic -nje in verbal nouns represents ń + j — two morphophonemic units — not a single fused sound. Writing њ for this sequence applies Karadžić's principle to a context where it was never intended, since in Serbian the verbal noun suffix actually does undergo full iotation (e.g., pisanje → Serbian писање with genuinely fused њ).

This tension between the phonemic script principle and morphological transparency is fundamental and has no perfect resolution. The current standardization of љ/њ for both iotation-derived and yer-derived softness prioritizes script consistency and practical readability, at the cost of morphological transparency in Cyrillic.

Solutions Considered​

The following solutions were analysed by the committee. Each addresses a different facet of the problem, and none is without drawbacks:

Solution 1: Require ĺ and ń in standard Latin orthography (before j in verbal nouns, etc.). This makes transliteration consistent (ĺj → љ, nj → њ) and is etymologically the most correct approach. However, it adds a diacritic requirement to the standard orthography (complicating keyboard input and learning), and it renders existing published Latin texts non-standard.

Solution 2: Standardize љ and њ in Cyrillic (absorbing the suffix ј into the digraph letter). This makes transliteration consistent in the other direction and produces Cyrillic text that is intuitive for Russian (знанье → знање), Ukrainian (питання → пытање), and Bulgarian (питане → пытање) readers. Published Cyrillic texts may need re-transliteration, but Latin texts remain unchanged. The cost is reduced morphological transparency in Cyrillic and the genitive plural derivability problem.

Solution 3: Allow both лј/нј and љ/њ as equal variants. This preserves the status quo and makes all existing texts correct. However, it produces a 1

asymmetry (one Latin form → two Cyrillic forms), complicates dictionary normalization, and leaves content creators unable to control which Cyrillic form the transliterator produces.

Solution 4: Change the suffixes to -lije/-nije (closer to Russian -ние/-лие). This eliminates the ambiguity entirely but constitutes a radical change affecting 5000+ words, distances Interslavic from all Slavic languages except Russian and Bulgarian, and renders all existing content obsolete.

Solution 5: Alternative suffix forms (-nie, -nıje, -ние, -ніе, -нѥ, etc.). These were explored and rejected as creating more problems than they solve — effectively the same objections as Solution 4 but worse.

Solution 6: Use a context-aware transliterator with an exception list. This preserves the grammar and existing texts but requires maintaining a list of ~100+ exceptional words, fails on unknown words (particularly neologisms and proper names), and creates inconsistency between different transliterator implementations.

Solution 7: Replace the digraphs nj/lj with diacritic letters ň/ľ everywhere (write koň, voľa, učeňje). This achieves a 1

ň↔њ mapping and solves the transliteration problem, but it is a radical reform of the Latin orthography, is unfamiliar to South Slavic readers, and distances Interslavic from Serbian/Croatian orthographic conventions that inspired the digraph system.

Solution 8: Merge yer-derived and iotation-derived softness into a single system (ĺ/ń everywhere, eliminating the digraphs lj/nj). This achieves the "one sound, one letter" principle but again constitutes a radical reform and is difficult for South Slavic speakers.

Solution with apostrophe: Use an apostrophe as the Latin equivalent of the Cyrillic soft sign ь — e.g., měd' (мєдь), učen'je (ученьје), kost' (кость). This creates a symmetrical 1

Latin↔Cyrillic mapping and is available on all keyboards. However, the apostrophe already has other functions in Slavic orthographies (marking elision in Italian-influenced traditions, marking hard sign in Ukrainian transliteration), and the approach was considered too radical a departure from existing practice.

The Adopted Decision​

The committee adopted Solution 2: standardize љ and њ in Cyrillic (rather than лј and нј) for all positions, including verbal nouns and other forms where the underlying structure is soft consonant + suffix -je. The Latin orthography remains unchanged — writers continue to write pisanje, usilje, with the optional etymological forms pisańje, usiĺje available in MS Plus.

This decision:

  • makes Latin-to-Cyrillic transliteration fully deterministic (nj → њ in all positions);
  • produces Cyrillic text that is natural for East Slavic and Bulgarian readers;
  • preserves all existing Latin texts as standard;
  • follows the precedent of the Slovenian language, where the same morphological situation exists and the orthography does not mark the suffix boundary.

The trade-offs are:

  • morphological transparency in Cyrillic is reduced (the suffix -je is no longer visible in forms like писање);
  • the genitive plural -ij cannot be derived from the standard Cyrillic spelling alone;
  • the etymological distinction between iotation-derived and yer-derived softness is neutralized in Cyrillic.
Exceptions

A small number of words contain the sequences нј or лј across a prefix or compound boundary, where the н/​л and ј belong to different morphemes and must NOT be fused into њ/​љ. Examples: конјунктивны (konjunktivny), конјугација (konjugacija), панјевропејскы (panjevropejsky). In these cases the morpheme boundary prevents fusion, parallel to how it works in Serbian (cf. Serbian ињекција, коњугација).

Practical Implications for Content Creators​

Writers working in the Latin script may continue to use the standard orthography (pisanje) or the etymological orthography (pisańje) without any change. When their text is transliterated to Cyrillic, the result will be писање in both cases — the etymological distinction is not preserved in the Cyrillic transliteration.

Writers working directly in Cyrillic should use њ and љ uniformly, including in verbal nouns. The sequences нј and лј should appear only at morpheme boundaries where the two letters belong to different morphemes (see exceptions above).

Keyboard and Input Considerations​

The letters љ and њ are available on all Serbian, Macedonian, and Montenegrin keyboard layouts. For writers using Russian, Ukrainian, or Bulgarian keyboard layouts (which lack љ and њ), the fallback forms ль and нь are acceptable (see the relevant section in the standard orthography specification).

On the Latin side, the optional etymological letters ĺ/​ľ and ń are available on Slovak keyboard layouts. Czech layouts provide ň and ď/​ť but not ĺ/​ľ. The Interslavic keyboard layouts for both Latin and Cyrillic scripts include all necessary characters.
