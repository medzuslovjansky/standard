Etymological (Scientific) Alphabet

The Interslavic etymological alphabet — historically known as Naučny Medžuslovjansky ("Scientific Interslavic"), also informally called MS Plus or etimologičny pravopis ("etymological orthography") — is an OPTIONAL extension of the standard orthography. It introduces additional diacritical letters that correspond to Proto-Slavic and Old Church Slavonic phonemes that have since diverged or disappeared in most modern Slavic languages.

The etymological alphabet does NOT constitute a separate language or dialect. It is the same Interslavic written with additional diacritics layered on top of the standard spelling. A reader unfamiliar with these diacritics MAY ignore them entirely and still read the text correctly using the standard pronunciation.

Purpose and Register​

The etymological alphabet is intended for use in scholarly, etymological, and high-precision contexts. It SHOULD NOT be used as the default orthography in everyday communication.

Specifically, the etymological alphabet serves the following purposes:

  • conveying etymological information in dictionaries, grammars, and linguistic studies;
  • supporting language learning by making Interslavic words easier to connect to their counterparts in other Slavic languages — the etymological diacritics encode systematic sound correspondences across Slavic languages, so that learning them effectively provides a key to recognising cognates in any natural Slavic language;
  • providing a more precise notation of the ideal (most central) pronunciation;
  • disambiguating homographs — the standard orthography inevitably produces pairs of words that are spelled identically but have different meanings and different etymologies; the etymological alphabet resolves these collisions (see below);
  • enabling flavorization — the adaptation of Interslavic for speakers of a specific Slavic language (see the relevant section);
  • enabling the transcription of any Slavic text into a unified Interslavic orthographic form.

An additional practical consideration motivates the etymological alphabet: the majority of Slavic speakers have an instinct to read words as they are written. In the standard orthography, where fewer distinctions are encoded, it can be difficult for a reader to determine the recommended pronunciation of an unfamiliar word. The etymological diacritics guide pronunciation more precisely, allowing both writers and speakers to produce — and listeners to perceive — the intended distinctions.

The etymological alphabet is a superset of the standard alphabet. The standard forms are always legitimate simplifications of the etymological forms. Users who choose to write etymologically are free to use any subset of the additional letters, with two restrictions:

  1. A character, once adopted in a text or conversation, MUST be used consistently throughout.
  2. Certain letters come in complementary pairs; using one member of a pair while omitting the other is discouraged. The established pairs are: ę/ų, ė/ȯ, t́/d́ (or ť/ď), and ś/ź.

Disambiguation of Homographs​

Because the standard orthography neutralises several Proto-Slavic distinctions, it inevitably produces homographs — pairs of words that are spelled identically but differ in meaning, etymology, and (in natural Slavic languages) pronunciation. The etymological alphabet resolves these collisions by restoring the lost distinctions.

Usage Guidance

Text written with many etymological diacritics may appear intimidating to learners, who might incorrectly assume that the diacritics are obligatory or that etymological spelling is somehow superior. When using this orthography, state explicitly why you are doing so and that these marks are optional additions.

Community Discussion: Pronunciation of ė and ȯ

Some speakers propose æ as the primary pronunciation of ė, arguing that strong yer *ь reflexes span from ɛ in most languages to a in Shtokavian and that æ represents a middle ground. The current official recommendation is ə, reflecting the reduced, fleeting character of the vowel.

Notes on ę (front nasal): Historically, ę was the Proto-Slavic front nasal vowel ɛ̃. In modern Interslavic it is NOT pronounced as a nasal but as ʲæ — intermediate between a and e with palatalization of the preceding consonant. Acceptable realizations also include ʲa (analogous to Russian я), ʲɛ (analogous to Czech ě), or simply e. In simplified Interslavic, ę is written and read as e; the etymological distinction is meaningful: e.g., česť "honour" vs. čęsť "part/portion."

Notes on ć and đ: These two letters are the only etymological consonants that require a non-trivial substitution in standard orthography. All other etymological consonants simplify to their base letter (losing only the diacritic); ć and đ map to č and dž respectively. Using ć where standard orthography requires č (and vice versa) constitutes an error, not merely a stylistic choice. Note that đ is the only case in MS Plus where a single letter replaces a standard digraph (dž).

The Latin letters ć and đ are taken directly from the Serbo-Croatian Latin alphabet (used in Croatian, Bosnian, Montenegrin, and Serbian Latin orthography), where they carry the same phonetic values [t͡ɕ] and [d͡ʑ]. They are among the few MS Plus letters borrowed from a naturally occurring alphabet rather than designed specifically for the system. In Cyrillic contexts (dictionaries, learning materials), the corresponding letters are the Serbian Ћ ћ and Ђ ђ. These were introduced in the early 19th century by reformers such as Vuk Karadžić and Dositej Obradović as modernizations of the Old Cyrillic letter đerv (Ꙉ), which historically served for both [t͡ɕ] and [d͡ʑ] — illustrating the close phonological relationship between the two sounds. Both ć and đ are widely available in standard Unicode fonts.

During the early development of the unified Interslavic project (shortly after the merger of the Slovianski and Novoslovensky projects), alternative diacritics were considered for these letters. One proposal was to use circumflex accents, yielding ĉ and dẑ instead of the current ć and đ. This approach would have been more systematic, giving the diacritic a unique function not shared with other letter types (since the acute accent on ć serves a different purpose than the acute on soft consonants like ś and ź). Ultimately, ć and đ were retained because they are naturally occurring letters whose established phonetic values in Serbo-Croatian match the intended MS Plus pronunciation.

Notes on ĺ (ľ) and ń: These letters occur primarily before a consonant (including before j); in principle they also occur word-finally where the root historically ended in a front yer *ь (see the note below for current dictionary inconsistencies). Phonetically they are typically realized identically to lj and nj; speakers may optionally keep ĺ distinct as lʲ (vs. ʎ for lj). The difference is that ĺ/ń mark etymologically justified softness that is NOT universally reflected in standard Interslavic digraphs. The letters ĺ and ľ are interchangeable variants of the same letter; similarly, the letters t́ and ť, and d́ and ď, are interchangeable pairs.

The distinction between ĺ/ń and the standard digraphs lj/nj encodes an important etymological difference. The digraphs lj and nj represent iotation-derived softness (Proto-Slavic *lj, *nj from consonant + *j), which is reflected as a soft consonant in all Slavic languages, North and South alike. By contrast, ĺ and ń represent yer-derived softness (Proto-Slavic *lь, *nь — consonant + front yer), which is soft in Northern Slavic languages but hard in Southern Slavic. For example: polje "field" has iotation-derived lj (Croatian also has polje with a soft L), while veĺmi "very" has yer-derived ĺ (Croatian veoma has a hard L, and Belarusian вельмі has a soft L). In standard orthography, iotation-derived softness is written with the digraphs (lj, nj), while yer-derived softness is dropped entirely (written as hard l, n). The MS Plus register restores the yer-derived softness with acute-accented letters (ĺ, ń, ŕ, ś, ź, t́, d́); háček variants (ľ, ť, ď) are accepted alternatives for some of these.

Unresolved: Scope of ĺ and ń in the Dictionary

The Interslavic dictionary and existing documentation are not yet fully consistent in applying the distinction between iotation-derived softness (lj, nj) and yer-derived softness (ĺ, ń). Some dictionary entries use the iotation digraphs lj or nj where the softness is historically yer-derived — for example, bolj instead of the expected bol/​boĺ (cf. Croatian bol with hard L), or koljce instead of kolce/​koĺce (from *kolьce, cf. kolo). The athematic masculine nouns (kamenj, korenj, prstenj, etc.) similarly use the iotation digraph nj in their nominative form, but their final softness is yer-derived (*kamy, oblique stem *kamen-), suggesting that the standard form should end in hard n and the MS Plus form in ń (i.e., kamen / kameń).

A systematic review of the lexicon is needed to ensure that iotation-derived and yer-derived softness are consistently distinguished in the etymological register.

Notes on t́/ť and d́/ď: These letters do not appear in any living Slavic alphabet. In Unicode they can only be formed with a combining diacritic (acute accent or haček), which means display may vary across fonts. When using a haček form (ť, ď), note that in many fonts the haček is rendered as an apostrophe.

Unicode and Rendering Challenges

The acute-accented forms T́ t́ and D́ d́ are not precomposed Unicode characters — they must be represented as a base letter followed by a combining acute accent (U+0301). This has practical consequences:

  • Font rendering: Many fonts do not correctly position the combining accent above uppercase T and D, resulting in the accent floating above the letter or colliding with ascenders. The problem is particularly severe on Windows systems with older font rendering engines.
  • Text processing: String operations (sorting, comparison, search) may behave inconsistently depending on whether Unicode normalization (NFC vs. NFD) is applied. A text editor that stores t́ as NFC may produce a different byte sequence than one that stores it as NFD, causing search mismatches.
  • Copy-paste interoperability: Copying text containing combining diacritics between applications may strip or reorder the combining characters, producing garbled output.

The háček forms Ť ť and Ď ď are precomposed Unicode characters (U+0164/U+0165 and U+010E/U+010F respectively), available in Czech and Slovak character sets. They render reliably in virtually all fonts and do not suffer from the combining-diacritic problems. This practical advantage is one argument in favour of the háček forms over the acute forms, independent of the phonological function debate.

For Ĺ ĺ: this precomposed character (U+0139/U+013A) exists in Unicode and is used in the Slovak alphabet, where it marks vowel length rather than softness. It renders reliably. However, some community members prefer Ľ ľ (U+013D/U+013E, also from Slovak), which marks softness in Slovak — a semantic match with the Interslavic usage. Both forms are accepted.

Soft T/D in Loanwords and the tj/dj Problem​

The etymological consonants ť and ď are defined as reflexes of Proto-Slavic yer-softened *tь and *dь. However, the Interslavic dictionary also contains a number of loanwords and proper names in which the standard orthography writes tj or dj — sequences that in practice imply a soft /tʲ/ or /dʲ/ even though standard Interslavic has no such phonemes (see a).

In the MS Plus register, ť and ď provide a natural way to represent genuine soft /tʲ/ and /dʲ/ in these words. For example, kostjum could be written kosťum, tjuŕma as ťuŕma, and tjulenj as ťulenj — making the intended pronunciation explicit and resolving the ambiguity that exists in standard orthography. Note also that tj and dj sequences may simply be pronounced as distinct consonants [tj] and [dj] — a natural option for speakers whose languages preserve these clusters.

This would extend the use of ť and ď beyond their etymological Proto-Slavic function to cover borrowed soft /tʲ/ and /dʲ/ from non-Slavic sources. The extension is natural: the phonetic value is the same ([tʲ] and [dʲ]), and the letters already exist in the MS Plus inventory. However, this usage has not yet been formally adopted.

Unresolved: Extending ť/ď to Loanwords in MS Plus

The MS Plus letters ť and ď currently have a strictly etymological scope (Proto-Slavic yer-derived softness). Several dictionary words contain tj and dj sequences that represent a borrowed soft /tʲ/ or /dʲ/ (e.g., kostjum, tjuŕma, tjulenj). Allowing ť and ď in these positions within the MS Plus register would resolve the pronunciation ambiguity without altering the standard orthography.

Note that this question is partly moot for words where the tj/dj sequence should be eliminated entirely in favour of a more natural Slavic form — e.g., tulenj instead of tjulenj, gospodža instead of gospodja (see a). The ť/ď extension would apply only to words where no better alternative exists and a genuine soft consonant is the intended pronunciation.

Note also that the strong soft yer ė — which is part of the MS Plus inventory and functions correctly in the declinator (e.g., pės → psy) — is not yet searchable in the dictionary tools. Since ė is an official component of the etymological alphabet, this represents a tooling gap that should be addressed.

Additional Cyrillic Characters​

In the Latin etymological alphabet, all additional information is conveyed exclusively by diacritical marks layered onto the standard letters. A reader who does not recognize a diacritic can always fall back to the standard reading.

An analogous approach for Cyrillic — using modified characters such as ӑ, ԙ, ө, ұ, ҷ, ӝ, etc. — would produce text that appears extremely artificial and would be of little help, since the modifications would not be self-explanatory. Nevertheless, among enthusiasts of Interslavic etymology who wish to write etymological diacritics in Cyrillic, the most commonly used convention for å is the Chuvash letter Ӑ ӑ (Cyrillic а with breve). This letter is available in most fonts that support extended Cyrillic. An alternative — a Cyrillic а with a combining ring-above diacritic — is sometimes seen but renders poorly in most fonts, because combining diacritics are not well supported for Cyrillic in the majority of typefaces. For handwriting, either variant is acceptable. An extended Cyrillic alphabet using historical OCS characters (ѣ, ѩ, ѫ) or regional characters (ћ, ђ) would be difficult to read for anyone not familiar with Old Church Slavonic orthography, thus defeating the purpose of Interslavic.

For these reasons, the Interslavic etymological alphabet has no standard Cyrillic equivalent.

However, a number of additional Cyrillic characters appear in scholarly publications and community resources (such as dictionaries and learning materials):

Softness Marking Through Inflection and Derivation​

When a root ends in a soft consonant in the etymological register, that softness is a lexical property of the root and should in principle persist through all inflected and derived forms. However, the orthography does not always make this visible, creating potential ambiguity.

Softness before i: When a soft-stem noun is inflected into a form ending in -i (e.g., genitive singular or nominative plural of feminine i-stems), the softness of the final consonant becomes unmarked in both standard and etymological orthography:

Nominative sg. (MS Plus)Genitive sg. / Nom. pl.Underlying pronunciation
kosťkostikɔstʲi — the tʲ persists but is not marked
sȯľsolisɔlʲi — the lʲ persists but is not marked
pamęťpamętipamʲætʲi — the tʲ persists but is not marked

In these forms, a reader unfamiliar with the paradigm might not realize that the consonant before -i is soft, since the soft diacritic is conventionally omitted before i.

Unresolved: Softness Marking Before I

Three approaches have been proposed to address the ambiguity of soft consonants before i:

  1. Preserve the soft mark even before i: Write kost́i, soĺi, etc. This is maximally explicit but typographically unusual and not attested in any living Slavic orthography.
  2. Use notation only in dictionaries and learning materials: Mark the softness with a special convention (e.g., i vs. í, or an underline) in reference materials, while leaving running text unmarked. This keeps running text clean but requires readers to consult reference materials.
  3. Accept the ambiguity: Most Slavic speakers will intuit the correct softness from context and from knowledge of the declension class. The i-stem feminine paradigm itself signals softness. This is the least interventionist approach but offers no help to learners.

No consensus has been reached. The current practice is effectively option 3 — softness before i is unmarked.

Softness through derivation: When a derivational suffix is attached to a root whose final consonant is soft, the softness should persist. In the etymological register this is marked explicitly (e.g., svińsky from svinь-), but in standard orthography the softness is lost (written svinsky). See the relevant sections for the full treatment of softness at root–suffix boundaries in adjective formation.

Diacritic Consistency in MS Plus​

The MS Plus diacritic system, while functional, exhibits an internal tension in the mapping between diacritics and phonological functions. Ideally, each diacritic would encode exactly one type of phonological information. In practice, however, the acute accent serves two distinct roles:

  • On consonants like ś, ź, ŕ, ń, ĺ, t́, d́, the acute marks yer-derived palatalization (softening caused by the historical front yer *ь).
  • On ć, however, the acute marks iotation (the reflex of Proto-Slavic *tj), which is a fundamentally different phonological process — iotation produces postalveolar or palatal affricates, while yer-softening produces palatalized versions of the base consonant.

Similarly, the háček (caron) has three distinct functions in broader Slavic orthographic tradition:

  • It marks postalveolar sibilants (š, ž, č) — its primary function in Interslavic.
  • In Czech and Slovak, it also marks palatalized stops (ť, ď, ň) — a softening function that overlaps with what Interslavic uses the acute for.
  • It marks the yat vowel (ě) in Czech, Slovak, and Interslavic — indicating the reflex of Proto-Slavic *ě.

This means that the choice between ť/t́ and ď/d́ is not purely aesthetic: it reflects a deeper question about whether the diacritic system should prioritize consistency of function (one diacritic = one phonological process) or naturalness of appearance (using letter forms already established in living Slavic alphabets). The current MS Plus system permits both háček and acute variants for soft T and D (i.e., ť/t́ and ď/d́ are interchangeable), effectively deferring the decision.

Unresolved: Diacritic Function Consistency

The MS Plus diacritic system mixes phonological functions across its diacritics: the acute accent marks both yer-derived softening (on ś, ź, ŕ, etc.) and iotation (on ć), while the háček marks both sibilant place of articulation (on š, ž, č) and — in the alternate forms ť, ď, ľ — palatalization. A fully consistent system would assign each diacritic a unique function, but this would require either abandoning the naturally occurring Serbo-Croatian ć/đ (which carry strong recognition value for South Slavic speakers) or introducing unfamiliar letter forms. The current compromise — permitting both háček and acute variants — avoids forcing the choice but leaves the system formally inconsistent.

The Role and Identity of MS Plus​

A persistent community tension concerns the positioning of MS Plus within the Interslavic ecosystem. The etymological alphabet was rebranded from its earlier designation as "MS Plus" — a name that implied an enhanced variant of everyday Interslavic — to "etymological alphabet," accompanied by explicit guidance that it "SHALL NOT be used as the default orthography in everyday communication."

This repositioning has been contested on several grounds:

  • Practical suitability for everyday use. MS Plus contains fewer distinct letters than the standard Slovak alphabet (which has 46 letters including diacritical variants), and every additional letter carries a concrete phonological or morphological function. The argument that it is too complex for daily use is therefore not self-evident.
  • Emotional and cultural connection. The MS Plus inventory draws letters from naturally occurring Slavic Latin alphabets — ć and đ from Serbo-Croatian, ě from Czech, ń and ś and ź from Polish, ľ and ť and ď from Slovak. This gives Latin-script Slavic speakers the experience of encountering familiar letters from their own writing systems, creating an immediate sense of recognition. Any shift toward artificial diacritic combinations (e.g., d́ instead of ď, or ė instead of è) dilutes this property.
  • Incomplete etymological coverage. MS Plus encodes only a fraction of the etymological information available in the Proto-Slavic source forms. It does not distinguish native from foreign g/h, does not mark the epenthetic l, does not encode the neuter -e/-o variation, and lacks many other distinctions that a true etymological or scientific notation would require. Calling it an "etymological alphabet" overstates its descriptive power while simultaneously discouraging the everyday use for which it is arguably well suited.

The counter-argument is that encouraging everyday MS Plus use risks creating a barrier to entry: texts dense with diacritics may intimidate learners, who might incorrectly assume that the marks are obligatory. The current positioning as a scholarly tool avoids this risk.

Unresolved: MS Plus Positioning

The community has not reached consensus on whether MS Plus should be actively encouraged for everyday use (as a "rich variant" of Interslavic that enhances both pronunciation guidance and cross-Slavic recognizability) or restricted to scholarly and etymological contexts (to avoid intimidating learners). The renaming from "MS Plus" to "etymological alphabet" reflects the latter position, but arguments for the former remain substantive — particularly the observation that MS Plus is no more complex than standard Slovak orthography and that its use of naturally occurring national-language letters creates valuable cultural resonance.

Scope Restrictions​

The etymological alphabet does NOT include:

  • vowel length or tone markers;
  • special letters for non-Slavic loanwords (e.g., OCS ѳ and ѵ used only for Greek words, or ü for words of Germanic or French origin);
  • any marker that would be obligatory rather than optional.

The etymological alphabet is designed so that any text written in it is also valid standard Interslavic text when the diacritics are stripped. This property ensures backward compatibility with tools and readers that handle only the standard orthography.

Yat (ě) after Functionally Soft Consonants​

The Problem​

In Proto-Slavic and Old Church Slavonic, the vowel yat (ѣ) appeared after all consonants, including those that were already phonetically palatalized (the "functionally soft" consonants: j, č, š, ž, and c). In the development of Interslavic, the letter ě was dropped after j, č, š, and ž on the grounds that these consonants are already soft and the additional softening marker is redundant — so OCS jěsti became jesti, and tęžěje became tęžeje.

However, this simplification was not applied consistently. The consonant c is also functionally soft (it is an affricate that patterns with the other postalveolar consonants in its palatalization behaviour), yet the word cěly retains ě after c. This creates an internal inconsistency in the orthography.

The Trade-Off​

The treatment of yat after functionally soft consonants involves a fundamental trade-off between two design principles of Interslavic:

  1. Orthographic simplicity — Removing ě after consonants that are already soft eliminates a redundant softening marker. Since č, š, ž, j, and c are inherently palatalized, writing e rather than ě after them produces no loss of phonetic information for standard Interslavic pronunciation.

  2. Cross-Slavic flavourization potential — Preserving etymological ě in these positions significantly improves the ability to adapt Interslavic text to individual Slavic languages:

    • Polish: ě → a/ą after these consonants (e.g., cěly → cały, jěsti → jeść via jadł)
    • Czech/Slovak: ě → í in Czech (e.g., jěsti → jíst, cěly → cíl "target"); ě → ie in Slovak
    • Bulgarian: the yat alternation (я/е) is better preserved (e.g., jěm → Bg. ям)
    • Russian/Belarusian: etymological ě supports "yokanie" flavourization (ě → ё/е pattern)

In other words, the etymological ě carries no additional phonetic information for standard Interslavic but encodes valuable cross-Slavic correspondence data that is used in flavourization.

Informal Proposal: Iotated Yat as a Higher-Level Code Point

Some community members have proposed distinguishing a "jotated yat" jě at a register above MS Plus — a letter that would produce full flavorizations (Czech jíst, Polish jadł, etc.) but reflect as plain e in standard and MS Plus orthography. This mirrors the historical Proto-Slavic distinction between plain yat (ѣ) and its jotated variant (ꙓ), attested informally in some Cyrillic transliteration tools. No decision has been reached; the proposal remains exploratory.

Pronunciation Note​

Regardless of which orthographic solution is adopted, the pronunciation rule is clear: after the functionally soft consonants j, č, š, ž, and c, the letter ě — if written — is pronounced simply as e. In practice, the doubled softening (soft consonant + palatalizing vowel) does not arise — after already-soft consonants, ě is simply realized as plain e.

Unresolved: Treatment of ě after functionally soft consonants

The current orthography is inconsistent: ě has been removed after j, č, š, and ž (e.g., jesti, tęžeje) but retained after c (e.g., cěly). Three solutions have been proposed:

  1. Restore etymological ě everywhere — write jěsti, jěhati, dråžěje, cěly, etc., with a pronunciation note that after already-soft consonants ě is realized as plain e. This maximises flavourization potential.
  2. Keep etymological ě everywhere but mark it as phonetically neutral — same spelling as (1), but the specification explicitly states that after j, č, š, ž, and c, the letter ě does not indicate additional softening.
  3. Remove ě after c as well — write cely instead of cěly, completing the simplification pattern. This maximises orthographic consistency at the cost of flavourization data.

The committee must weigh orthographic regularity against the practical benefits of etymological transparency for cross-Slavic adaptation.
