Spelling Conventions

This section specifies the normative orthographic conventions for Standard Interslavic beyond the letter-by-letter correspondences already established in the relevant section The rules below apply equally to both the Latin and Cyrillic scripts unless a script is explicitly named.

One-Phoneme–One-Grapheme Principle and Exceptions​

The standard orthography of Interslavic strives for a bijective (one-to-one) correspondence between phonemes and graphemes. Each phoneme SHOULD be represented by exactly one grapheme or digraph, and each grapheme or digraph SHOULD represent exactly one phoneme.

The following documented exceptions to strict bijectivity are normative:

SituationDescription
ě / є optionalityThe grapheme ě (Latin) / є (Cyrillic) MAY be simplified to e / е in informal registers without violating standard orthography (see the relevant section).
y / ы optionalityThe grapheme y (Latin) / ы (Cyrillic) MAY be substituted by i / и by speakers whose native language does not distinguish the two (see the relevant section).
Digraphs as single graphemesdž, lj, nj (Latin) and дж, љ, њ (Cyrillic) each represent a single phoneme and MUST be treated as single orthographic units for all purposes including capitalization and syllabification.
Etymological diacriticsLetters of the etymological alphabet carry diacritics that are not phonemically contrastive in standard Interslavic and SHOULD be omitted in non-etymological registers, with the exceptions of ć→č and đ→dž.

Scripts MUST NOT be mixed within a single continuous text. A text written in the Latin script MUST use only Latin orthographic conventions; a text written in the Cyrillic script MUST use only Cyrillic orthographic conventions.

Capitalization Rules​

Sentence-Initial Capitalization​

The first letter of every sentence SHALL be written in uppercase. This rule applies after every full stop, question mark, and exclamation mark that ends a sentence.

  • Dobro jutro. Kak se imaš?

When a sentence begins with a digraph (Dž, Lj, Nj in Latin; Дж in Cyrillic), only the first letter of the digraph is capitalized.

  • Latin: Džungla je opasna. (not DŽungla)
  • Cyrillic: Џунгла је опасна. (the digraph Дж acts as a single unit; uppercase form is Џ in Cyrillic)

Proper Noun Capitalization​

The following categories SHALL be capitalized in both scripts:

  • Personal names: Ivan, Marija
  • Geographical names (countries, cities, rivers, mountains): Polska, Moskva, Dunaj
  • Names of languages: Medžuslovjansky, Rusky, Česky
  • Names of organizations and institutions when used as official titles
  • Names of historical periods and events when used as proper names: Renesansa, Reformacija

Adjectives Derived from Proper Nouns​

Adjectives derived from proper nouns SHALL be written in lowercase in both scripts. This follows the majority Slavic convention and distinguishes Interslavic from English practice.

Derived adjectiveGlossCorresponding proper noun
slovjansky / словјанскыSlavicSlovjanstvo
rusky / рускыRussianRusija
parisky / парискыParisianPariz
medžuslovjansky / меджусловјанскыInterslavicMedžuslovjansky (as a language name)

Exception: When an adjective is part of an official proper name (e.g., the name of a publication, organization, or geographical entity used attributively as a title), it SHALL follow the capitalization of that title.

Months, Days of the Week, and Seasons​

Month names, day-of-the-week names, and season names SHALL be written in lowercase in ordinary prose in both scripts:

  • januar, februar, mart
  • ponedělok, vtornik, srěda
  • vesna, lěto, jesen, zima

These words are capitalized only when they appear as the first word of a sentence or as part of a formal proper title.

Punctuation Rules​

Standard Punctuation Marks and Their Usage​

Interslavic uses the following punctuation marks with the specified normative functions:

MarkNamePrimary function
.točkaEnds a declarative sentence or abbreviation
,zapjatajaSeparates clauses, list items, and parenthetical elements
;točka s zapjatojuSeparates coordinate clauses with internal commas; separates items in complex lists
:dvotočjeIntroduces a list, quotation, or explanatory clause
?pytajny znakEnds a direct question
!vykličny znakEnds an exclamation or emphatic imperative
—tire / dlouhe tireMarks a parenthetical aside, a sudden break, or a predicate without copula in certain registers
…mnogotočjeIndicates an omission or a trailing pause

The space before and after punctuation marks follows continental European convention: no space before a mark, one space after a mark that ends a unit. The colon and semicolon follow the same pattern. The em-dash (—) is set with a thin space or no space on either side consistently within a document; either style is acceptable provided it is applied consistently.

Quotation Marks​

Three styles of quotation marks are acceptable in Interslavic. The choice of style MAY reflect regional or typographic convention, but MUST be applied consistently within a single document:

StyleOpeningClosingNotes
Guillemets«»Common in Russian, Serbian, Bulgarian typography
Low-high„"Common in Czech, Slovak, German typography
Curly (English)""Acceptable in digital contexts

Nested quotations use the alternative style from the outermost quotation: if the outer quotation uses «…», the inner quotation uses „…" or "…".

Direct quotations of speech MAY alternatively be introduced with an em-dash at the start of a new paragraph, following South Slavic typographic practice:

— Dobro jutro, — reče Ivan.

Comma Usage in Complex Sentences​

The following rules govern comma placement. They reflect the majority Slavic norm, which differs from English practice in several respects:

Subordinate clauses: Every subordinate clause SHALL be set off from the main clause by a comma, regardless of its position (initial, medial, or final).

  • Kogda prispejem, otpočnejem.
  • On verit, čto ona pravi.

Coordinating conjunctions: Coordinating conjunctions (i, a, no, ale, ili, ili...ili) that join independent clauses SHALL be preceded by a comma. When they join only words or phrases (without a new finite verb), the comma is omitted.

  • Ona pišet, i on čita. (two clauses → comma)
  • Ona pišet i čita. (same subject, two predicates → no comma)

Parenthetical elements: Words, phrases, and clauses used parenthetically SHALL be set off by commas on both sides: Moj brat, Ivan, živi v Praze.

Vocatives: Direct address (vocative) SHALL always be set off by commas: Prijatelju, kamo ideš?

Compound Word Orthography​

Solid Compounds​

Compounds formed from two or more native Slavic roots SHALL be written as single words without a hyphen. This is the default form for productive compounding in Interslavic:

LatinCyrillicGlossComponents
rukopisрукописmanuscriptruka + pisati
železnicaжелезницаrailwayželezo + -nica
milosrdnyмилосрдныmercifulmilosrdje
starosvětskyстаросвѣтскыold-worldstary + světy
bogobojazlivyбогобојазливыgod-fearingbog + bojati

The solid compound form applies regardless of the grammatical categories of the constituent elements (noun + noun, adjective + noun, verb root + noun, etc.).

Hyphenated Compounds​

A hyphen SHALL be used in the following environments:

  1. Compounds containing a proper-noun component: When one element of a compound is or contains a proper noun (a name of a person, place, or language), the compound is written with a hyphen to mark the boundary:

    • češsko-rusky slovnik / чєшско-рускы словник (Czech-Russian dictionary)
    • slovjano-germanske otnošenja / словјано-германске одношења (Slavic-Germanic relations)

  2. Coordinate (copulative) compounds: When two equal-status adjectives or nouns are joined to express a combined or dual quality, a hyphen is used:

    • bělо-črveny / бѣло-чрвены (white-and-red)
    • sěvero-vostočny / сѣверо-восточны (north-eastern)

  3. Compounds with stand-alone prefixal elements in certain environments: Prefixes that retain independent lexical meaning (such as ne-, prě-, su-) are written solid without a hyphen. However, when a prefix attaches to a proper noun, a hyphen MUST be used:

    • ne-Ivan (non-Ivan, a hypothetical reference) — hyphen required before a proper noun
    • ne znaju → neznam — no hyphen in standard derivation

  4. Repeated or reduplicated forms used for emphasis or distributive meaning: malo-malo, den za den-den — these stylistic forms may use a hyphen.

Linking Vowel in Compounds​

When two roots are joined in a solid compound, a linking vowel (interfiks) is inserted between them according to the following rule:

  • -o- after a root ending in a hard consonant (the default linking vowel):

    • ruk-o-pis → rukopis / рукопис
    • železo- → železnica / железница

  • -e- after a root ending in a soft consonant (š, ž, č, j, lj, nj) or a root that historically ends in a soft consonant:

    • oruž-e-nosec → oruženosec / оружeносeц (arms-bearer)
    • duš-e-spasitelny / душeспаситeлны (soul-saving)

The linking vowel is absent when the first element already ends in a vowel or when the compound is formed with a bound prefix .

When the linking vowel is preceded by a fleeting vowel in the root (i.e., a vowel that disappears before suffixes — see the relevant section), the fleeting vowel is dropped and the linking vowel is inserted normally.

Soft Consonant Spelling at Morpheme Boundaries​

When a derivational or inflectional suffix is attached to a root ending in a soft consonant, the following spelling conventions apply:

Iotation-derived softness (lj, nj, rj — from Proto-Slavic consonant + *j) is always written with the digraph, even before a suffix:

  • morje → morjsky (not morsky)
  • konj → konjsky (not konsky)

Yer-derived softness (from Proto-Slavic consonant + *ь) is written in the etymological register with the appropriate diacritic (ń, ĺ, ŕ, ś, ź, t́, d́) but is dropped in the standard register:

  • MS Plus: svińsky, standard: svinsky
  • MS Plus: ruśsky, standard: russky

Before i: Soft consonants before the vowel i are not explicitly marked in either register. The softness is implicit from the declension class or derivational context. See the relevant section for a discussion of this ambiguity and proposed solutions.

Unresolved: Scope of Pre-Suffix Softening

The standard orthography does not distinguish between roots that end in an inherently soft consonant (where softness should persist through derivation) and roots that end in a hard consonant (where no softening occurs). In the standard register, both morjsky (soft root morj-) and russky (potentially soft root ruś- in MS Plus) look like they follow different rules — the first preserves softness visibly (as a digraph), while the second does not.

This asymmetry arises because iotation-derived softness is always visible in standard orthography (via digraphs lj, nj), while yer-derived softness is invisible in standard orthography (no diacritic). The etymological register resolves this by making both types visible, but most users write in the standard register. A clearer statement of the rules governing which consonants soften before which suffixes — and whether this softening extends to s and z — is needed. See the relevant sectiona.

Number Formatting​

Cardinal Numerals in Text​

The following conventions govern when numerals are written as words versus digits:

  • Numbers from one to ten SHALL be written as words in running prose: jeden, dva, tri, …, deset.
  • Numbers from eleven upward MAY be written as digits in running prose, especially when precision is important or the number appears in a table or technical context.
  • Numbers beginning a sentence SHALL always be written as words regardless of magnitude.
  • Approximate or round numbers (hundreds, thousands) are typically written as words in literary or formal prose: sto, tyseč, million.
  • When two numerals appear adjacent in a sentence, at least one SHALL be expressed as a word to avoid ambiguity.

Large numerals expressed in digits MAY use a thin space or period as a thousands separator (see the relevant section).

Ordinal Numerals in Text​

Ordinal numerals in running text SHOULD be written as words: prvy, drugy, treti.

In references to numbered items (sections, articles, volumes, pages), ordinal numerals MAY be expressed as a digit followed by a period: 1., 2., 3.. This convention follows continental European typographic practice and MUST NOT be confused with a terminal sentence-full-stop.

  • v 3. paragrafu (in the 3rd paragraph)
  • na str. 45 (on page 45)

When an ordinal numeral expressed as digit-plus-period falls at the end of a sentence, only one period is written.

Decimal and Thousands Separators​

Interslavic follows continental European numeric convention:

FunctionSymbolExample
Thousands separatorperiod (.) or thin space`` or 1 000 000
Decimal separatorcomma (,)3,14

The thin-space convention for thousands grouping (1 000 000) is preferred in typeset documents as it avoids ambiguity with the sentence-final period. The period-as-thousands-separator (1.000) is acceptable in informal or technical writing.

Note: The decimal comma contrasts with English convention (which uses a decimal point). Authors translating or adapting content from English sources MUST convert decimal points to decimal commas.

Syllabification and Hyphenation at Line Breaks​

Syllabification Principles​

When a word must be broken across a line, the break SHALL occur at a syllable boundary. The following principles determine syllable boundaries in Interslavic:

  1. Every syllable contains exactly one vowel nucleus (or one syllabic r).
  2. Intervocalic single consonants belong to the following syllable (onset principle): ma-ti, bra-ta.
  3. Digraphs are never split. The digraphs dž, lj, nj (Latin) and дж, љ, њ (Cyrillic) are treated as single consonants for syllabification purposes and SHALL NOT be divided across a line break.
  4. A break SHOULD NOT leave a single letter isolated at the beginning or end of a line.

Rules for Consonant Clusters at Word Breaks​

When two or more consonants stand between two vowels, the break point is determined by the onset-maximization principle: as many consonants as are permissible as a word-initial cluster in Interslavic SHALL be assigned to the following syllable.

Examples:

WordSyllabificationNotes
sestrases-trastr- is a valid onset
prosbapros-basb- is not a standard onset
učitelkau-či-tel-kasingle consonants to following syllable
medžuslovjanskymed-žu-slov-jan-skysl-, j- are valid onsets
vostočnyvos-toč-nytoč- onset

Prefixes and suffixes retain their morphological identity at line breaks: a break MAY occur at a prefix-root or root-suffix boundary even if it does not align perfectly with the phonological syllable boundary. This is a secondary preference when the primary syllabic boundary falls within a morpheme that a reader might find difficult to parse:

  • ob-učati is preferred over obu-čati at a line break, since ob- is a recognizable prefix.

Consistency of Yat (ě) in Lexical Stems​

Principle​

When a lexical root contains etymological yat (ě), the yat SHOULD be written consistently across all derivatives and prefixed forms of that root. Inconsistent alternation between ě and e within a single morpheme family undermines the regularity of the orthography and obscures the etymological relationships that the Interslavic spelling system is designed to preserve.

This principle applies to:

  • unprefixed and prefixed forms of the same verb (e.g., plěniti and zaplěniti, not *pleniti alongside zaplěniti);
  • verb stems and their derived nouns, adjectives, and verbal nouns;
  • aspectual pairs formed by prefixation or suffixation.

The ě/e Distinction in Verb Endings​

As a general rule, verb infinitive endings in Interslavic are -ěti or -ęti, not *-eti. The ending -eti is legitimate only after functionally soft consonants (j, č, š, ž, c), where ě is simplified to e by the standard orthographic rule (see the relevant section). In all other environments, a verb ending that appears as -eti should be checked against its Proto-Slavic and Old Church Slavonic etymology to determine whether it should be -ěti.

Unresolved: Inconsistent ě in verb stems in the dictionary

Several verb families in the current dictionary exhibit inconsistent treatment of etymological ě:

  • plěn- family: The unprefixed forms plen, pleniti lack ě, while the prefixed forms zaplěniti, zaplěnjati have it. The Old Church Slavonic form плѣнъ confirms that all forms should use plěn-.
  • trěti "to rub": Currently listed as treti / utreti, but the etymological vowel is ě (cf. Russian тереть, Czech rozetřít). The correct form should be trěti.
  • mrěti "to die": Currently listed as zamreti / umreti, but the etymological vowel is ě (cf. Russian замереть, Czech zemřít). The correct form should be zamrěti / umrěti.
  • dreti vs. drati: The form dreti appears in some sources, but the majority of Slavic languages reflect *dьrati → drati (analogous to *bьrati → brati), with present tense dere. The preferred form is likely drati (dere) rather than either dreti or drěti.

These inconsistencies should be resolved by systematic comparison with Proto-Slavic and OCS etymologies, applying the principle that etymological ě is preserved in all positions except after functionally soft consonants.
