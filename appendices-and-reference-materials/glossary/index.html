9.1. Glossary

info

The glossary provides definitions for technical terms used throughout the specification. Users unfamiliar with specific linguistic terminology are encouraged to refer to this section for clarification.

  1. Ablaut: A system of vowel changes that indicates grammatical contrasts, such as tense or number.
  2. Abessive Case: A grammatical case indicating absence or lack of something.
  3. Absolute Construction: A phrase with a noun and a participle that functions independently within a sentence.
  4. Abstract Noun: A noun that represents an idea, quality, or state, rather than a concrete object.
  5. Accentuation: The application of stress or emphasis on certain syllables in words.
  6. Accusative Case: A grammatical case used to mark the direct object of a verb.
  7. Active Voice: A sentence structure where the subject performs the action of the verb.
  8. Adjective Agreement: The grammatical rule requiring adjectives to match the gender, number, and case of the nouns they modify.
  9. Adjective Comparison: The formation of comparative and superlative forms of adjectives.
  10. Adjectival Declension: The inflection of adjectives based on gender, number, and case.
  11. Adjunct: An optional element in a sentence that adds information but is not essential to its structure.
  12. Adposition: A preposition or postposition used to express relationships between elements in a sentence.
  13. Adverbial Modifier: A word or phrase that modifies a verb, adjective, or other adverb, often providing information about time, manner, place, or degree.
  14. Affixation: The process of adding prefixes, suffixes, infixes, or circumfixes to a root to form a new word.
  15. Affricate: A consonant sound that begins as a stop and transitions into a fricative.
  16. Agglutinative Language: A type of language in which words are formed by stringing together morphemes, each with a distinct meaning or grammatical function.
  17. Agreement Rules: Grammatical rules requiring parts of a sentence to agree in features like gender, number, or case.
  18. Aorist Tense: A past tense used to describe simple or completed actions.
  19. Apocope: The loss of a sound or syllable at the end of a word.
  20. Apposition: A grammatical construction in which two elements, usually noun phrases, are placed side by side, with one identifying or explaining the other.
  21. Archaism: A word or expression that is outdated but used for stylistic or historical effect.
  22. Article: A word used to define a noun as specific (definite) or unspecific (indefinite).
  23. Aspectual Pair: A pair of verbs that express the same action but differ in aspect (e.g., imperfective vs. perfective).
  24. Assimilation: The process by which a sound becomes more like a neighboring sound in articulation.
  25. Attributive Adjective: An adjective that directly modifies a noun (e.g., "red apple").
  26. Augmentative: A form of a word that indicates a larger or more intense version of something.
  27. Auxiliary Verb: A verb used to form tenses, moods, or voices of other verbs (e.g., "to be," "to have").
  28. Back Vowel: A vowel sound produced with the tongue positioned towards the back of the mouth.
  29. Balto-Slavic Languages: A branch of the Indo-European language family including Baltic and Slavic languages.
  30. Bilabial Consonant: A consonant sound made with both lips (e.g., "p," "b").
  31. Borrowing: The adoption of words or phrases from another language.
  32. Cardinal Numeral: A numeral that expresses quantity (e.g., "one," "two").
  33. Case Endings: The inflectional suffixes that indicate grammatical case.
  34. Causative Verb: A verb that expresses the action of causing something to happen.
  35. Cerebral Consonant: A retroflex consonant produced with the tongue curled back towards the palate.
  36. Circumflex Accent: A diacritical mark used to indicate a specific type of stress or tone.
  37. Clitic: A word with syntactic independence but phonological dependence on another word.
  38. Codification: The process of standardizing and developing rules for a language.
  39. Collective Numeral: A numeral used to denote a group or set (e.g., "a pair," "a dozen").
  40. Common Slavic: The hypothetical ancestor language of all Slavic languages.
  41. Comparative Degree: The form of an adjective or adverb used to compare two entities.
  42. Complementizer: A word used to introduce a subordinate clause (e.g., "that," "if").
  43. Compound Word: A word formed by combining two or more words (e.g., "sunflower").
  44. Conditional Mood: A verb form expressing hypothetical or contingent actions.
  45. Conjugation Class: A category of verbs with the same pattern of inflection.
  46. Conjunction: A word used to connect clauses, sentences, or words (e.g., "and," "but").
  47. Consonant Cluster: A sequence of two or more consonant sounds within a syllable.
  48. Consonant Gradation: A systematic alternation of consonants in different morphological contexts.
  49. Consonant Mutation: A change in consonant sound due to grammatical or phonological factors.
  50. Continuant: A consonant sound produced with continuous airflow (e.g., "s," "m").
  51. Copula: A verb that links the subject to a complement (e.g., "is" in "He is a teacher").
  52. Coronal Consonant: A consonant articulated with the front part of the tongue.
  53. Corpus Planning: Efforts to develop, expand, and standardize the vocabulary and structure of a language.
  54. Cursive Script: A style of writing where letters are joined together for faster handwriting.
  55. Dative Case: A grammatical case used to indicate the indirect object of a verb.
  56. Declarative Sentence: A sentence that makes a statement or provides information.
  57. Definite Article: A word that specifies a noun as something particular (e.g., "the" in English).
  58. Definiteness: A grammatical category indicating whether a noun is specific or known.
  59. Demonstrative Pronoun: A pronoun used to point to specific things (e.g., "this," "that").
  60. Denominal Verb: A verb derived from a noun (e.g., "to fish" from "fish").
  61. Dental Consonant: A consonant articulated with the tongue against the upper teeth (e.g., "t," "d").
  62. Dependent Clause: A clause that cannot stand alone and depends on a main clause.
  63. Derivation: The process of creating new words by adding affixes or altering roots.
  64. Descriptive Grammar: The study of language as it is actually used, without prescribing rules.
  65. Determiner: A word that modifies a noun to indicate reference (e.g., "a," "the," "some").
  66. Diachronic Linguistics: The study of the historical development and evolution of languages.
  67. Dialectology: The study of regional language variations and dialects.
  68. Diglossia: A sociolinguistic situation where two varieties of a language are used in different contexts.
  69. Diminutive: A word form that conveys smallness or endearment (e.g., "duckling" from "duck").
  70. Diphthongization: The process of a single vowel sound becoming a diphthong.
  71. Discourse Analysis: The study of how language is used in texts and contexts.
  72. Disjunctive Pronoun: A pronoun used independently or for emphasis (e.g., "me" in "It’s me").
  73. Dissimilation: A phonological process where two similar sounds become less similar.
  74. Dual Number: A grammatical number category used for exactly two entities.
  75. Dynamic Verb: A verb that indicates an action or process rather than a state.
  76. Elision: The omission of a sound or syllable in speech or writing (e.g., "I’m" for "I am").
  77. Ellipsis: The omission of elements that are understood from context in a sentence.
  78. Emphatic Particle: A word used to add emphasis (e.g., "just" in "I just wanted to say").
  79. Endocentric Compound: A compound word where one part defines the overall meaning (e.g., "toothbrush").
  80. Epenthesis: The insertion of an extra sound into a word (e.g., "athlete" pronounced as "ath-a-lete").
  81. Ergative Case: A case marking the subject of a transitive verb in ergative-absolutive languages.
  82. Etymology: The study of the origin and history of words.
  83. Euphony: The quality of being pleasing to the ear, often achieved through harmonious sounds.
  84. Exocentric Compound: A compound word whose meaning cannot be deduced from its parts (e.g., "pickpocket").
  85. Experimental Phonetics: The study of speech sounds using experimental methods.
  86. Extensive Reading: A language learning approach focusing on reading large amounts of text for general understanding.
  87. Facultative Form: A grammatical form that is optional or context-dependent.
  88. Feminine Gender: A grammatical category used to classify nouns, pronouns, and adjectives associated with female entities.
  89. Finite Verb: A verb that is marked for tense, person, or number and can serve as the main verb of a clause.
  90. First Conjugation: A category of verbs that share the same inflectional patterns, often based on their endings.
  91. First Declension: A category of nouns that share common inflectional patterns, typically feminine in gender.
  92. Flapped Consonant: A consonant sound produced with a single rapid movement of the tongue against the alveolar ridge (e.g., "r" in some dialects).
  93. Focus Particle: A word that emphasizes or highlights a part of a sentence (e.g., "only," "even").
  94. Foreigner Talk: Simplified speech patterns used by native speakers to communicate with language learners.
  95. Formant: A concentration of acoustic energy in speech sounds, important in vowel identification.
  96. Fortis Consonant: A consonant produced with greater force or intensity compared to lenis consonants.
  97. Fricative: A consonant sound made by forcing air through a narrow constriction (e.g., "f," "s").
  98. Front Vowel: A vowel produced with the tongue positioned towards the front of the mouth.
  99. Future Perfect: A verb tense indicating an action that will have been completed by a certain point in the future.
  100. Future Tense: A verb tense used to describe actions that will occur in the future.
  101. Gender Agreement: The grammatical requirement that adjectives, pronouns, and verbs match the gender of the noun they refer to.
  102. Genitive Case: A grammatical case used to indicate possession, origin, or relationship.
  103. Gerundive: A verbal adjective expressing necessity or obligation (e.g., "to be read").
  104. Glide: A sound produced with little obstruction of the airstream, often transitioning to or from a vowel (e.g., "w" in "we").
  105. Glottal Stop: A consonant sound produced by briefly obstructing airflow at the glottis (e.g., the sound between the syllables of "uh-oh").
  106. Grapheme: The smallest unit of a writing system, such as a letter or character.
  107. Grimm's Law: A historical linguistics rule describing sound changes in the Germanic branch of Indo-European languages.
  108. Habitual Aspect: A verb aspect indicating actions that occur regularly or habitually.
  109. Hard Sign: A Cyrillic letter (ъ) used to indicate a slight separation between syllables.
  110. Head-final Language: A language in which the head of a phrase (e.g., the verb in a clause) typically comes last.
  111. Head-initial Language: A language in which the head of a phrase typically comes first.
  112. High Vowel: A vowel sound produced with the tongue positioned high in the mouth (e.g., "i" in "machine").
  113. Homograph: Words that are spelled the same but may differ in pronunciation or meaning (e.g., "lead" [to guide] vs. "lead" [a metal]).
  114. Homonym: Words that sound alike or are spelled alike but have different meanings (e.g., "bat" [animal] vs. "bat" [sports equipment]).
  115. Homophone: Words that sound the same but have different meanings or spellings (e.g., "flower" and "flour").
  116. Hypercorrection: A nonstandard usage resulting from the incorrect application of a perceived rule.
  117. Hypocorism: A pet name or diminutive form of a name (e.g., "Maggie" for "Margaret").
  118. Imperative Mood: A verb form used to express commands, requests, or instructions.
  119. Impersonal Verb: A verb that does not have a specific subject, often referring to weather or general conditions (e.g., "It rains").
  120. Indefinite Pronoun: A pronoun that refers to nonspecific persons or things (e.g., "someone," "anything").
  121. Independent Clause: A clause that can stand alone as a complete sentence.
  122. Indicative Mood: A verb form used for statements of fact or reality.
  123. Indirect Object: The recipient of the action of a verb (e.g., "She gave him a book").
  124. Infinitive: The base form of a verb, often preceded by "to" in English (e.g., "to run").
  125. Inflectional Paradigm: The complete set of inflected forms of a word.
  126. Instrumental Case: A grammatical case used to indicate the means by which an action is performed.
  127. Intensive Pronoun: A pronoun used to emphasize its antecedent (e.g., "I myself did it").
  128. Interfix: A linking element used between parts of a compound word.
  129. Interrogative Particle: A word or particle used to form questions (e.g., "li" in Slavic languages).
  130. Intervocalic: A sound occurring between two vowels.
  131. Intransitive Verb: A verb that does not require a direct object (e.g., "She sleeps").
  132. Irregular Verb: A verb that does not follow standard inflectional patterns.
  133. Isogloss: A geographical boundary marking the distribution of linguistic features.
  134. Iterative Aspect: A verb aspect indicating repeated or habitual actions.
  135. Labialization: The rounding of the lips during the articulation of a sound.
  136. Labiodental Consonant: A consonant produced with the lower lip against the upper teeth (e.g., "f," "v").
  137. Language Acquisition: The process of learning a language, either as a first or additional language.
  138. Language Family: A group of related languages descended from a common ancestral language.
  139. Language Planning: Efforts to develop, promote, or modify the structure and use of a language.
  140. Language Policy: Rules or guidelines governing the use and status of languages within a community or country.
  141. Lateral Consonant: A consonant produced by airflow around the sides of the tongue (e.g., "l").
  142. Lexeme: The base form or dictionary entry of a word, including all its inflected forms.
  143. Lexical Borrowing: The incorporation of words from one language into another.
  144. Lexical Gap: A concept or idea for which a language lacks a word.
  145. Lexicography: The practice of compiling dictionaries.
  146. Linguistic Typology: The study of similarities and differences among languages to classify them into types.
  147. Loan Translation: A type of borrowing where a phrase or word is translated literally (e.g., "skyscraper" as "wolkenkratzer" in German).
  148. Locative Case: A grammatical case indicating location.
  149. Low Vowel: A vowel sound produced with the tongue positioned low in the mouth (e.g., "a" in "father").
  150. L-vocalization: The replacement of the sound "l" with a vowel or semivowel (common in some dialects).
  151. Metathesis: A linguistic phenomenon where sounds or letters in a word switch places (e.g., "aks" for "ask").
  152. Middle Voice: A grammatical voice indicating the subject performs an action that affects itself or is inherently reflexive.
  153. Minimal Pair: Two words that differ by only one sound and have different meanings (e.g., "bat" and "pat").
  154. Modal Verb: A type of auxiliary verb that expresses possibility, necessity, or permission (e.g., "can," "must").
  155. Modality: The linguistic expression of attitudes or possibilities, such as necessity, ability, or likelihood.
  156. Monophthong: A single, pure vowel sound (e.g., "a" in "father").
  157. Morphological Case: A grammatical category that indicates the function of a noun or pronoun in a sentence.
  158. Morphophonemics: The study of how morphemes are affected by phonological rules in speech.
  159. Nasal Assimilation: A process where a nasal sound changes to match the place of articulation of a neighboring sound.
  160. Nasal Consonant: A consonant produced by allowing airflow through the nose (e.g., "m," "n").
  161. Nasalization: The process of making a vowel or consonant nasal by allowing air to pass through the nose.
  162. Natural Gender: The grammatical gender corresponding to the biological sex of the noun's referent.
  163. Negation: The grammatical process of denying or contradicting a statement (e.g., "not" in "I do not know").
  164. Neologism: A newly coined word or expression.
  165. Neuter Gender: A grammatical category often used for inanimate objects or abstract concepts.
  166. Nominalization: The process of converting a verb or adjective into a noun (e.g., "to decide" → "decision").
  167. Nominative Case: The grammatical case used for the subject of a sentence.
  168. Non-finite Verb: A verb form that does not indicate tense, person, or number (e.g., infinitives, participles).
  169. Number Agreement: A grammatical rule requiring agreement in number between parts of a sentence, such as subjects and verbs.
  170. Numeral Classifier: A word or morpheme used in some languages to categorize nouns when counting.
  171. Oblique Case: A general term for any grammatical case other than the nominative or vocative.
  172. Old Church Slavonic: The oldest documented Slavic language, used in religious texts and liturgy.
  173. Onomatopoeia: A word that imitates the sound associated with its meaning (e.g., "buzz," "clang").
  174. Open Syllable: A syllable that ends in a vowel rather than a consonant.
  175. Optative Mood: A grammatical mood used to express wishes, hopes, or desires.
  176. Orthographic Reform: Changes made to standardize or simplify a writing system.
  177. Orthographic Standardization: The process of establishing consistent spelling and writing conventions.
  178. Palatal Approximant: A consonant sound where the tongue approaches but does not touch the hard palate (e.g., "j" in "yes").
  179. Palatalization: A phonetic process where a consonant becomes closer to a palatal sound, often influenced by neighboring vowels.
  180. Paradigm: A set of related forms of a word showing its inflectional variations (e.g., verb conjugations).
  181. Paragogic Vowel: An extra vowel added at the end of a word, often for phonological reasons.
  182. Participle: A non-finite verb form used as an adjective or for tense formation (e.g., "running" in "running water").
  183. Particle Verb: A verb combined with a particle to create a specific meaning (e.g., "give up").
  184. Passive Voice: A sentence structure where the subject receives the action of the verb (e.g., "The book was read by her").
  185. Past Perfect: A verb tense expressing an action completed before another past action (e.g., "She had left").
  186. Past Tense: A verb tense used to describe actions that happened in the past.
  187. Perfect Aspect: A verb aspect indicating completed actions with relevance to the present.
  188. Periphrastic Construction: A grammatical construction using multiple words to express what could be conveyed by a single word or inflection (e.g., "will be running").
  189. Pharyngealization: A secondary articulation involving retraction of the root of the tongue toward the pharynx.
  190. Phoneme: The smallest unit of sound in a language that can distinguish meaning.
  191. Phonemic Orthography: A writing system where each grapheme corresponds to a single phoneme.
  192. Phonetics: The study of speech sounds and their physical properties.
  193. Phonological Process: A pattern of sound change or alteration in speech.
  194. Phonotactics: The rules governing the permissible arrangements of sounds in a language.
  195. Pitch Accent: A system where variations in pitch differentiate words or syllables.
  196. Plosive: A consonant sound produced by stopping airflow completely and then releasing it (e.g., "p," "t").
  197. Politeness Form: A grammatical or lexical form used to express politeness or formality.
  198. Polysynthetic Language: A language type where words often consist of multiple morphemes representing complex ideas.
  199. Portmanteau Morpheme: A single morpheme that expresses multiple grammatical meanings (e.g., French "au" for "à + le").
  200. Possessive Adjective: An adjective indicating ownership (e.g., "my," "his").
  201. Possessive Pronoun: A pronoun that shows ownership (e.g., "mine," "yours").
  202. Postposition: A type of adposition that follows the noun or pronoun it governs (e.g., "ago" in "two years ago").
  203. Prepositional Phrase: A phrase consisting of a preposition and its object (e.g., "in the house").
  204. Present Continuous: A verb tense used to describe actions currently happening (e.g., "I am reading").
  205. Present Perfect: A verb tense describing actions completed at an unspecified time in the past with relevance to the present (e.g., "I have finished").
  206. Present Tense: A verb tense describing actions happening now or general truths.
  207. Preterite: A verb tense used to describe actions completed in the past, common in certain languages.
  208. Productive Suffix: A suffix actively used to form new words in a language.
  209. Progressive Aspect: A verb aspect indicating ongoing or continuous actions (e.g., "She is working").
  210. Proto-Indo-European: The reconstructed common ancestor of the Indo-European languages.
  211. Proto-Slavic: The reconstructed common ancestor of the Slavic languages.
  212. Pulmonic Egressive: A speech sound produced by pushing air out of the lungs, the most common airflow mechanism in languages.
  213. Quantifier: A word or phrase expressing quantity (e.g., "all," "some," "many").
  214. Recessive Accent: An accent pattern where stress tends to move toward the beginning of a word.
  215. Reciprocal Pronoun: A pronoun expressing mutual actions or relationships (e.g., "each other").
  216. Reduplication: A morphological process where a word or part of a word is repeated to express a grammatical or semantic change.
  217. Reflexive Pronoun: A pronoun referring back to the subject of the sentence (e.g., "myself").
  218. Reflexive Verb: A verb where the subject and object are the same, often marked by a reflexive pronoun (e.g., "She washed herself").
  219. Register: A variety of language used in specific social contexts, ranging from formal to informal.
  220. Relative Clause: A subordinate clause that modifies a noun, often introduced by a relative pronoun (e.g., "who," "which").
  221. Relative Pronoun: A pronoun used to introduce a relative clause (e.g., "who," "which," "that").
  222. Retroflex Consonant: A consonant produced by curling the tongue back toward the palate (e.g., certain "r" sounds).
  223. Rhotic Consonant: A consonant associated with "r"-like sounds.
  224. Root Morpheme: The base or core part of a word that carries its primary meaning.
  225. Rounded Vowel: A vowel produced with rounded lips (e.g., "o" in "go").
  226. Sandhi: A set of phonological changes occurring at word or morpheme boundaries (e.g., assimilation).
  227. Schwa: The mid-central vowel sound represented by [ə], often found in unstressed syllables (e.g., the "a" in "sofa").
  228. Second Conjugation: A category of verbs sharing the same inflectional patterns, often identified by their endings.
  229. Second Declension: A category of nouns sharing similar inflectional endings, typically masculine or neuter.
  230. Semantic Drift: The gradual change in the meaning of a word over time.
  231. Semivowel: A sound that is phonetically similar to a vowel but functions as a consonant (e.g., "w," "y").
  232. Short Form Adjective: A form of an adjective used in certain contexts, often contrasting with a longer or more inflected form.
  233. Soft Sign: A Cyrillic letter (ь) that indicates palatalization of the preceding consonant.
  234. Sonorant: A speech sound produced with continuous airflow and minimal constriction, such as vowels, nasals, and liquids.
  235. Source Language: The language from which a word, phrase, or grammatical structure is borrowed or translated.
  236. Standard Language: A standardized form of a language used in formal settings and official communication.
  237. Stem-changing Verb: A verb that undergoes changes in its root or stem when conjugated (e.g., "write" → "wrote").
  238. Stress Accent: A feature of prosody where certain syllables are emphasized through greater loudness or pitch.
  239. Subjunctive Mood: A verb mood used to express doubts, wishes, or hypothetical situations.
  240. Sublation: A linguistic process where a feature or form is replaced by another over time.
  241. Subordinate Clause: A clause that cannot stand alone and depends on a main clause to form a complete sentence.
  242. Substantive: A term for a noun or a word functioning as a noun.
  243. Suffixation: The addition of a suffix to a root or stem to form a new word or change its grammatical function.
  244. Superlative Degree: The form of an adjective or adverb used to express the highest degree of a quality (e.g., "best," "most quickly").
  245. Syllabary: A writing system where each symbol represents a syllable.
  246. Syllable Nucleus: The central part of a syllable, typically a vowel or syllabic consonant.
  247. Syncope: The omission of sounds or syllables within a word (e.g., "family" pronounced as "famly").
  248. Syncretism: The merging of distinct grammatical forms into a single form (e.g., the same ending for different cases).
  249. Syntactic Ambiguity: A situation where a sentence can be interpreted in more than one way due to its structure.
  250. Syntactic Parsing: The process of analyzing a sentence to identify its grammatical components and structure.
  251. Synthetic Language: A type of language where grammatical relationships are expressed through inflection or word modifications, rather than word order.
  252. Telic Verb: A verb that implies a clear endpoint or goal for the action (e.g., "build a house").
  253. Temporal Adverb: An adverb that indicates time (e.g., "yesterday," "soon").
  254. Third Conjugation: A category of verbs sharing common inflectional patterns, typically based on their endings.
  255. Third Declension: A group of nouns with similar inflectional endings, often feminine or irregular in nature.
  256. Transcription: The written representation of spoken language, often using phonetic symbols.
  257. Transitive Verb: A verb that requires a direct object to complete its meaning (e.g., "She reads a book").
  258. Triphthong: A sequence of three vowel sounds pronounced within a single syllable.
  259. Umlaut: A phonological process where a vowel is altered to become more similar to a vowel in a nearby syllable.
  260. Unaccusative Verb: An intransitive verb where the subject experiences or undergoes the action (e.g., "The vase broke").
  261. Unaspirated Consonant: A consonant produced without a burst of air (e.g., the "p" in "spin").
  262. Uncountable Noun: A noun that cannot be pluralized and is measured in terms of quantity rather than number (e.g., "water," "information").
  263. Uvular Consonant: A consonant produced with the back of the tongue against the uvula (e.g., the French "r").
  264. Velar Consonant: A consonant produced with the back of the tongue against the soft palate (e.g., "k," "g").
  265. Velarization: A secondary articulation where the back of the tongue approaches the velum during the production of a consonant.
  266. Verbal Aspect: A grammatical category expressing how an action extends over time (e.g., imperfective vs. perfective).
  267. Verbal Noun: A noun derived from a verb that retains some verbal characteristics (e.g., "running" in "Running is fun").
  268. Verbal Prefix: A prefix added to a verb to modify its meaning, often related to aspect or direction.
  269. Verb-final Sentence: A sentence structure where the verb comes at the end (common in languages like Japanese).
  270. Verb-initial Sentence: A sentence structure where the verb appears first (e.g., in Celtic languages).
  271. Verb-second Rule: A syntactic rule where the verb always appears as the second element in a sentence (e.g., German main clauses).
  272. Vocative Case: A grammatical case used to address or call someone directly (e.g., "John!" in English).
  273. Voiced Consonant: A consonant sound produced with vibration of the vocal cords (e.g., "b," "d").
  274. Voiceless Consonant: A consonant sound produced without vocal cord vibration (e.g., "p," "t").
  275. Vowel Harmony: A phonological process where vowels within a word harmonize to share certain features (e.g., frontness or backness).
  276. Vowel Length: The duration of a vowel sound, which can distinguish meaning in some languages.
  277. Word Order: The arrangement of words in a sentence, which can vary by language and affect meaning.
  278. Zero Copula: The omission of the verb "to be" in sentences where it would normally occur (e.g., "She happy" instead of "She is happy").
  279. Zero Derivation: The creation of a new word without any change to the base form (e.g., using "run" as a noun or a verb).
  280. Zero Ending: The absence of an explicit ending on a word, typically in inflected forms (e.g., nominative singular in some nouns).
  281. Zero Morpheme: An implied grammatical morpheme that has no phonological realization.
  282. Zero-marking Language: A language that uses word order or context rather than explicit markers to indicate grammatical relationships.
  283. Zirconization: A playful or hypothetical linguistic term to denote the refinement or modernization of a language element.
