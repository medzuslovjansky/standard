# 9.1. Glossary

:::info

The glossary provides definitions for technical terms used throughout the specification. Users unfamiliar with specific linguistic terminology are encouraged to refer to this section for clarification.

:::

1. **Ablaut**: A system of vowel changes that indicates grammatical contrasts, such as tense or number.
2. **Abessive Case**: A grammatical case indicating absence or lack of something.
3. **Absolute Construction**: A phrase with a noun and a participle that functions independently within a sentence.
4. **Abstract Noun**: A noun that represents an idea, quality, or state, rather than a concrete object.
5. **Accentuation**: The application of stress or emphasis on certain syllables in words.
6. **Accusative Case**: A grammatical case used to mark the direct object of a verb.
7. **Active Voice**: A sentence structure where the subject performs the action of the verb.

9. **Adjective Comparison**: The formation of comparative and superlative forms of adjectives.
10. **Adjectival Declension**: The inflection of adjectives based on gender, number, and case.
11. **Adjunct**: An optional element in a sentence that adds information but is not essential to its structure.
12. **Adposition**: A preposition or postposition used to express relationships between elements in a sentence.
13. **Adverbial Modifier**: A word or phrase that modifies a verb, adjective, or other adverb, often providing information about time, manner, place, or degree.
14. **Affixation**: The process of adding prefixes, suffixes, infixes, or circumfixes to a root to form a new word.
15. **Affricate**: A consonant sound that begins as a stop and transitions into a fricative.
16. **Agglutinative Language**: A type of language in which words are formed by stringing together morphemes, each with a distinct meaning or grammatical function.
17. **Agreement Rules**: Grammatical rules requiring parts of a sentence to agree in features like gender, number, or case.
18. **Aorist Tense**: A past tense used to describe simple or completed actions.
19. **Apocope**: The loss of a sound or syllable at the end of a word.
20. **Apposition**: A grammatical construction in which two elements, usually noun phrases, are placed side by side, with one identifying or explaining the other.
21. **Archaism**: A word or expression that is outdated but used for stylistic or historical effect.
22. **Article**: A word used to define a noun as specific (definite) or unspecific (indefinite).

27. **Auxiliary Verb**: A verb used to form tenses, moods, or voices of other verbs (e.g., "to be," "to have").
28. **Back Vowel**: A vowel sound produced with the tongue positioned towards the back of the mouth.
29. **Balto-Slavic Languages**: A branch of the Indo-European language family including Baltic and Slavic languages.
30. **Bilabial Consonant**: A consonant sound made with both lips (e.g., "p," "b").
31. **Borrowing**: The adoption of words or phrases from another language.
32. **Cardinal Numeral**: A numeral that expresses quantity (e.g., "one," "two").
33. **Case Endings**: The inflectional suffixes that indicate grammatical case.
34. **Causative Verb**: A verb that expresses the action of causing something to happen.
35. **Cerebral Consonant**: A retroflex consonant produced with the tongue curled back towards the palate.
36. **Circumflex Accent**: A diacritical mark used to indicate a specific type of stress or tone.
37. **Clitic**: A word with syntactic independence but phonological dependence on another word.
38. **Codification**: The process of standardizing and developing rules for a language.
39. **Collective Numeral**: A numeral used to denote a group or set (e.g., "a pair," "a dozen").
40. **Common Slavic**: The hypothetical ancestor language of all Slavic languages.
41. **Comparative Degree**: The form of an adjective or adverb used to compare two entities.
42. **Complementizer**: A word used to introduce a subordinate clause (e.g., "that," "if").
