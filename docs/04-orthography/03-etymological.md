# 4.4. Etymological (Scientific) Alphabet

The Interslavic etymological alphabet — historically known as _Naučny Medžuslovjansky_ ("Scientific Interslavic"), also informally called **MS Plus** — is an OPTIONAL extension of the standard orthography.
It introduces additional diacritical letters that correspond to Proto-Slavic and Old Church Slavonic phonemes that have since diverged or disappeared in most modern Slavic languages.

The etymological alphabet does NOT constitute a separate language or dialect.
It is the same Interslavic written with additional diacritics layered on top of the standard spelling.
A reader unfamiliar with these diacritics MAY ignore them entirely and still read the text correctly using the standard pronunciation.

---

## 4.4.1. Purpose and Register

The etymological alphabet is intended for use in scholarly, etymological, and high-precision contexts.
It SHALL NOT be used as the default orthography in everyday communication.

Specifically, the etymological alphabet serves the following purposes:

- conveying etymological information in dictionaries, grammars, and linguistic studies;
- supporting language learning by making Interslavic words easier to connect to their counterparts in other Slavic languages;
- providing a more precise notation of the ideal (most central) pronunciation;
- enabling flavorization — the adaptation of Interslavic for speakers of a specific Slavic language (see §9);
- enabling the transcription of any Slavic text into a unified Interslavic orthographic form.

The etymological alphabet is a superset of the standard alphabet.
The standard forms are always legitimate simplifications of the etymological forms.
Users who choose to write etymologically are free to use any subset of the additional letters, with two restrictions:

1. A character, once adopted in a text or conversation, MUST be used consistently throughout.
2. Certain letters come in complementary pairs; using one member of a pair while omitting the other is discouraged.
   The established pairs are: **:latn[ę]/:latn[ų]**, **:latn[ė]/:latn[ȯ]**, **:latn[t́]/:latn[d́]** (or **:latn[ť]/:latn[ď]**), and **:latn[ś]/:latn[ź]**.

:::caution[Usage Guidance]
Text written with many etymological diacritics may appear intimidating to learners, who might incorrectly assume that the diacritics are obligatory or that etymological spelling is somehow superior.
When using this orthography, state explicitly why you are doing so and that these marks are optional additions.
:::

## 4.4.2. Additional Latin Characters

The etymological Latin alphabet adds **13 letters** to the standard 27-letter inventory.
These letters are divided into additional vowels and additional consonants.

### 4.4.2.1. Additional Vowels (5)

| Letter | IPA | Proto-Slavic / OCS source | Standard equivalent | Gloss (example) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| :latn[å] | :ipa[å ~ ɔ] | Liquid + vowel sequences (*tъrt, *tьrt, *tolt, *telt) | :latn[o] | :isv[gråh] → :isv[groh] (pea) |
| :latn[ė] | :ipa[ə] (fleeting) | Strong yer *ь [ĭ] | :latn[e] (or silent) | :isv[pės] → :isv[pes] (dog) |
| :latn[ę] | :ipa[ɛ̃ ~ ʲæ] | Proto-Slavic front nasal \*ę; OCS :cyrl[Ѧ] (_jus maloj_) | :latn[e] | :isv[pęť] → :isv[pet] (five) |
| :latn[ȯ] | :ipa[ə] (fleeting) | Strong yer *ъ [ŭ] | :latn[o] (or silent) | :isv[sȯn] → :isv[son] (dream) |
| :latn[ų] | :ipa[ʊ] | Proto-Slavic back nasal \*ǫ; OCS :cyrl[Ѫ] (_jus bolšoj_) | :latn[u] | :isv[rųka] → :isv[ruka] (hand) |

**Notes on :latn[å]:**
The vowel :latn[å] occurs exclusively in the sequences :latn[rå] and :latn[lå] followed by a consonant.
It corresponds to positions where Polish has `ro`/`ró` and `ło`/`łó`, and East Slavic has `(o)ro` and `(o)lo` (the _polnoglasije_ reflex).
It MUST NOT appear word-initially, after a vowel, or in positions other than after a liquid.

| Etymological | Standard | Polish | Russian | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| :latn[gråh] | :latn[groh] | `groch` | `горох` | pea |
| :latn[tråva] | :latn[trova] | `trawa` | `трава` | grass |
| :latn[mråz] | :latn[mroz] | `mróz` | `мороз` | frost |
| :latn[kråva] | :latn[krova] | `krowa` | `корова` | cow |
| :latn[glåva] | :latn[glova] | `głowa` | `голова` | head |

**Notes on :latn[ė] and :latn[ȯ] (fleeting vowels / strong yers):**
The vowels :latn[ė] and :latn[ȯ] represent strong yers: the front yer *ь and the back yer *ъ respectively.
In standard Interslavic they surface as :latn[e] and :latn[o]; the etymological notation flags them as historically yer-derived vowels that participate in the **fleeting vowel** alternation (they disappear in certain inflectional contexts).

| Etymological | Standard | Gloss | Alternation example |
|---|---|---|---|
| :latn[pės] | :latn[pes] | dog | nom. :isv[pes] → gen. :isv[psa] |
| :latn[sȯn] | :latn[son] | dream | nom. :isv[son] → gen. :isv[sna] |
| :latn[lėv] | :latn[lev] | lion | nom. :isv[lev] → gen. :isv[lva] |
| :latn[dȯšť] | :latn[došť] | rain | nom. :isv[došť] → gen. :isv[dšťа] |

It is also acceptable to write :latn[è] and :latn[ò] instead of :latn[ė] and :latn[ȯ].

**Notes on :latn[ę] (front nasal):**
Historically, :latn[ę] was the Proto-Slavic front nasal vowel :ipa[ɛ̃].
In modern Interslavic it is NOT pronounced as a nasal but as :ipa[ʲæ] — intermediate between :ipa[a] and :ipa[e] with palatalization of the preceding consonant.
Acceptable realizations also include :ipa[ʲa] (analogous to Russian _я_), :ipa[ʲɛ] (analogous to Czech _ě_), or simply :ipa[e].
In simplified Interslavic, :latn[ę] is written and read as :latn[e]; the etymological distinction is meaningful: e.g., :isv[česť] "honour" vs. :isv[čęsť] "part/portion."

The following table illustrates the reflexes of etymological :latn[ę] across Slavic languages:

| Etymological | OCS | Russian | Ukrainian | Polish | Czech | Slovak | S/Cr/Sln | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| :isv[ręd] | рѧдъ | ряд | ряд | rząd | řád | rád | red | row, order |
| :isv[desęť] | десѧть | десять | десять | dziesięć | deset | desať | deset | ten |
| :isv[język] | ѩзыкъ | язык | язик | język | jazyk | jazyk | jezik | language |
| :isv[pamęť] | памѧть | память | пам'ять | pamięć | paměť | pamäť | pamet | memory |

**Notes on :latn[ų] (back nasal):**
Historically, :latn[ų] was the Proto-Slavic back nasal vowel :ipa[ɔ̃].
In modern Interslavic it is NOT pronounced as a nasal but as :ipa[ʊ] — a sound intermediate between :ipa[u] and :ipa[o].
It is also acceptable to pronounce it simply as :ipa[u], making no spoken distinction between :latn[ų] and :latn[u].
The grapheme :latn[ų] was chosen over :latn[ǫ] because in the majority of Slavic languages this vowel shifted to :ipa[u].

| Etymological | OCS | Russian | Bulgarian | Macedonian | Polish | Slovene | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| :isv[pųť] | пѫть | путь | път | пат | pąć | pot | path |
| :isv[dųb] | дѫбъ | дуб | дъб | даб | dąb | dob | oak |
| :isv[sųd] | сѫдъ | суд | съд | суд | sąd | sod | court |
| :isv[rųka] | рѫка | рука | ръка | рака | ręka | roka | hand |

**Notes on :latn[ć] and :latn[đ]:**
These two letters are the only etymological consonants that require a non-trivial substitution in standard orthography.
All other etymological consonants simplify to their base letter (losing only the diacritic); :latn[ć] and :latn[đ] map to :latn[č] and :latn[dž] respectively.
Using :latn[ć] where standard orthography requires :latn[č] (and vice versa) constitutes an error, not merely a stylistic choice.

**Notes on :latn[ĺ] (:latn[ľ]) and :latn[ń]:**
These letters occur only before a consonant (including before :latn[j]).
Phonetically they are identical to :latn[lj] and :latn[nj]; the difference is that :latn[ĺ]/:latn[ń] mark etymologically justified softness that is NOT universally reflected in standard Interslavic digraphs.
The letters :latn[ĺ] and :latn[ľ] are interchangeable variants of the same letter; similarly, the letters :latn[t́] and :latn[ť], and :latn[d́] and :latn[ď], are interchangeable pairs.

**Notes on :latn[t́]/:latn[ť] and :latn[d́]/:latn[ď]:**
These letters do not appear in any living Slavic alphabet.
In Unicode they can only be formed with a combining diacritic (acute accent or haček), which means display may vary across fonts.
When using a haček form (:latn[ť], :latn[ď]), note that in many fonts the haček is rendered as an apostrophe.

## 4.4.3. Additional Cyrillic Characters

In the Latin etymological alphabet, all additional information is conveyed exclusively by diacritical marks layered onto the standard letters.
A reader who does not recognize a diacritic can always fall back to the standard reading.

An analogous approach for Cyrillic — using modified characters such as :cyrl[ӑ], :cyrl[ԙ], :cyrl[ө], :cyrl[ұ], :cyrl[ҷ], :cyrl[ӝ], etc. — would produce text that appears extremely artificial and would be of little help, since the modifications would not be self-explanatory.
An extended Cyrillic alphabet using historical OCS characters (:cyrl[ѣ], :cyrl[ѩ], :cyrl[ѫ]) or regional characters (:cyrl[ћ], :cyrl[ђ]) would be difficult to read for anyone not familiar with Old Church Slavonic orthography, thus defeating the purpose of Interslavic.

For these reasons, **the Interslavic etymological alphabet has no standard Cyrillic equivalent**.

However, in scholarly publications concerned specifically with the history of Slavic languages, the following OCS-derived Cyrillic letters are used conventionally for the etymological vowels:

| Latin (etym.) | Cyrillic (conventional) | OCS name | Standard Cyrillic equiv. |
|---|---|---|---|
| :latn[ę] | :cyrl[ѧ] | _jus maloj_ (small yus) | :cyrl[е] |
| :latn[ų] | :cyrl[ѫ] | _jus bolšoj_ (big yus) | :cyrl[у] |
| :latn[ję-] (word-initial) | :cyrl[ѩ] | _jotated small yus_ | :cyrl[е] |
| :latn[jų-] (word-initial) | :cyrl[ѭ] | _jotated big yus_ | :cyrl[ю] |

These Cyrillic historical forms MAY be used in scholarly contexts but are NOT part of the normative Interslavic Cyrillic orthography.
Their use in ordinary Interslavic texts is explicitly discouraged.

### 4.4.4.3. Scope Restrictions

The etymological alphabet does NOT include:

- vowel length or tone markers;
- special letters for non-Slavic loanwords (e.g., OCS :cyrl[ѳ] and :cyrl[ѵ] used only for Greek words, or :latn[ü] for words of Germanic or French origin);
- any marker that would be obligatory rather than optional.

The etymological alphabet is designed so that any text written in it is also valid standard Interslavic text when the diacritics are stripped.
This property ensures backward compatibility with tools and readers that handle only the standard orthography.
