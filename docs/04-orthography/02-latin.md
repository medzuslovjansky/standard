# Standard Latin and Cyrillic Orthography
Interslavic has two fully equivalent standard alphabets — one Latin-based and one Cyrillic-based — reflecting the historical division of Slavic writing traditions. Neither alphabet is based on any single national orthography; both were designed as compromises that are intuitively readable across the Slavic world and that permit straightforward, unambiguous transliteration between the two scripts. Published texts are encouraged to provide both Latin and Cyrillic versions so that readers on either side of the Latin–Cyrillic boundary can engage with the text without difficulty.

## Core Latin Alphabet (27 Letters)
The standard Interslavic Latin alphabet consists of 27 letters. It uses 23 letters of the basic Latin alphabet — all letters except :latn[q], :latn[w], and :latn[x] — plus four letters carrying a caron diacritic: :latn[č], :latn[š], :latn[ž], and :latn[ě].

| Letter | IPA | Notes |
|--------|-----|-------|

| :latn[B b] | :ipa[b] | Voiced bilabial stop |
| :latn[C c] | :ipa[t͡s] | Voiceless alveolar affricate; as in :isv[car] |

The letters :latn[q], :latn[w], and :latn[x] do not belong to the standard alphabet. They appear only in unadapted loanwords from non-Slavic languages.

All vowels are pronounced fully in all positions; vowel reduction does not apply — Interslavic has high vowel quality, meaning vowels in unstressed positions are pronounced the same as in stressed positions. Word-final devoicing is optional: most Slavic languages apply it (except Ukrainian and Shtokavian), and speakers may follow their native habits. Consonants are hard except before :latn[ě] (where softening is obligatory), and the permanently soft sounds written with digraphs :latn[lj] and :latn[nj]. Before :latn[i], softening is permitted but not required.

## Digraphs
The standard Latin orthography recognizes three digraphs that function as single phonemes:

| Digraph | IPA | Notes |
|---------|-----|-------|
| :latn[Dž dž] | :ipa[d͡ʒ] | Voiced postalveolar affricate; the voiced counterpart of :latn[č] |
| :latn[Lj lj] | :ipa[ʎ] ~ :ipa[lʲ] | Palatal lateral approximant; always soft: :isv[ljubov], :isv[polje] |
| :latn[Nj nj] | :ipa[ɲ] ~ :ipa[nʲ] | Palatal nasal; always soft: :isv[njuhati], :isv[konj] |

The sequence :latn[šč] (:ipa[ʃt͡ʃ]) also occurs in Interslavic and corresponds to Russian :cyrl[щ], as in :isv[čudovišče], :isv[zaščita]. It is a cluster of two distinct phonemes, not a digraph.

The sequence :latn[rj] also occurs frequently in Interslavic (e.g., :isv[morje], :isv[burja], :isv[zarje]) and behaves phonetically like a soft counterpart of :isv[r], parallel to how :latn[lj] and :latn[nj] function as soft counterparts of :isv[l] and :isv[n]. However, unlike :latn[lj] and :latn[nj], the Commission has not formally recognized :latn[rj] as a digraph or phoneme pair. In practice, nearly all occurrences of :latn[rj] in the Interslavic lexicon trace back to Proto-Slavic soft \*r (via iotation: \*rj), which makes :latn[rj] a de facto digraph in everything but name.

:::info[Note on digraphs and yer-derived softness]
The digraphs :latn[lj] and :latn[nj] in standard orthography represent **iotation-derived softness** — that is, softness arising from Proto-Slavic iotation (\*l + \*j, \*n + \*j), which is reflected in all Slavic languages. A distinct phenomenon, **yer-derived softness** (from Proto-Slavic \*lь, \*nь — consonant adjacent to a front yer), produces the same phonetic result in Northern Slavic but a hard consonant in Southern Slavic. In the etymological register (MS Plus), yer-derived softness is written with háček letters :latn[ĺ] and :latn[ń], while the standard orthography writes the consonant as hard. The digraphs :latn[lj] and :latn[nj] should therefore be used only for iotation-derived softness. See the relevant section for details.
:::

**Capitalization of digraphs.** When a digraph begins a capitalized word or sentence, only the first letter is capitalized: :latn[Dž], :latn[Lj], :latn[Nj]. In all-caps text all letters are capitalized: :latn[DŽ], :latn[LJ], :latn[NJ].

## Special Characters and Diacritics
The four letters with diacritics — :latn[č], :latn[š], :latn[ž], and :latn[ě] — are integral members of the standard alphabet and are mandatory in normative writing. They are not stylistic variants; each represents a phoneme distinct from its unaccented base letter.

**Č, Š, Ž** carry a caron (háček). Omitting the caron and writing :latn[c], :latn[s], :latn[z] in their place produces a different phoneme and therefore a different word. These substitutions are not acceptable in standard orthography.

**Ě ě** (the *yat* letter) represents a front vowel that causes softening of the preceding consonant in most Slavic languages (all except Slovene, Serbian, and Macedonian). Its Cyrillic equivalent is :cyrl[є], borrowed from Ukrainian. When a speaker does not know the correct distribution of :latn[ě], writing plain :latn[e] is an accepted simplification, analogous to the optional use of :cyrl[ё] in Russian. The distinction is nonetheless recommended in careful writing.

**Y y** represents the close central (or back) unrounded vowel :ipa[ɨ], familiar to Russian, Belarusian, and Polish speakers. In South Slavic languages and in spoken Czech and Slovak this vowel has merged with :ipa[i]. Writers targeting a predominantly South Slavic audience, or those uncertain about the correct distribution, may substitute :latn[i] for :latn[y] throughout.

## The Sequences `tj` and `dj` in Standard Orthography
A number of words in the Interslavic dictionary contain the letter sequences :latn[tj] and :latn[dj]: e.g., :isv[bratja], :isv[kostjum], :isv[tjulenj], :isv[tjutjun], :isv[tjuŕma], :isv[inoplanetjan] (with :latn[tj]); :isv[adjutant], :isv[gospodja], :isv[Madjar] (with :latn[dj]). These sequences create a systematic ambiguity in the standard orthography.

Standard Interslavic does not have soft :ipa[/tʲ/] or soft :ipa[/dʲ/] as core phonemes — those sounds exist only in the etymological register (MS Plus), where they are written :latn[ť] and :latn[ď] and derive from Proto-Slavic yer-softened consonants. The sequences :latn[tj] and :latn[dj] in the standard alphabet are therefore intended to represent two separate hard sounds — :ipa[[t]] followed by :ipa[[j]], and :ipa[[d]] followed by :ipa[[j]] — not a softened consonant. However, to any reader familiar with Slavic orthographic conventions, :latn[tj] visually suggests a soft :ipa[[tʲ]] and :latn[dj] suggests a soft :ipa[[dʲ]], since this is precisely how softness is marked in many Slavic languages (cf. Russian :cyrl[тья], Croatian :latn[đ] from historical :latn[dj]).

This ambiguity is compounded by inconsistent behaviour of the Latin-to-Cyrillic transliterator: :latn[dj] is rendered with an apostrophe (e.g., :cyrl[Мад'яр], :cyrl[господ'я]), suggesting that the two sounds are separate, while :latn[tj] is rendered without one (e.g., :cyrl[братя], :cyrl[тютюн]), suggesting a soft consonant. The two treatments are contradictory.

:::warning[Unresolved: Status of `tj` and `dj` Sequences]
Standard Interslavic lacks soft :ipa[/tʲ/] and :ipa[/dʲ/] as phonemes, yet the orthography contains :latn[tj] and :latn[dj] sequences that imply them — creating ambiguity about pronunciation and transliteration. The following solutions have been proposed:

1. **Remove :latn[tj]/:latn[dj] where a natural alternative exists.** Several of these words have well-attested Slavic forms without a soft :ipa[/t/] or :ipa[/d/]: :isv[tjulenj] → :isv[tulenj] (cf. Czech _tuleň_), :isv[tjutjun] → :isv[tutun] or :isv[tytun], :isv[gospodja] → :isv[gospodža] (cf. Russian _госпожа_, Serbian _госпођа_, Macedonian _госпоѓа_), :isv[Madjar] → :isv[Madžar] (cf. Russian _мадьяр_ but also the widely attested affricate form).
2. **Retain :latn[tj]/:latn[dj] only at prefix or morpheme boundaries**, where the sequence genuinely represents two separate hard sounds — e.g., :isv[ad-jutant] (Latin prefix _ad-_ + root _jutant_), :isv[kon-jugacija]. In these cases the pronunciation should be explicitly marked as :ipa[[tj]] / :ipa[[dj]] (two sounds, hard :ipa[[t]]/​:ipa[[d]] + :ipa[[j]]).
3. **For MS Plus**, allow :latn[ť] and :latn[ď] explicitly in borrowed words where a soft :ipa[/tʲ/] or :ipa[/dʲ/] is appropriate (e.g., :isv[ťuŕma], :isv[kosťum]), extending their use beyond the Proto-Slavic yer-derived context.

Until a resolution is adopted, writers should be aware that :latn[tj] and :latn[dj] in standard orthography are intended as hard :ipa[[t]] + :ipa[[j]] and hard :ipa[[d]] + :ipa[[j]], not as soft consonants.
:::

## Keyboard-Friendly Fallback Spellings
When a writer cannot produce diacritic characters on a given keyboard or input system, the following substitutions are permitted in informal digital contexts (messaging, social media, plain-text environments). Fallback spellings are **not** accepted in normative documents, dictionaries, or formal publications.

| Standard | Fallback options | Notes |
|----------|-----------------|-------|
| :latn[č] | :latn[cz], :latn[cx] | Do **not** use :latn[ch]; West Slavs read :latn[ch] as :ipa[x] |
| :latn[š] | :latn[sz], :latn[sx] | |
| :latn[ž] | :latn[zs], :latn[zx] | Do **not** use :latn[zh]; Polish :latn[ż] → :latn[zs] is natural for Polish speakers |
| :latn[ě] | :latn[ie], :latn[je], or :latn[e] | Plain :latn[e] is the simplest and most widely understood option |
| :latn[y] | :latn[i] | Acceptable simplification; see the relevant section above |

Consistency is required within a single text or conversation. A writer who adopts :latn[sz] for :latn[š] must maintain that choice throughout and must not simultaneously use :latn[š] or :latn[sx]. Mixing different fallback conventions creates ambiguity.

The so-called *Volapük encoding* (using :latn[q], :latn[w], :latn[x] as substitutes for Slavic characters) is explicitly discouraged. Non-letter characters such as `^`, `*`, or `'` attached to base letters are similarly discouraged. The English-based digraphs :latn[ch], :latn[sh], :latn[zh] are problematic because :latn[ch] is read as :ipa[x] by West Slavic speakers and :latn[sh]/:latn[zh] are unfamiliar in any Slavic tradition.

## Special Cyrillic Letters
Five letters in the Cyrillic alphabet require specific comment because they are not universal across all Cyrillic-script Slavic languages.

**Є є** (= Latin :latn[ě]). The Cyrillic rendering of the yat phoneme. The Commission chose this letter from Ukrainian, where it has a pronunciation (:ipa[[jɛ]]) that ideally reflects the Interslavic pronunciation of yat. The historically more correct character :cyrl[ѣ] (the traditional yat) was deliberately replaced for the sake of maximum intelligibility: :cyrl[ѣ] is not used in any living Slavic language and is unfamiliar to most readers. Writing plain :cyrl[е] instead of :cyrl[є] is an accepted simplification, exactly parallel to writing :latn[e] instead of :latn[ě] in the Latin script.

**Ы ы** (= Latin :latn[y]). This letter represents the close central unrounded vowel :ipa[ɨ]. It is familiar to East Slavic and Polish readers but absent from South Slavic languages. Writers who are uncertain about its distribution, or who address a predominantly South Slavic audience, may substitute :cyrl[и] throughout.

**Ј ј** (= Latin :latn[j]). Interslavic adopts this letter from Serbian and Macedonian Cyrillic rather than using Russian/Ukrainian :cyrl[й], because :cyrl[й] has distributional restrictions (it cannot freely precede consonants or appear in all positions that :latn[j] can). For writers who cannot produce :cyrl[ј], the following context-sensitive substitutions are recommended:

- :cyrl[ја] → :cyrl[я], :cyrl[ју] → :cyrl[ю] (iotated vowels; most natural solution, though less familiar to some Serbian and Macedonian readers)
- :cyrl[јо]: write as :cyrl[јо]; Russian :cyrl[ё] is to be avoided as it carries different connotations
- :cyrl[је] after a vowel: write :cyrl[е]; after a consonant: write :cyrl[ье]; word-initially: write :cyrl[је]
- :cyrl[ји] after a consonant: write :cyrl[ьи]; elsewhere: write :cyrl[и]

**Љ љ** (= Latin :latn[lj]). From Serbian and Macedonian Cyrillic. Writers without this letter on their keyboard may write :cyrl[ль] instead.

**Њ њ** (= Latin :latn[nj]). From Serbian and Macedonian Cyrillic. Writers without this letter on their keyboard may write :cyrl[нь] instead.

## Digraphs in Cyrillic
The standard Cyrillic orthography uses one digraph and one common cluster:

| Cyrillic | Latin | IPA | Notes |
|----------|-------|-----|-------|
| :cyrl[Дж дж] | :latn[Dž dž] | :ipa[d͡ʒ] | Standard Cyrillic digraph for the voiced affricate |
| :cyrl[Шч шч] | :latn[Šč šč] | :ipa[ʃt͡ʃ] | Cluster corresponding to Russian :cyrl[щ]; not a true digraph |

The letter :cyrl[щ] is not part of the standard Interslavic Cyrillic alphabet, though it may appear in texts aimed at a Bulgarian readership as an informal variant of :cyrl[шч].

## Script Parity and Usage Guidelines
Both alphabets are considered fully equal in status. The choice of script is the writer's prerogative and reflects audience and context rather than any linguistic hierarchy. The following practical guidelines apply to both scripts:

1. Follow the standard Latin or Cyrillic orthography as closely as possible to ensure maximum intelligibility, especially for readers new to Interslavic.
2. Do not mix Latin and Cyrillic letters within a single word or sentence.
3. Keyboard layouts for both scripts are available and are the recommended input method for standard orthography.
4. When a diacritic character cannot be produced, use the established fallback spellings ( for Latin;  for Cyrillic) rather than ad hoc substitutions.
5. Maintain consistency: if a fallback spelling is chosen, apply it uniformly throughout the entire text.
6. Do not use archaic Cyrillic characters (:cyrl[ѣ], :cyrl[ѩ], :cyrl[ѫ], :cyrl[ѱ], etc.) in the standard orthography. Their use in running text seriously impedes intelligibility for modern readers.
