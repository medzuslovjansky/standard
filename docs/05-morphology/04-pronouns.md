# 5.5. Pronouns

This section specifies the forms, inflection, and usage rules for all pronominal categories in Interslavic. All normative statements use MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, and MAY as defined in RFC 2119.

---

## 5.5.1. Personal Pronouns

### 5.5.1.1. Paradigm: 1st, 2nd, 3rd person singular and plural

Personal pronouns in Interslavic inflect for six cases (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, instrumental, locative). There is no vocative for pronouns. The reflexive pronoun :isv[sebe] inflects identically to the second-person singular but has no nominative form.

Third-person pronouns also distinguish gender (masculine, feminine, neuter in the singular) and animacy (masculine plural only: :isv[oni] for groups containing male referents, :isv[one] for all others). The third person is historically derived from demonstrative pronouns and therefore behaves differently from first- and second-person forms.

**Full paradigm — singular:**

| Case | 1sg :isv[ja] | 2sg :isv[ty] | 3sg m. :isv[on] | 3sg n. :isv[ono] | 3sg f. :isv[ona] |
|------|----------|----------|-------------|--------------|--------------|
| Nom. | :isv[ja] | :isv[ty] | :isv[on] | :isv[ono] | :isv[ona] |
| Acc. | :isv[mene, mę] | :isv[tebe, tę] | :isv[jego, go] | :isv[jego, go] | :isv[jų] |
| Gen. | :isv[mene] | :isv[tebe] | :isv[jego] | :isv[jego] | :isv[jej] |
| Dat. | :isv[mně, mi] | :isv[tobě, ti] | :isv[jemu, mu] | :isv[jemu, mu] | :isv[jej] |
| Ins. | :isv[mnojų] | :isv[tobojų] | :isv[jim] | :isv[jim] | :isv[jejų] |
| Loc. | :isv[(o) mně] | :isv[(o) tobě] | :isv[(o) njem] | :isv[(o) njem] | :isv[(o) njej] |

**Full paradigm — plural:**

| Case | 1pl :isv[my] | 2pl :isv[vy] | 3pl m. :isv[oni] | 3pl f./n. :isv[one] |
|------|----------|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Nom. | :isv[my] | :isv[vy] | :isv[oni] | :isv[one] |
| Acc. | :isv[nas] | :isv[vas] | :isv[jih] | :isv[jih] |
| Gen. | :isv[nas] | :isv[vas] | :isv[jih] | :isv[jih] |
| Dat. | :isv[nam] | :isv[vam] | :isv[jim] | :isv[jim] |
| Ins. | :isv[nami] | :isv[vami] | :isv[jimi] | :isv[jimi] |
| Loc. | :isv[(o) nas] | :isv[(o) vas] | :isv[(o) njih] | :isv[(o) njih] |

**Notes on the third-person plural:**

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The source material does not describe this distinction as "obligatory" nor specify the exact conditions for using :isv[oni] vs. :isv[one] in this level of detail.
:::

In the plural, Interslavic maintains two nominative forms: :isv[oni] is used for groups that include at least one male person or male animal; :isv[one] is used for all other groups (exclusively female, mixed-gender inanimate, neuter). This distinction is obligatory in the nominative.

### 5.5.1.2. Long vs. short (clitic) forms

Several personal pronouns have two sets of forms: a **long (full) form** and a **short (clitic) form**. The clitic forms appear in parentheses in the paradigm above (e.g., :isv[mę], :isv[tę], :isv[go], :isv[mi], :isv[ti], :isv[mu]).

The following rules govern their distribution:

1. The long form MUST be used after a preposition: :isv[k mně] "to me", :isv[za tebe] "for you", :isv[pri njim] "with him".
2. The long form MUST be used in emphatic or contrastive contexts: :isv[Podavali sųt ne mně, a jemu] "They served not me but him."
3. The long form SHOULD be used in sentence-initial position: :isv[Mně ješče ne podali sųt obědų] "They have not yet served me lunch."
4. The short clitic form SHOULD be used in all other positions (unstressed, non-initial, not after a preposition): :isv[Prinesi mi tų knigų] "Bring me that book"; :isv[Vidiš li mę?] "Do you see me?"

Because not all verb endings are equally transparent to all speakers of Slavic languages, pro-drop (omitting personal pronouns when they are the subject of a clause) is NOT RECOMMENDED. Speakers SHOULD include the subject pronoun explicitly: :isv[ja čitaju] is preferred over bare :isv[čitaju], though the latter is not ungrammatical.

The pronoun :isv[on] refers to any noun of masculine grammatical gender, not only to male beings. The pronoun :isv[ona] refers to any noun of feminine grammatical gender.

## 5.5.2. Reflexive Pronouns

### 5.5.2.1. Sebe / sę paradigm

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The source material consistently writes the reflexive clitic as :isv[se] (e.g. :isv[Ja myju se]), not :isv[sę]. The use of :isv[sę] throughout this section cannot be verified against the source.
:::

The reflexive pronoun :isv[sebe] has no nominative form. It does not inflect for person, number, or gender. Its case endings are identical to those of the second-person singular pronoun :isv[ty].

| Case | Long form | Short (clitic) form |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| Nom. | — | — |
| Acc. | :isv[sebe] | :isv[sę] |
| Gen. | :isv[sebe] | — |
| Dat. | :isv[sobě] | :isv[si] |
| Ins. | :isv[sobojų] | — |
| Loc. | :isv[(o) sobě] | — |

The short clitic :isv[sę] is used with reflexive verbs: :isv[Ja myju sę] "I am washing myself." When emphasis is required, the long form MUST be used: :isv[Ja myju jedino sebe] "I wash nobody but myself." After a preposition, the long form MUST be used: :isv[on boji sę sebe].

The absence of number and person inflection in the reflexive pronoun means it can indicate that the action of a transitive verb is directed at the subject regardless of whether that subject is singular or plural.

The reflexive pronoun also serves as a **reciprocal** pronoun. :isv[Oni bijut sę] may mean either "They are hitting themselves" or "They are hitting each other." To make the reciprocal reading explicit, the formula :isv[jedin drugogo] SHOULD be added: :isv[Oni bijut sę jedin drugogo].

### 5.5.2.2. Reflexive possessive: svoj

The reflexive possessive :isv[svoj] "one's own" is used whenever the possessor is coreferential with the grammatical subject of the same clause, regardless of person or number.

:isv[Ja myju svoje avto] "I am washing my (own) car."
:isv[My ljubimo svoju zemlju] "We love our (own) land."
:isv[Pjotr dal Ivanu svoju knigu] "Pjotr gave Ivan his [= Pjotr's] book."
:isv[Pjotr dal Ivanu jegovu knigu] "Pjotr gave Ivan his [= Ivan's] book."

The contrast above is normatively significant: when the subject in a third-person clause is also the possessor, :isv[svoj] MUST be used to express that meaning; use of :isv[jego] / :isv[jej] / :isv[jih] unambiguously refers to a possessor distinct from the subject.

## 5.5.3. Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns agree with the noun they modify in gender, number, and case. They are inflected like adjectives, with the exception of the masculine nominative singular (and inanimate accusative singular), where the zero ending is used rather than the adjectival :isv[-y] / :isv[-i].

The core possessive pronouns are:

| Pronoun | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| :isv[moj] | my, mine |
| :isv[tvoj] | your, yours (singular familiar) |
| :isv[naš] | our, ours |
| :isv[vaš] | your, yours (plural or formal) |
| :isv[svoj] | one's own (reflexive possessive; see §5.5.2.2) |

|------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|----------|
| Nom. | :isv[moj] | :isv[moj] | :isv[moje] | :isv[moja] | :isv[moji] | :isv[moje] | :isv[moje] | :isv[moje] |

The pronouns :isv[naš] and :isv[vaš] follow the soft adjectival pattern (:isv[svěži]-type) in their oblique forms. The pronouns :isv[moj], :isv[tvoj], :isv[naš], :isv[vaš], :isv[svoj], and :isv[čij] decline like :isv[svěži]; see §5.3.2.2.

**Third-person possessives:**

For third-person possession, Interslavic most commonly uses the genitive form of the corresponding personal pronoun, which is uninflected:

| Form | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| :isv[jego] | his, its (masc./neut.) |
| :isv[jej] | her, its (fem.) |
| :isv[jih] | their |

These genitive-possessive forms are not declined and do not change to agree with the possessed noun.

Alternatively, the following adjectival possessive forms MAY be used. These are declined like hard adjectives (:isv[dobry]-type):

| Form | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| :isv[jegov, jegova, jegovo] | his |
| :isv[jejin, jejina, jejino] | her |
| :isv[jihny, jihna, jihno] | their |

The genitive forms (:isv[jego], :isv[jej], :isv[jih]) are preferred in standard Interslavic. The adjectival forms (:isv[jegov], :isv[jejin], :isv[jihny]) are fully acceptable and reflect patterns found in several Slavic literary norms.

**Interrogative and indefinite possessives:**

The interrogative possessive pronoun :isv[čij] "whose" and its derived forms :isv[ničij] "nobody's", :isv[něčij] "somebody's", etc. are inflected like :isv[moj]. For the full system of derived possessives see §5.5.6 (indefinite pronouns) and §5.5.8 (correlative table).

## 5.5.4. Demonstrative Pronouns

### 5.5.4.1. Primary demonstrative: toj / ten

The primary demonstrative pronoun is :isv[toj] (masculine), :isv[ta] (feminine), :isv[to] (neuter), meaning "this / that". This form SHOULD be used whenever there is no need to distinguish explicitly between proximal "this" and distal "that".

The variant form :isv[ten] for the masculine nominative singular is used in western Slavic contexts; in other contexts :isv[toj] is the preferred form.

### 5.5.4.2. Proximal vs. distal distinction: tutoj and tamtoj

When a speaker needs to explicitly distinguish between "this one (here)" and "that one (over there)", Interslavic provides:

- :isv[tutoj] "this (near the speaker)"
- :isv[tamtoj] "that (over there, distant from both speaker and addressee)"

Both :isv[tutoj] and :isv[tamtoj] are declined exactly like :isv[toj] (see table above). These forms SHOULD be used when spatial deixis needs to be made explicit.

### 5.5.4.3. Three-way deictic distinction (archaic/literary)

A more precise three-way deictic system exists and MAY be used, particularly in literary or more archaic registers:

| Pronoun | Nominative | Meaning |
|---------|------------|---------|
| :isv[sej] | :isv[sej / sa / se] | "this" (near the speaker) |
| :isv[toj] | :isv[toj / ta / to] | "that" (near the addressee) |
| :isv[onoj] | :isv[onoj / ona / ono] | "yonder" (distant from both) |
| :isv[ovoj] | :isv[ovoj / ova / ovo] | "this" (near the speaker; variant of :isv[sej]) |

The pronoun :isv[sej] has largely disappeared from most modern Slavic languages except in fossilized forms. Its use may therefore not be immediately transparent to all readers. Speakers choosing this system SHOULD ensure context makes meaning clear.

## 5.5.5. Interrogative and Relative Pronouns

### 5.5.5.1. Kto (who) and čto / što (what)

The primary interrogative pronouns are :isv[kto] "who?" and :isv[čto] (also written :isv[što]) "what?". These pronouns do not inflect for number or gender.

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The source material mentions the inflection of :isv[kto] and :isv[čto] but does not provide the actual case forms in the available text. The specific forms in this table (e.g. :isv[kym / kěm], :isv[čim]) cannot be verified against the source.
:::

| Case | :isv[kto] | :isv[čto] |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| Nom. | :isv[kto] | :isv[čto] |
| Acc. | :isv[kogo] | :isv[čto] |
| Gen. | :isv[kogo] | :isv[čego] |
| Dat. | :isv[komu] | :isv[čemu] |
| Ins. | :isv[kym / kěm] | :isv[čim] |
| Loc. | :isv[(o) kom] | :isv[(o) čem] |

Both :isv[kto] and :isv[čto] serve as interrogative pronouns in direct and indirect questions, and as relative pronouns in subordinate clauses. The same uninflected form functions in both roles:

- :isv[Oni sųt besědovali o tom, čto bylo je pisano v gazetah.] "They were talking about what had been written in the newspapers."
- :isv[Ja zapytal sòm, u kogo oni sųt kupili ovų rybų.] "I asked from whom they bought that fish."

### 5.5.5.2. Ktory / koj (which, relative)

The pronoun :isv[ktory] "which, that" is the standard relative pronoun. It inflects like a hard adjective (:isv[dobry]-type) and agrees with its antecedent in gender and number; it takes its case from its function in the relative clause.

The South Slavic alternative :isv[koj] is inflected like :isv[moj] and is fully interchangeable with :isv[ktory] in both relative and interrogative uses.

A third, more archaic option is :isv[iže]. In the nominative it is invariable across genders and numbers. In oblique cases it is formed from the corresponding form of the personal pronoun :isv[on] / :isv[ona] / :isv[ono] with the suffix :isv[-že]: genitive masculine singular :isv[jegože], dative masculine singular :isv[jemuže], etc. This form MAY be used in literary or archaic-register texts.

### 5.5.5.3. Other interrogative pronouns: kaky, čij, koliko

Additional interrogative determiners and pronouns include:

| Form | Meaning | Inflection |
|------|---------|-----------|
| :isv[koj] | which? | like :isv[moj] |
| :isv[ktory] | which? | hard adjective |
| :isv[čij] | whose? | like :isv[moj] (zero ending in masc. nom. sg.) |
| :isv[kaky] | what kind of? | hard adjective |
| :isv[koliko] | how many/much? | uninflected |
| :isv[kak] | how? | uninflected (adverb) |
| :isv[kdě] | where? | uninflected (adverb) |
| :isv[kamo] | whither? (goal) | uninflected (adverb) |
| :isv[kųdy] | whither? (path) | uninflected (adverb) |
| :isv[odkųdy] | whence? | uninflected (adverb) |
| :isv[kogda] | when? | uninflected (adverb) |
| :isv[čemu] | why? (cause) | uninflected (adverb) |
| :isv[začto] | why? (purpose) | uninflected (adverb) |

The pronoun :isv[čij] "whose?" is declined like :isv[moj] with zero ending in the masculine nominative singular.

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The claim that :isv[koliko] is uninflected in Interslavic is not discussed in the source material.
:::

The pronoun :isv[koliko] "how many/much?" is uninflected in Interslavic, unlike its Russian cognate *сколько*.

The pronoun :isv[kaky] "what kind of?" and :isv[ktory] "which?" are declined as hard adjectives. For speakers who wish to sound more natural to South Slavic audiences, :isv[koj] may serve in place of both :isv[kaky] and :isv[ktory] in interrogative function.

## 5.5.6. Indefinite Pronouns

### 5.5.6.1. Formation with ně-

The prefix :isv[ně-] attached to an interrogative pronoun or adverb forms an **indefinite** pronoun or adverb, equivalent to English "some-" or "-some":

| Form | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| :isv[někto] | someone, somebody |
| :isv[něčto] | something |
| :isv[někoj] | some (which) |
| :isv[někaky] | some kind of |
| :isv[něčij] | someone's |
| :isv[několiko] | some amount of, a few |
| :isv[někdě] | somewhere |
| :isv[někamo] | to somewhere |
| :isv[někųdy] | by some path |
| :isv[někogda] | sometime, once |
| :isv[někak] | somehow |

The forms :isv[někto], :isv[něčto], etc. are inflected like :isv[kto] and :isv[čto] respectively. The forms :isv[někoj], :isv[něčij], etc. are inflected like :isv[moj].

### 5.5.6.2. Formation with vs- (universal/collective)

The prefix :isv[vs-] forms **universal (collective)** pronouns meaning "every-, all-":

| Form | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| :isv[vsi] or :isv[vsekto] | everyone, everybody |
| :isv[vse] or :isv[vsečto] | everything |
| :isv[ves] (f. :isv[vsa], n. :isv[vse]) | whole, entire; all |
| :isv[vsaky] | each, every |
| :isv[vsekaky] | all kinds of |
| :isv[vsečij] | everybody's |

The pronoun :isv[ves] "whole, all" follows a special declension:

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The source material states that :isv[ves] has a special declension but the actual case forms in the available source text are not fully spelled out. The specific forms in this table cannot be verified cell-by-cell.
:::

| Case | Masc. sg. (anim.) | Masc. sg. (inan.) | Neut. sg. | Fem. sg. | Pl. (all) |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Nom. | :isv[ves] | :isv[ves] | :isv[vse] | :isv[vsa] | :isv[vsi / vse] |
| Acc. | :isv[vsego] | :isv[ves] | :isv[vse] | :isv[vsų] | :isv[vsih / vse] |
| Gen. | :isv[vsego] | :isv[vsego] | :isv[vsego] | :isv[vsej] | :isv[vsih] |
| Dat. | :isv[vsemu] | :isv[vsemu] | :isv[vsemu] | :isv[vsej] | :isv[vsim] |
| Ins. | :isv[vsim] | :isv[vsim] | :isv[vsim] | :isv[vsejų] | :isv[vsimi] |
| Loc. | :isv[(o) vsem] | :isv[(o) vsem] | :isv[(o) vsem] | :isv[(o) vsej] | :isv[(o) vsih] |

The neuter singular :isv[vse] is used in the collective sense "everything, all (of it)", equivalent to Russian *всё*. The plural :isv[vsi] and :isv[vse] are used in the collective sense "all (people/things)", with the same animacy distinction as the third-person plural pronoun: :isv[vsi ljudi] "all people (men)", :isv[vsi mųži] "all men", but :isv[vse ženy] "all women".

## 5.5.7. Negative Pronouns

### 5.5.7.1. Formation with ni-

The prefix :isv[ni-] attached to an interrogative pronoun or adverb forms a **negative** pronoun or adverb:

| Form | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| :isv[nikto] | no one, nobody |
| :isv[ničto] | nothing |
| :isv[nikoj] | not a single (which) |
| :isv[nijedin] | not a single one |
| :isv[nikaky] | no kind of |
| :isv[ničij] | nobody's |
| :isv[nikoliko] | none (in quantity) |
| :isv[nikdě] | nowhere |
| :isv[nikamo] | to nowhere |
| :isv[nikųdy] | by no path |
| :isv[nikogda] | never |
| :isv[nikak] | in no way |

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The forms :isv[nikoliko], :isv[nikamo], and :isv[nikųdy] are not listed in the source material's negative pronoun inventory. They appear to be regular derivations but are unattested in the source.
:::

The forms :isv[nikto], :isv[ničto], etc. are inflected like :isv[kto] and :isv[čto] respectively. The forms :isv[nikoj], :isv[ničij], etc. are inflected like :isv[moj].

### 5.5.7.2. Double negation requirement

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The double negation requirement and the constructions equivalent to Russian "некого/нечего" described below are not discussed in the source material.
:::

Negative pronouns MUST co-occur with the verbal negation particle :isv[ne]. Double negation is **grammatically obligatory** in Interslavic and is not an error:

- :isv[Nikto ne zna.] "Nobody knows."
- :isv[Ničto ne pomaga.] "Nothing helps."
- :isv[Nikdě ne byh.] "I was nowhere."

Omitting :isv[ne] when a negative pronoun is present is a grammatical error in Interslavic. This rule aligns with the universal Slavic pattern of negative concord.

**Note on constructions equivalent to Russian "некого/нечего":**

Interslavic does not have a separate series of pronouns corresponding to Russian *некого, нечего, негде* (meaning "there is no one to…, there is nothing to…, there is nowhere to…"). The equivalent construction in Interslavic uses a verbal copula in the present tense:

- :isv[Ne jest kogo spytati.] "There is no one to ask."
- :isv[Ne jest o čem govoriti.] "There is nothing to talk about."
- :isv[Ne jest kdě sę zajmati.] "There is nowhere to study."

## 5.5.8. Pronominal Adverbs and the Correlative System

Interslavic has a systematic **table of correlatives**: a set of interrelated pronouns, determiners, and adverbs built on a regular grid of pronominal roots and semantic prefixes/suffixes. The system covers the semantic categories of person (:isv[kto] / *who*), thing (:isv[čto] / *what*), quality (:isv[kaky] / *what kind*), quantity (:isv[koliko] / *how many*), possession (:isv[čij] / *whose*), selection (:isv[koj] / *which*), manner (:isv[kak] / *how*), location (:isv[kdě] / *where*), direction-to (:isv[kamo] / *whither*), path (:isv[kųdy] / *which way*), origin (:isv[odkųdy] / *whence*), and time (:isv[kogda] / *when*).

**Semantic prefix keys:**

| Prefix | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| *(none)* | interrogative / relative | :isv[kto] "who?" |
| :isv[tu-] | here/proximal | :isv[tukde] "here" |

| :isv[on-] | yonder | :isv[ondě] "over there" |

| :isv[poně-] | some (plural/vague amount) | :isv[poněkdě] "in some places" |
| :isv[-koli / -либо / -небудь] | any- (free choice) | :isv[ktokoli] "anyone" |
| :isv[ni-] | no- (negative) | :isv[nikto] "no one" |
| :isv[vs-] | every- (universal) | :isv[vsekto] "everyone" |
