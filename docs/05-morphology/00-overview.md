# 5. Morphology: Overview

Interslavic morphology is founded on the common grammatical categories shared by the Slavic language family: grammatical gender, number, case, verbal aspect, tense, mood, and person.
The forms prescribed in this chapter are based on patterns attested in the majority of Slavic languages; where variation exists, the most broadly recognizable solution is selected.
The result is a system that is both internally regular and immediately familiar to speakers of any Slavic language.

This chapter covers all inflectional and derivational morphology.
Sections 5.2 through 5.9 treat inflectional categories for each part of speech in turn.
Section 5.10 covers derivational morphology, i.e., the formation of new lexemes by means of productive affixes.

---

## 5.1. Overview of Morphological Structure

### 5.1.1. Morpheme types

Interslavic words are analyzed in terms of five morpheme categories:

**Root** — the irreducible semantic core of a word, shared by all members of a lexical family.
For example, the root `del-` underlies `dělati` 'to do', `dělanje` 'doing', and `dělnik` 'worker'.

**Prefix** — a bound morpheme attached before the root, typically modifying aspect or spatial meaning in verbs, and semantic scope in nouns and adjectives.
Examples: `na-` in `napisati` 'to write (pf.)', `bez-` in `bezradostny` 'joyless'.

**Suffix** — a bound morpheme attached after the root or stem, used both inflectionally and derivationally.
Derivational suffixes form new lexemes (`-nik` in `dělnik`); inflectional suffixes express grammatical values (`-ov-` in `del-ov-ogo`).

**Interfix (linking element)** — a connecting morpheme used in compound words.
The vowel `-o-` is the default linking element after a hard consonant; `-e-` is used after a soft consonant.
Examples: `rukopis` 'manuscript' (< `ruka` + `-o-` + `pisati`), `zemjepis` 'geography' (< `zemja` + `-e-` + `pisati`).

**Ending (inflectional suffix)** — the final morpheme in an inflected word form, expressing grammatical categories such as case, number, gender, tense, and person.
The ending is the primary domain of inflectional morphology; alternations in the stem (see §3.4) may accompany changes of ending.

Every inflected word form in Interslavic can be segmented as:

```
[ prefix(es) ] + root + [ derivational suffix(es) ] + inflectional ending
```

### 5.1.2. Inflectional vs. derivational morphology

**Inflectional morphology** produces different grammatical forms of the same lexeme without changing its core lexical identity or part of speech.
Nominal inflection varies the form of nouns, adjectives, pronouns, and numerals across case, number, and (for adjectives) gender.
Verbal inflection varies the form of verbs across tense, mood, aspect, person, number, and gender of the L-participle.
Inflectional morphology is treated in Sections 5.2 through 5.9.

**Derivational morphology** forms a new lexeme — typically of a different part of speech or with a distinct lexical meaning — from an existing base.
For example, the noun `dělnik` 'worker' is derived from the verb `dělati` 'to do' by means of the agentive suffix `-nik`.
Derivational morphology is treated in Section 5.10.

The boundary between inflection and derivation is drawn at the lexeme: any process that produces a form that would receive a separate dictionary entry is classified as derivational.
Aspect pairing of verbs (§5.7.1) occupies a special position: perfective/imperfective pairs are treated as distinct but related lexemes, and their relationship is documented under verbal morphology.

### 5.1.3. Productive vs. unproductive processes

Morphological processes in Interslavic are classified along a productivity scale:

**Fully productive** — the process applies freely to all bases of the relevant formal class, including newly coined and borrowed items.
Fully productive affixes SHOULD be used when forming new words.
Examples: the verbal noun suffix `-nje`/`-tje` (§5.10.1.2); the adverb-forming suffix `-o`/`-e` from adjectives (§5.4.1); the comparative prefix `naj-` (§5.3.6.4).

**Semi-productive** — the process applies to a definable subclass of bases but does not extend freely to all formal members of that class.
Semi-productive affixes MAY be used when the base belongs to the specified subclass.
Example: the agentive suffix `-tel` (§5.10.1.1), which is semi-productive with verbal bases denoting controlled activity.

**Lexicalized (frozen)** — the process is no longer synchronically active; the morphological structure is recoverable by analysis but the form is stored as a whole in the lexicon.
Lexicalized patterns are described for transparency only and MUST NOT be treated as productive rules.
Example: certain frozen prefix-root combinations in which the prefix no longer contributes its canonical spatial meaning.

The productivity classification of each affix is stated explicitly in Section 5.10.
Where an affix is marked as lexicalized, its section serves an informative purpose only.

## 5.10. Derivational Morphology: Summary

Section 5.10 specifies productive affixes organized by the part of speech of the derived output:

- **§5.10.1** — Noun-forming suffixes: agent nouns (`-nik`, `-tel`, `-ec`, `-ač`), abstract nouns (`-ost`, `-stvo`, `-nje`/`-tje`, `-ina`), collectives, diminutives, augmentatives, feminine person nouns, and inhabitant/nationality nouns.
- **§5.10.2** — Adjective-forming suffixes: relational (`-ny`, `-sky`), possessive (`-ov`, `-in`), and material (`-any`, `-eny`).
- **§5.10.3** — Verb-forming processes: denominative verbs (`-iti`, `-ovati`), deadjectival verbs, and prefix-based aspect formation.
- **§5.10.4** — Prefixes: the full inventory of native Slavic prefixes with their productive meanings and aspect effects.
