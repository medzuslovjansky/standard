# 5.3. Adjectives

This section specifies the inflection, short forms, possessive formation, and degrees of comparison of adjectives in Interslavic. All normative rules use the terms MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, and MAY as defined in RFC 2119.

---

## 5.3.1. Adjectival Agreement

Adjectives in Interslavic MUST agree with the head noun they modify in **gender**, **number**, and **case**. This requirement applies both in attributive position (adjective preceding or following the noun) and in predicative position (adjective linked to the noun by a copula).

Adjectival declension is entirely regular. Three systematic rules govern all paradigms:

1. **Hard vs. soft stem alternations.** Adjective stems ending in *š*, *ž*, *č*, or *j* are soft stems. After soft stems, every `-o-` becomes `-e-` and every `-y-` becomes `-i-` in the endings (see §5.3.2.2).

2. **Animate masculine accusative.** The accusative of an adjective modifying an animate masculine noun MUST be identical to the genitive, both in the singular and in the plural. For inanimate masculines and for all other genders the accusative equals the nominative (singular and plural).

3. **Nominative–accusative syncretism in the plural.** Except when modifying animate masculine nouns, the nominative plural and the accusative plural are **identical** across all genders.

## 5.3.3. Short (Predicative) Form

### 5.3.3.1. Distribution of Short Forms

Interslavic possesses a vestigial **short form** of adjectives, a relic of the indefinite (non-pronominal) declension inherited from Common Slavic. The short form differs from the regular (long) form **only in the masculine nominative singular**, where the ending `-y` / `-i` is omitted, leaving a bare stem.

Short forms do not constitute a separate declension class; all oblique forms of short-form adjectives are identical to those of the corresponding long-form adjective. The short form MUST NOT be confused with adverbs, which are also derived from the bare stem (see §5.4.1).

The short form occurs productively in two classes of adjective:

1. **Possessive adjectives in `-ov` and `-in`** (§5.3.4): the masculine nominative singular is zero-ended — :isv[bratov], :isv[mamin] — while all other forms add regular long endings.
2. A small number of other adjectives where tradition preserves the short masculine nominative, e.g. :isv[velik] alongside :isv[veliky].

### 5.3.3.2. Short Form in Predicative Position

The short form MAY be used when the adjective functions as a **predicate nominal** (i.e., after a copula such as :isv[jest] "is"):

- :isv[Petr jest ščestliv.] — "Peter is happy."
- :isv[Dom jest velik.] — "The house is big."

Use of the long form in predicative position is equally acceptable:

- :isv[Petr jest ščestlivy.] — "Peter is happy."
- :isv[Dom jest veliky.] — "The house is big."

Neither form is obligatory in predicative position; the long form is more common in contemporary usage.

### 5.3.4.2. Scope Restriction: Single-Word Possessor Only

Possessive adjectives in `-ov` / `-ev` and `-in` MUST be used only when the possessor is expressed as a **single uninflected word** (a simple noun without modifiers or additional words):

- :isv[bratova kniga] — "brother's book" (single-word possessor → possessive adjective)
- :isv[kniga mojego brata] — "my brother's book" (modified possessor → genitive phrase)
- :isv[Puškinova poezija] — "Pushkin's poetry" (single proper name → possessive adjective)
- :isv[poezija Aleksandra Puškina] — "Aleksandr Pushkin's poetry" (full name → genitive phrase)

When the possessor is a phrase, a multi-word name, or requires its own inflection, the **genitive** form of the possessor noun MUST be used instead.

## 5.3.5. Demonstratives as Quasi-Articles

### 5.3.5.1. :isv[Toj] / :isv[ta] / :isv[to] (Proximal and Distal Demonstratives)

Interslavic has **no grammaticalized definite or indefinite article**. The demonstrative pronoun :isv[toj] (masc.), :isv[ta] (fem.), :isv[to] (neut.) — "that; this" — serves as the closest functional equivalent to a definiteness marker.

The full declension of :isv[toj] / :isv[ta] / :isv[to] follows the standard adjectival paradigm with the following forms:

| Case | Masc. sg. (anim.) | Masc. sg. (inan.) | Neut. sg. | Fem. sg. | Pl. (all genders) |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Nominative | :isv[toj] | :isv[toj] | :isv[to] | :isv[ta] | :isv[ti] |
| Accusative | :isv[togo] | :isv[toj] | :isv[to] | :isv[tų] | :isv[tyh] (anim.); :isv[te] (inan.) |
| Genitive | :isv[togo] | :isv[togo] | :isv[togo] | :isv[toj] | :isv[tyh] |
| Dative | :isv[tomu] | :isv[tomu] | :isv[tomu] | :isv[toj] | :isv[tym] |
| Instrumental | :isv[tym] | :isv[tym] | :isv[tym] | :isv[tojų] | :isv[tymi] |
| Locative | :isv[tom] | :isv[tom] | :isv[tom] | :isv[toj] | :isv[tyh] |

### 5.3.5.2. Anaphoric and Deictic Uses

Demonstratives in Interslavic serve two functions:

1. **Deictic use**: pointing to a referent in the physical or discourse context — :isv[toj čelověk] "that man (over there)".
2. **Anaphoric use**: referring back to a previously mentioned or contextually established referent — :isv[Kupil jesm knigu. Ta kniga je interesna.] "I bought a book. That/The book is interesting."

Because Interslavic lacks a definite article, the demonstrative in its anaphoric use is the standard means of marking definiteness when contextual clarity requires it. Its use in this function is OPTIONAL; definiteness need not be marked when the context is unambiguous.

## 5.3.6. Degrees of Comparison

Adjectives (and the adverbs derived from them; see §5.4.2) have three degrees of comparison: **positive**, **comparative**, and **superlative**.

### 5.3.6.1. Positive (Base) Form

The **positive degree** is the uninflected base form of the adjective, expressing a quality without comparison: :isv[dobry] "good", :isv[prosty] "simple", :isv[svěži] "fresh".

### 5.3.6.2. Synthetic Comparative (`-ějši` / `-ejši`)

The primary synthetic comparative is formed by replacing the ending `-y` / `-i` with the suffix **`-ějši`** (after hard stems) or **`-ejši`** (after soft stems, i.e. after *š*, *ž*, *č*, *j*):

| Positive | Comparative | Gloss |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| :isv[bogaty] | :isv[bogatějši] | "richer" |
| :isv[slaby] | :isv[slabějši] | "weaker" |
| :isv[zeleny] | :isv[zelenějši] | "greener" |
| :isv[svěži] | :isv[svěžejši] | "fresher" |

The consonant alternations **k → č**, **g → ž**, **h → š** apply before the comparative suffix:

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The source uses `blagy → blažejši` to illustrate the g→ž alternation. The examples `tihy → tišejši`, `tonky → tončejši`, and `drågy → dråžejši` are not in the source. Additionally, `tonky → tončejši` is questionable since `-ky` adjectives normally use the shorter `-ši` suffix (as stated in the next subsection).
:::

| Positive | Comparative | Gloss |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| :isv[tihy] | :isv[tišejši] | "quieter" |
| :isv[tonky] | :isv[tončejši] | "thinner" |
| :isv[drågy] | :isv[dråžejši] | "dearer" |

**Adjectives in `-ky`, `-eky`, `-oky`.** Adjectives with these suffixes form the comparative with the shorter suffix **`-ši`** attached directly to the root (after the same consonant alternations):

| Positive | Comparative | Gloss |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| :isv[kratky] | :isv[kratši] | "shorter" |
| :isv[tenky] | :isv[tenši] | "thinner" |
| :isv[daleky] | :isv[dalši] | "farther" |
| :isv[vysoky] | :isv[vysši] | "higher" |

The comparative form is a fully inflected adjective and MUST agree with its head noun in gender, number, and case, following the soft adjective paradigm (§5.3.2.2).

**Comparative adverbs** are formed from the synthetic comparative by replacing `-ši` with `-e` (yielding the ending `-ěje` / `-eje`):

| Positive adverb | Comparative adverb | Gloss |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------|
| :isv[novo] | :isv[nověje] | "more newly" |
| :isv[čisto] | :isv[čistěje] | "more cleanly" |
| :isv[tiho] | :isv[tišeje] | "more quietly" |
| :isv[svěže] | :isv[svěžeje] | "more freshly" |

For adjectives in `-ky` / `-eky` / `-oky`, the comparative adverb is formed by adding `-je` to the root, triggering iotation of the preceding consonant:

| Positive adverb | Comparative adverb | Gloss |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------|
| :isv[daleko] | :isv[dalje] | "farther" |
| :isv[široko] | :isv[širje] | "more broadly" |
| :isv[vysoky] → :isv[vysoko] | :isv[vyše] | "higher" |
| :isv[blizko] | :isv[bliže] | "closer" |
| :isv[rědko] | :isv[rědže] | "more rarely" |

### 5.3.6.3. Analytic Comparative (:isv[vyše] / :isv[bolje] + positive)

The analytic comparative is formed by placing **:isv[vyše]** or **:isv[bolje]** ("more") before the positive form of the adjective or adverb:

- :isv[vyše prosty] — "simpler, more simple"
- :isv[bolje sintetičny] — "more synthetic"
- :isv[vyše smrdeči] — "more stinking"

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The source says the analytic form "is most likely encountered in the case of very long words, foreign words, and participles." The doc upgrades this observation to a normative SHOULD recommendation and adds the criterion "difficult to pronounce or understand," which is not in the source.
:::

The analytic form MAY be used for any adjective or adverb. It SHOULD be preferred for:
- Long words and words of foreign origin;
- Participles used as adjectives;
- Contexts in which the synthetic form would be difficult to pronounce or understand.

The analytic and synthetic forms are equivalent in meaning. In most everyday adjectives, either form is acceptable.

**Less-than comparative.** The word **:isv[menje]** ("less") functions as the analytic marker for the opposite direction:

- :isv[menje prosty] — "less simple"
- :isv[menje prosto] — "less simply"

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The rules distinguishing comparative adjective vs. adverb usage, the comparison examples with `neželi`, and the constructions with `od` and `než`/`neželi` are not discussed in the source material.
:::

**Adjective vs. adverb in comparisons.** When comparing a property of a noun, the **comparative adjective** (agreeing with the noun) MUST be used. When comparing a property of an action or verb, the **comparative adverb** is used:

- :isv[Aleksandr je bystrějši, neželi Dmitri.] — "Alexander is faster than Dmitri." (adjective)
- :isv[Aleksandr bystrěje běgaje, neželi Dmitri.] — "Alexander runs faster than Dmitri." (adverb)

When introducing the standard of comparison, use the genitive with the preposition :isv[od] "from" or the conjunction :isv[než] / :isv[neželi] "than":

- :isv[Ljubov je silnějša od smŕti.] — "Love is stronger than death."
- :isv[Goręći čaj je lěpši, než hlådny.] — "Hot tea is better than cold."

### 5.3.6.4. Superlative (`naj-` Prefix)

The **superlative** is formed by adding the prefix **`naj-`** to the comparative form:

| Comparative | Superlative (adj.) | Superlative (adv.) |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| :isv[novějši] | :isv[najnovějši] | :isv[najnověje] |
| :isv[prostějši] | :isv[najprostějši] | :isv[najprostěje] |
| :isv[vysši] | :isv[najvysši] | :isv[najvyše] |

The prefix `naj-` MAY also be applied to the analytic comparative:

- :isv[najvyše sintetičny] — "most synthetic"
- :isv[najbolje prosty] — "simplest, most simple"

As a simplification, `naj-` MAY be prefixed directly to the **positive** form of the adjective or adverb (following the pattern of Bulgarian and Macedonian):

- :isv[najnovy] — "newest" (adjective)
- :isv[najnovo] — "most newly" (adverb)

**Least-superlative.** The adverb **:isv[najmenje]** expresses the opposite extreme ("least"):

- :isv[najmenje prosty] — "least simple"
- :isv[najmenje prosto] — "least simply"

**Excessive degree.** The prefix **`prě-`** attached to the positive expresses an extreme or excessive degree ("very", "too", "overly"):

- :isv[prěprosty] — "too simple, overly simple"
- :isv[prěneprijatny] — "extremely unpleasant"

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The example `prěneprijatny` is not in the source. The source also spells the negative example as `neprijetny` (with `-e-`), not `neprijatny` (with `-a-`) as used below.
:::

**Negative.** The prefix **`ne-`** creates a negative adjective:

- :isv[neprijatny] — "unpleasant, not pleasant"
