### 2.2.1.1 Monophthongs

Interslavic utilizes a set of monophthongs common to Slavic languages.

- **2.2.1.1.1 Front Vowels**

  - **[i]** as in *mi* (we)
  - **[e]** as in *me* (me)

- **2.2.1.1.2 Central Vowel**

  - **[a]** as in *ma* (mother)

- **2.2.1.1.3 Back Vowels**

  - **[o]** as in *dom* (house)
  - **[u]** as in *ruka* (hand)

- **2.2.1.1.4 High Central Vowel**

  - **[y]** as in *syn* (son)

### 2.2.1.2 Nasal Vowels

Nasal vowels are retained to reflect historical and etymological accuracy.

- **2.2.1.2.1 [ę] Representing Nasalized [e]**

  The letter **Ę ę** represents a nasal front vowel, crucial for preserving Proto-Slavic nasal sounds.

  > **Example**  
  > *mięso* (meat) pronounced as [mʲɛ̃so]

- **2.2.1.2.2 [ų] Representing Nasalized [u]**

  The letter **Ų ų** denotes a nasal back vowel.

  > **Example**  
  > *rųka* (hand) pronounced as [rũka]

#### 2.2.1.2.1. Nasal Assimilation

Nasal vowels can influence adjacent consonants, leading to assimilation where the nasal quality extends to neighboring sounds.

> **Implementation Note**  
> Language processing algorithms should account for nasal assimilation when performing phonetic transcription or speech synthesis.

#### 2.2.1.2.2. Denasalization Trends

In some dialects or rapid speech, nasal vowels may lose their nasal quality.

> **Warning** ⚠️  
> Denasalization can alter word meanings and should be carefully managed in language education and software applications.

### 2.2.1.3 Vowel Length

Vowel length is not phonemic in Interslavic; however, natural elongation occurs due to stress and intonation patterns.
