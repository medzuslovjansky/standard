# 7. Semantics and Pragmatics — Overview

This section specifies the semantic and pragmatic rules of Interslavic: how grammatical and lexical choices map onto meaning, how context shapes interpretation, and how speakers deploy language to perform social actions. The rules defined here apply to all standard written and spoken Interslavic regardless of flavorizational register.

## 7.0.2. Core Semantic Properties

Interslavic exhibits the following semantic properties that shape interpretation across all domains of the grammar:

1. **Grammatical aspect as a primary semantic category.** Every finite verb form in Interslavic carries either perfective (pf.) or imperfective (impf.) aspect. Aspect is not merely stylistic — it determines whether an event is construed as a bounded, completed whole or as an ongoing/habitual/repeated process. This interacts systematically with tense to produce the full range of temporal meanings (see §7.1.1).

2. **No grammatical articles; definiteness is pragmatic.** Because Interslavic has no articles, the specific vs. generic and definite vs. indefinite distinction is conveyed through context, demonstratives (*toj / та / то*), and information structure. Context and word order are therefore semantically significant (see §7.3.3).

3. **Modality expressed lexically and through the conditional.** Interslavic does not have a dedicated morphological mood for epistemic or deontic meanings beyond the indicative, conditional, and imperative. Modal meanings are instead expressed through modal predicates (*možno, trěba, dolžen, moći, uměti*) and through the conditional mood (see §7.1.4).

4. **Evidentiality is not grammaticalized.** Interslavic does not encode the source of information in verb morphology. Evidential meanings ("reportedly", "apparently", "I heard that") are expressed by lexical means only (see §7.1.5).

## 7.0.3. Normative Status

All rules in Chapter 7 that use the modal verbs **MUST**, **SHALL**, **SHOULD**, **MAY**, and **SHOULD NOT** carry the following normative force:

- **MUST / SHALL** — an absolute semantic or pragmatic requirement.
- **SHOULD** — the strongly recommended default; deviation is permitted but may reduce communicative transparency.
- **MAY** — an explicitly permitted option.
- **SHOULD NOT** — a usage that is technically grammatical but actively discouraged as opaque or misleading to a cross-Slavic audience.
