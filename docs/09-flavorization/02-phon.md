# Phonological Flavorization
Phonological flavorization makes use of the **etymological alphabet**  — the set of optional letters whose standard pronunciation is fixed but whose realization varies historically across the Slavic dialect continuum.
By writing and/or pronouncing these letters according to the reflexes of a particular dialect area, a text can be made more immediately familiar to speakers of that area without altering its standard Interslavic meaning.

All vowel and consonant substitutions specified in this chapter are **optional**.
Their application is governed by the target audience, not by any intrinsic requirement of the language.
Standard Interslavic remains correct regardless of whether flavorization is applied.

---

## Vowel Substitutions
### `y` → `i` (Southern/Ukrainian Flavor)
The phoneme /y/ (:isv[y] in Latin) is distinct from /i/ in Russian, Belarusian, Polish, and Sorbian.
In South Slavic languages, in Ukrainian, and in colloquial Czech and Slovak, /y/ and /i/ have merged into a single vowel.

**Rule:** When addressing an audience of South Slavic or Ukrainian speakers, all occurrences of :isv[y] SHOULD be replaced by :isv[i].

| Standard | Flavorized | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| :isv[byti] | :isv[biti] | to be |
| :isv[ryba] | :isv[riba] | fish |
| :isv[syn] | :isv[sin] | son |
| :isv[dobry] | :isv[dobri] | good |

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The claim that this substitution has "no effect on intelligibility for North Slavic speakers" is not stated in the source material. The source recommends the substitution for South Slavic audiences but does not assess its impact on North Slavic intelligibility.
:::

This substitution has no effect on intelligibility for North Slavic speakers, since both :isv[y] and :isv[i] are already distinct in their languages and the standard form remains recoverable.

### `ě` Realizations by Target Language
The etymological vowel :isv[ě] has excellent compromise qualities precisely because it has developed into different vowels in different Slavic languages.
No single realization is optimal for all audiences simultaneously.

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The specific standard pronunciation values (:ipa[jɛ] for East Slavic, :ipa[e] for South Slavic) are not stated in the source material, which only describes the compromise qualities of `ě` without prescribing a standard pronunciation.
:::

**Standard pronunciation:** In standard Interslavic, :isv[ě] is pronounced :ipa[jɛ] by East Slavic speakers and approximately :ipa[e] by South Slavic speakers; both are acceptable.

The following table documents the historically attested realization of Proto-Slavic :isv[ě] in each major language group and the recommended flavorized form for each target audience:

| Target audience | Realization of `ě` | Flavorized form | Example (:isv[rěka]) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard (default) | :ipa[jɛ] ~ :ipa[e] | :isv[ě] | :isv[rěka] |
| Russian / Polish | merged with `e` or `je` | :isv[e] or :isv[je] | :isv[reka] |
| Ukrainian | :ipa[i] after hard consonant | :isv[i] | :isv[rika] |
| Serbian / Macedonian | :ipa[e] | :isv[e] | :isv[reka] |
| Croatian / Bosnian | :ipa[ije] (long) / :ipa[je] (short) | :isv[ije] / :isv[je] | :isv[rijeka] |
| Slovene | :ipa[e] | :isv[e] | :isv[reka] |
| Czech | :ipa[e] | :isv[e] | :isv[reka] |
| Slovak | :ipa[ie] | :isv[ie] | :isv[rieka] |

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The Slovak realization of `ě` as `ie` in the table above is not explicitly attributed to Slovak in the source material. The source lists the possible reflexes of `ě` as `i`, `ije`, `je`, `ja`, or `a` without specifying which applies to Slovak.
:::

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The claim that `ě` always follows a hard consonant and the distinction between hard `e` and etymological `ě` are not discussed in the source material. The source only notes that "`e` always remains hard `e`" but does not elaborate on the phonotactic constraints of `ě`.
:::

**Note:** The character :isv[ě] always follows a hard consonant in standard Interslavic .
The hard consonant :isv[e] and the etymological :isv[ě] are distinct: :isv[e] always remains a plain :ipa[e], while :isv[ě] carries the variable realizations listed above.
This contrast is why :isv[ě] has compromise value: a Serb reads :isv[rěč] as their own _reč_, a Croat as their own _riječ_, a Ukrainian as their own _rič_, all without any ambiguity.

### `ę` → `ja` / `a` (Northern Flavor)
In standard Interslavic, the etymological vowel :isv[ę] is written and pronounced as :isv[e] as a default simplification, which is a feature characteristic of South Slavic.

**Rule for Northern flavor:** The etymological :isv[ę] SHOULD be realized as :isv[ja] after a hard consonant, or as :isv[a] after a soft consonant.
This approximates the reflexes of North Slavic languages (cf. Russian _я_ [ja], Polish _ię/ą_).

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The etymological form `:isv[rěčeęt]` shown in parentheses is not attested in the source material. The source provides no specific verb conjugation examples for `ę` substitution.
:::

| Standard | Northern | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| :isv[rěčet] (:isv[rěčeęt]) | :isv[rěčjat] | they speak |
| :isv[pęť] | :isv[pjať] | five |
| :isv[meso] (from :isv[męso]) | :isv[mjaso] | meat |
| :isv[pamęť] | :isv[pamjať] | memory |

This substitution is one of the clearest markers of Northern flavor.

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The MUST NOT constraint about not applying this substitution when the letter is `e` rather than `ę` is not explicitly stated in the source material. The source simply describes the substitution without this warning.
:::

It MUST NOT be applied when the etymological letter is actually :isv[e] rather than :isv[ę]: the distinction requires access to the etymological form.

### `ų` Substitutions by Language Group
The etymological vowel :isv[ų] corresponds to :isv[u] in most Slavic languages and MAY simply be pronounced as standard :ipa[u] in all registers.
Flavorized forms reflect the distinct development in specific languages:

| Language / group | Reflex of `ų` | Flavorized form |
|---|---|---|
| Standard Interslavic | :isv[u] | :isv[u] |
| Polish | `ą` (nasal) | `ą` |
| Slovene | `o` | :isv[o] |
| Macedonian | `a` | :isv[a] |
| Bulgarian | `ъ` (yer) | — |
| All others | :isv[u] | :isv[u] |

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The Polish-flavored example `ręka` is inconsistent with the table above, which states that the Polish reflex of `ų` is `ą` (nasal). By the table's own rule, the Polish-flavored form should use `ą`, not `ę`. The actual Polish word is indeed `ręka`, but this reflects a different vowel mapping than what the table documents.
:::

**Example:** Standard :isv[rųka] (hand) → Polish-flavored `ręka`, Slovene-flavored :isv[roka], Macedonian-flavored :isv[raka].

For the Polish-based orthographic flavorization that combines :isv[ų] with the ogonek diacritic, see the relevant section

### `å` → `o` (Northern Flavor)
The etymological letter :isv[å] does not occur as a separate phoneme in any modern Slavic language.
Standard Interslavic uses :isv[a] in this position, as do Old Church Slavonic, South Slavic, Czech, Slovak, and many Russian words.

**Rule for Northern flavor:** :isv[å] SHOULD be replaced by :isv[o], reflecting the North Slavic (especially Russian and Polish) reflex of this etymological vowel.

| Standard (å → a) | Northern (å → o) | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| :isv[trava] | :isv[trova] | grass |
| :isv[razuměti] | :isv[rozuměti] | to understand |
| :isv[krava] | :isv[krova] | cow |
| :isv[glava] | :isv[glova] | head |

**Note:** :isv[å] occurs only between a liquid consonant (:isv[r] or :isv[l]) and a following consonant .
The substitution :isv[å] → :isv[o] is therefore restricted to this specific phonotactic environment.

Czech and Slovak typically show `ra`/`rá` and `la`/`lá` reflexes in this position (e.g., Czech `kráva`, `hlava`, `mráz`), closely matching the standard Interslavic forms.
In Russian, many words have both a native East Slavic form with _polnoglasije_ (`голова`, `борода`, `молоко`) and a South Slavic borrowing via Old Church Slavonic (`глава`, `блато`, `враг`); in some cases one form has displaced the other, while in others both coexist with differentiated meanings (e.g., `голова` "head (anatomical)" vs. `глава` "chapter; leader").

### `ȯ` Substitutions (Hard Jer)
The etymological letter :isv[ȯ] represents the historical strong hard jer.
Its reflex varies considerably across the Slavic languages:

| Language / group | Reflex of `ȯ` | Flavorized form |
|---|---|---|
| Standard Interslavic (default) | :isv[o] | :isv[o] |
| East Slavic, Slovak, Upper Sorbian, Macedonian | `o` | :isv[o] |
| Slovene, remaining West Slavic | `e` | :isv[e] |
| Serbo-Croatian | `a` | :isv[a] |
| Bulgarian | `ъ` | — |

:::caution[Unverified Content]
Slovene is a South Slavic language, not West Slavic as implied by "West Slavic (Slovene, Czech) character." The source correctly groups Slovene with "the remaining West Slavic languages" for the `e` reflex, but labeling the combined flavor as "West Slavic" while including Slovene is misleading.
:::

**Rule for West Slavic flavor:** :isv[ȯ] SHOULD be replaced by :isv[e], giving the text a West Slavic (Slovene, Czech) character.
**Rule for South Slavic / Croatian flavor:** :isv[ȯ] SHOULD be replaced by :isv[a].

Standard Interslavic uses :isv[o] as the default realization of :isv[ȯ], which best serves East Slavic and most Slavic audiences.

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The source material includes a section on the etymological letter `ė` (strong soft jer / `ь`), noting that it has developed into `e` in all Slavic languages except Serbo-Croatian (which has `a`). This doc file omits `ė` entirely, which is a gap relative to the source.
:::

### Syllabic `r` → `or` / `er` (Northern/Eastern Flavor)
In Czech, Slovak, Slovene, Serbo-Croatian, and Macedonian, the syllabic consonant /r/ is retained as a syllabic nucleus .
In Russian, Polish, Ukrainian, and Belarusian, syllabic /r/ was historically resolved by inserting a vowel.

**Rule for Northern/Eastern flavor:**
- Syllabic hard :isv[r] SHOULD be written and pronounced as :isv[or].
- Syllabic soft :isv[ŕ] SHOULD be written and pronounced as :isv[er].

| Standard | Northern | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| :isv[trg] | :isv[torg] | market |
| :isv[cukr] | :isv[cukór] | sugar |
| :isv[pŕvy] | :isv[pervy] | first |
| :isv[čŕv] | :isv[červ] | worm |

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The accented form `cukór` in the table above (with a vowel-length mark) is not attested in the source material. The source describes the substitution as simply inserting `or` for syllabic `r`, which would yield `cukor` without the accent.
:::

This substitution is one of the most salient phonological markers distinguishing Northern from Southern Interslavic.

## Consonant Substitutions
### `g` → `h` / `ɦ` (Ukrainian, Belarusian, Czech, Slovak Flavor)
In Ukrainian, Belarusian, Czech, Slovak, and Upper Sorbian, the velar stop /g/ has shifted to a voiced velar or glottal fricative :ipa[ɣ] or :ipa[ɦ].
Standard Interslavic uses /g/ as the conservative form.

**Rule:** In texts oriented toward Ukrainian, Belarusian, Czech, or Slovak speakers, :isv[g] MAY be pronounced as an aspirated or fricative :isv[h] (:ipa[ɣ] or :ipa[ɦ]).
:::caution[Unverified Content]
The claim that this is a "pronunciation-level adaptation only" with the written form remaining `g` is not stated in the source material. The source simply notes the pronunciation difference without specifying whether the adaptation is limited to pronunciation or also applies to writing.
:::

This is a **pronunciation-level** adaptation only; the written form remains :isv[g] in standard and Northern Latin, unless a specific orthographic flavorization  is also applied.

### Optional Palatalized Consonants for Northern Flavor
Standard Interslavic has two obligatory soft consonants: :isv[lj] /lʲ/ and :isv[nj] /nʲ/.
The etymological alphabet  provides five additional optional soft consonants: :isv[ĺ] (:isv[ľ]), :isv[ń], :isv[ŕ], :isv[t́] (:isv[ť]), :isv[d́] (:isv[ď]), :isv[ś], :isv[ź].

**Rule for Northern flavor:** All seven etymological soft consonants SHOULD be retained and written explicitly.
This gives the text a distinctly North Slavic (Polish, Russian) character and maximizes intelligibility for readers accustomed to richly marked palatalization.

The five additional etymological consonants (:isv[ť], :isv[ď], :isv[ś], :isv[ź], :isv[ŕ]) are **largely absent** from South Slavic; :isv[ś] and :isv[ź] are also absent from standard Czech and Slovak; :isv[ŕ] is absent from Slovak and Belarusian.
Their use is therefore a Northern-oriented choice.

**Orthographic note:** When marking all soft consonants consistently:
- In Latin: use the acute accent or háček consistently: :isv[ť], :isv[ď], :isv[ś], :isv[ź], :isv[ŕ], :isv[ĺ] (or :isv[ľ]), :isv[ń].

An alternative Latin convention, used in Polish, Sorbian, and certain forms of Belarusian Łacinka and Ukrainian Latynka, is to use `l` for the soft [ʎ] and `ł` for the hard [l].
This convention MAY be used in place of :isv[ĺ] / :isv[ľ].

### `ć` `đ` Retention (Southern Flavor)
The etymological affricates :isv[ć] /t͡ɕ/ and :isv[đ] /d͡ʑ/ are, in standard Interslavic, written and pronounced as :isv[č] and :isv[dž] respectively .
Unlike other iotated consonants (\*lj, \*nj, \*rj), the reflexes of Proto-Slavic \*tj and \*dj have diverged dramatically across Slavic languages, making these among the most variable correspondences in the family.
In South Slavic languages — particularly Serbian and Croatian — `ć` and `đ` are distinct phonemes.

The following table shows the reflexes of Proto-Slavic \*tj (= ISV :isv[ć]) and \*dj (= ISV :isv[đ]) in the major Slavic languages:

| Language | Reflex of \*tj | Reflex of \*dj | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Russian | щ :ipa[[ɕː]] or ч :ipa[[t͡ɕ]] | жд :ipa[[ʐd]] or ж :ipa[[ʐ]] | :isv[noč] → _ночь_, :isv[međa] → _межа_ |
| Ukrainian | ч :ipa[[t͡ʂ]] | дж :ipa[[d͡ʐ]] / ж :ipa[[ʐ]] | :isv[noč] → _ніч_, :isv[međa] → _межа_ |
| Belarusian | ч :ipa[[t͡ʂ]] | дж :ipa[[d͡ʐ]] / ж :ipa[[ʐ]] | :isv[noč] → _ноч_, :isv[međa] → _мяжа_ |
| Polish | c :ipa[[t͡s]] | dz :ipa[[d͡z]] | :isv[noč] → _noc_, :isv[međa] → _miedza_ |
| Czech | c :ipa[[t͡s]] | z :ipa[[z]] | :isv[noč] → _noc_, :isv[međa] → _mez_ |
| Slovak | c :ipa[[t͡s]] or ť :ipa[[c]] | dz :ipa[[d͡z]] or ď :ipa[[ɟ]] | :isv[noč] → _noc_, :isv[međa] → _medza_ |
| Serbo-Croatian (Shtokavian) | ć :ipa[[t͡ɕ]] | đ :ipa[[d͡ʑ]] | :isv[noč] → _noć_, :isv[međa] → _međa_ |
| Slovene | č :ipa[[t͡ʃ]] or t :ipa[[t]] | j :ipa[[j]] or d :ipa[[d]] | :isv[noč] → _noč_, :isv[međa] → _meja_ |
| Macedonian | ќ :ipa[[c]], ш :ipa[[ʃ]], or т :ipa[[t]] | ѓ :ipa[[ɟ]] or д :ipa[[d]] | :isv[noč] → _ноќ_, :isv[međa] → _меѓа_ |
| Bulgarian | щ :ipa[[ʃt]], or т :ipa[[t]] | жд :ipa[[ʒd]] or д :ipa[[d]] | :isv[noč] → _нощ_, :isv[međa] → _межда_ |

The Russian double reflex (щ/ч for \*tj and жд/ж for \*dj) reflects the mixed East and South Slavic heritage of the Russian literary language, with the South Slavic reflexes (щ, жд) entering through Old Church Slavonic influence.

**Rule for Southern flavor:** The etymological :isv[ć] and :isv[đ] MAY be retained in both writing and pronunciation.

**Critical constraint:** :isv[ć] and :isv[đ] MUST NOT be used for the sequences :isv[tj] and :isv[dj] (soft :isv[t] and soft :isv[d]).
These are distinct etymological entities.
Confusing them constitutes an error, not a flavor variant.

| Standard | Southern | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| :isv[noč] | :isv[noć] | night |
| :isv[svěča] | :isv[svjeća] | candle |
| :isv[međa] | :isv[međa] | border |

### Epenthetic L (Southern vs. Northern/Western Flavor)
In a number of Slavic words, an epenthetic (inserted) :isv[l] appears between a labial or dental consonant and the glide :isv[j]. This epenthetic :isv[l] is present in East Slavic (Russian, Ukrainian, Belarusian), Shtokavian (Serbian/Croatian/Bosnian), and Slovenian, but absent in West Slavic (Polish, Czech, Slovak) and in Bulgarian and Macedonian.

The phenomenon is most visible in words like :isv[zemlja] "earth/land" (with epenthetic L) vs. the West Slavic forms _ziemia_ (Polish), _země_ (Czech), _zem_ (Slovak), and the Bulgarian/Macedonian _земя_/_земја_. Other common examples include :isv[zdravlje] "health" and verb forms like :isv[dobavljati] "to supply."

Standard Interslavic includes the epenthetic :isv[l] in these words (following the majority pattern), which means that West Slavic and Bulgarian/Macedonian speakers encounter a consonant cluster absent from their own languages. This is one of several points where the "middle ground" of Interslavic necessarily leans toward one group of languages at the expense of another.

:::warning[Unresolved: Epenthetic L Encoding]
The epenthetic :isv[l] in words like :isv[zemlja], :isv[zdravlje], :isv[dobavljati] is present in East Slavic, Shtokavian, and Slovenian, but absent in West Slavic, Bulgarian, and Macedonian. Standard Interslavic includes it (following the numerical majority), but this choice is not encoded in the etymological alphabet — there is no diacritic or special notation to flag the :isv[l] as epenthetic rather than original. This means automated flavorization cannot determine whether a given :isv[lj] sequence should be preserved (for East Slavic / South-West Slavic audiences) or stripped (for West Slavic / Bulgarian / Macedonian audiences) without external etymological data. Encoding this distinction — for example, with a marked variant of :isv[l] in the scientific register — would improve flavorization accuracy but would add complexity to the already large etymological inventory.
:::

### `šč` → `št` (Southern Flavor)
The consonant cluster :isv[šč] occurs in standard Interslavic.
In South Slavic, this cluster has merged to :isv[št].

**Rule for Southern flavor:** The sequence :isv[šč] SHOULD be replaced by :isv[št].

| Standard | Southern | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| :isv[ješče] | :isv[ješte] | still, yet |
| :isv[čiščenje] | :isv[čištenje] | cleaning |

## Worked Phonological Flavorization Examples
The following parallel texts illustrate phonological flavorization across four registers: the etymological form, standard Interslavic, Northern flavorization (East Slavic orthography), and Southern flavorization (Croatian-based orthography).

**Source sentence (etymological register):**

> :isv[Međuslovjańsky jest orųďje dlja komunikacije s Slovjanami.]

---

**Standard:**

> :isv[Medžuslovjansky jest orudje dlja komunikacije s Slovjanami.]

---

**Northern flavorization — East Slavic Cyrillic orthography:**

> _Меджусловяньски ест орудье для комуникацие с Словянами._

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The bullet-point annotations below are editorial additions not present in the source material. The source provides the example text without itemized change descriptions. Additionally, the first bullet describes `y→i` in the Northern variant, but Northern flavor typically retains the `y`/`i` distinction -- the `y→i` substitution is documented in the doc as a Southern/Ukrainian flavor (), creating an internal inconsistency.
:::

Changes applied relative to standard:
- :isv[ы] → :isv[и] in :isv[јанскы] → :isv[яньски] (Northern: soft consonants marked, :isv[y] kept as :isv[ы] for East Slavic readers but stem adapted)
- :isv[ję] in verbal forms → :isv[ja] (Northern flavor)
- :isv[lj] / :isv[nj] sequences written with explicit soft sign: `нь`, `ть`
- Syllabic :isv[r] resolved: e.g., :isv[čŕt] → :isv[čert]

---

**Northern flavorization — Polish-based Latin orthography:**

> _Miedżusłowiański jest orudie dla komunikacije s Słowianami._

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The bullet-point annotations below are editorial additions not present in the source material. The source provides the example text without itemized change descriptions. These orthographic mappings (`v→w`, `č→cz`, etc.) belong to the orthographic flavorization section (9.5), not phonological flavorization.
:::

Changes applied relative to standard:
- `v` → `w`
- `č` → `cz`, `š` → `sz`, `ž` → `ż`
- `ě` → `ie`
- Etymological `ę` represented as ogonek forms where applicable

---

**Southern flavorization — Croatian-based Latin orthography:**

> :isv[Međuslovjanski jest orudje dlja komunikacije s Slovjanami.]

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The bullet-point annotations below are editorial additions not present in the source material. Also, the notation `:isv[šт]` mixes Latin `š` with Cyrillic `т`, which appears to be a typographical error (should likely be `:isv[št]`).
:::

Changes applied relative to standard:
- :isv[šč] → :isv[šт] (if occurring)
- :isv[ě] → :isv[je] / :isv[ije] per Croatian reflex
- :isv[ć] and :isv[đ] retained where etymologically justified
- :isv[y] → :isv[i] throughout
