# 6. Syntax — Overview

This section specifies the syntactic rules of Interslavic: the principles that govern how words combine into phrases, clauses, and sentences. The rules defined here apply to all standard written and spoken Interslavic regardless of flavorizational register.

## 6.0.2. Core Structural Properties

Interslavic exhibits the following typological properties, which together determine the overall shape of its syntax:

1. **Morphologically rich inflection.** Grammatical relations (subject, object, indirect object, etc.) are expressed by case endings on nouns, pronouns, adjectives, and numerals. Word order is therefore available to express discourse-level functions (topic, focus, contrast) rather than grammatical functions.

2. **Neutral word order: SVO.** Subject–Verb–Object is the pragmatically unmarked default. Other orderings are grammatical but discourse-marked (see §6.1.1–6.1.2).

3. **Pro-drop.** Overt subject pronouns are optional when recoverable from verb morphology (see §6.1.4).

4. **Prepositional phrases and case government.** Prepositions govern specific cases. The case selected by a preposition depends on the preposition's lexical identity and, for spatial prepositions, on whether motion or static location is expressed (see §6.2–6.3).

5. **No articles.** Interslavic has no grammatical articles. Definiteness MAY be indicated pragmatically by the demonstrative *toj / та / то* (*тoj / та / то*), but this is not obligatory (see §6.2.3).

## 6.0.3. Relationship Between Syntax and Morphology

The syntactic rules in this chapter presuppose the morphological forms specified in Chapter 5. In particular:

- Case assignment rules (§6.2, §6.3) reference the seven-case system described in §5.2–§5.4.
- Verbal agreement rules (§6.1.3) reference the person/number paradigms in §5.5.
- Coordination agreement (§6.4.2) references the gender system in §5.2.1.

---

## 6.0.4. Normative Status

All rules in Chapter 6 that use the modal verbs **MUST**, **SHALL**, **SHOULD**, **MAY**, and **SHOULD NOT** carry the following normative force:

- **MUST / SHALL** — an absolute grammatical requirement; violation produces an ungrammatical sentence.
- **SHOULD** — the strongly recommended default; deviation is grammatical but stylistically marked.
- **MAY** — an explicitly permitted option; neither preferred nor dispreferred.
- **SHOULD NOT** — a usage that is grammatical but actively discouraged as stylistically poor or cross-dialectally opaque.
