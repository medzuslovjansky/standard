# 6.1. Basic Clause Structure

This section specifies the structural principles governing simple clauses in Interslavic: canonical constituent order, pragmatic deviations from that order, subject-verb agreement, pro-drop, and the syntax of existential sentences.

---

## 6.1.1. Canonical Word Order: SVO

Interslavic employs **Subject – Verb – Object (SVO)** as its neutral, pragmatically unmarked word order. This is the preferred order for declarative sentences where no constituent bears special emphasis or discourse prominence.

> :isv[Otec kupil knigu.] — "Father bought a book."

Other orderings (SOV, VSO, OVS, etc.) are **syntactically grammatical** but **pragmatically marked**: they signal topic–focus structuring, contrast, or stylistic register. Writers and speakers SHOULD use SVO as the default unless a specific pragmatic effect is intended.

**Modifiers** (adjectives, demonstratives, genitival attributes) normally precede the noun they modify. This is not an absolute requirement, but pre-nominal placement is the most neutral and unambiguous order.

---

## 6.1.2. Word Order and Pragmatic Function

Because Interslavic uses inflectional case endings to signal grammatical roles, constituent order is available to convey **information structure** rather than grammatical function.

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The following Topic/Focus/clause-final position table and the pragmatically marked order examples are not present in the source material (grammar-syntax.md). The source states only that word order is free and that placing the object before the subject does not help clarity. The detailed information-structure analysis is an unsourced elaboration.
:::

| Position | Pragmatic value |
|---|---|
| Pre-verbal (sentence-initial) | **Topic** — given, already-known information |
| Post-verbal | **Focus** — new, emphasized, or contrastive information |
| Clause-final | Heaviest stress in neutral intonation; most prominent focus position |

Placing the object before the subject does not in itself clarify meaning, because case endings already identify who does what to whom. However, sentences where a personal pronoun or the pronoun :isv[kto] functions as subject or object are always unambiguous even with inverted order, since these forms have dedicated accusative forms.

**Examples of pragmatically marked order:**

- Topic-initial (object fronted for contrast):
  - :isv[Knigu otec kupil, ne žurnalu.]
    "The **book** father bought, not the magazine."

- Focus on subject (subject postposed):
  - :isv[Kupil knigu otec.]
    "It was **father** who bought the book."

## 6.1.3. Subject-Verb Agreement

Finite verbs in Interslavic **MUST** agree with their subject in **person** and **number**.

- :isv[Ja čitam.] — "I read." (1sg)
- :isv[On čita.] — "He reads." (3sg)
- :isv[My čitamo.] — "We read." (1pl)

In compound tenses formed with the **L-participle** (past/conditional), the L-participle **MUST** agree with the subject in **gender** and **number**. The auxiliary :isv[byti] carries person–number agreement.

| Subject | L-participle form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Masculine singular | -*l* | :isv[on kupil] |
| Feminine singular | -*la* | :isv[ona kupila] |
| Neuter singular | -*lo* | :isv[ono kupilo] |
| Plural (all genders) | -*li* | :isv[oni kupili] |

When the subject is a coordinated NP with heads of mixed gender, the plural L-participle defaults to the masculine plural form *-li*.

## 6.1.4. Null/Pro-Drop Subject

Interslavic is a **pro-drop language**: an overt subject pronoun is **OPTIONAL** when the grammatical person and number of the subject are recoverable from the verb's inflectional ending.

- :isv[Čitam knigu.] — "I am reading a book." (subject :isv[ja] omitted)
- :isv[Kupili smo hljěb.] — "We bought bread." (subject :isv[my] omitted)

Overt subject pronouns **SHOULD** be used:
- for **emphasis**: :isv[Ja to znam, ne ty.] — "**I** know that, not you."
- for **contrast**: :isv[On pišet, ona čita.] — "He writes, she reads."
- when **disambiguation** is needed and the inflectional form is ambiguous (e.g., some archaic or colloquial 1sg/3sg syncretic forms).

Subject pronouns are **NOT** required in any position; their absence is the stylistically neutral choice in running prose when the referent is clear.

## 6.1.5. Existential and Presentational Sentences

### 6.1.5.1. Affirmative Existential Sentences

Existential sentences ("there is / there are") are formed with the copula :isv[jest] (3sg) or :isv[sųt] (3pl) followed by a **nominative** subject NP, typically accompanied by a locative or circumstantial phrase.

- :isv[V městě jest bolnica.] — "There is a hospital in the city."
- :isv[Na stolu sųt knigi.] — "There are books on the table."

The existential subject is typically indefinite or newly introduced into the discourse (presentational use).

### 6.1.5.2. Negative Existential Sentences: Genitive of Negation

Under **sentential negation**, the subject (or direct object) of an existential/presentational clause **SHOULD** shift from nominative (accusative) to **genitive**. This is the genitive of negation.

- Affirmative: :isv[Jest voda.] — "There is water." (nominative)
- Negative: :isv[Nět vody.] — "There is no water." (genitive)
- Affirmative: :isv[Sųt studenti.] — "There are students."
- Negative: :isv[Nět studentov.] — "There are no students."

The negative existential particle :isv[nět] ("there is no") is regularly used in this construction and does not require a separate form of :isv[byti].

### 6.1.5.3. Omission of *jest* in Third-Person Copular Sentences

The copula :isv[jest] is **frequently omitted** in 3rd person present tense copular and existential sentences, particularly in informal or neutral register. This omission is grammatical and common:

- :isv[On *(jest)* doktor.] — "He is a doctor."
- :isv[Ona *(jest)* zdrava.] — "She is healthy."

The omission of :isv[jest] is not permissible in other persons (1sg :isv[jesm], 2sg :isv[jesi], etc.) in copular sentences.

## 6.1.6. Passive Constructions and Alternatives

The passive voice is **grammatically available** in Interslavic but SHOULD be used with caution, as its naturalness varies across the Slavic readership.

### 6.1.6.1. Analytic Passive

The analytic passive is formed with :isv[byti] + **past passive participle** (PPP, suffix *-n-/-t-*):

- :isv[Pica je dělana.] — "The pizza is being made." (present passive)
- :isv[Pica była dělana.] — "The pizza was made." (past passive)

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The specific mention of Bulgarian and Macedonian as languages that do not use PPP in present passive constructions is not in the source material. The source states more generally that "for others the past passive participle cannot be used for a present tense construction at all" without naming specific languages.
:::

While grammatically correct, this construction can sound unnatural to Slavic speakers unaccustomed to frequent use of :isv[byti] in the present tense, or to those whose native languages (e.g., Bulgarian, Macedonian) do not use PPP in present passive constructions in the same way.

### 6.1.6.2. Preferred Alternatives to the Passive

When the agent is **unknown or indefinite**, two constructions are preferred over the analytic passive:

1. **Third-person plural with null subject** ("impersonal active"):
   - :isv[Dělajut picu.] — "They make pizza / One makes pizza / Pizza is being made."

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The reflexive particle is written as :isv[sę] here, but the source material (grammar-syntax.md) uses :isv[se] in the same example: ":isv[Pica dělaje se]". Verify whether :isv[sę] or :isv[se] is the correct standard form.
:::

2. **Reflexive-passive with :isv[sę]**:
   - :isv[Pica dělaje sę.] — Literally "Pizza makes itself"; translated as "One makes pizza / Pizza is being made."

When the agent **is** known, a normal active sentence SHOULD be used in preference to the passive.

## 6.1.7. Purpose (Final) Clauses

Purpose clauses express the goal or intention of the action in the main clause. They are introduced by the conjunctions :isv[že] or :isv[da] followed by the **conditional** (:isv[by] + L-participle).

- :isv[Turisti posětet Niderlandiju, že by fotografovali tulipany.]
  "Tourists visit the Netherlands **to take** photos of the tulips."

- :isv[Turisti posětili Niderlandiju, že by fotografovali tulipany.]
  "Tourists visited the Netherlands **to take** photos of the tulips."

- :isv[Ona prinosi jabloka, da by děti byli zdrave.]
  "She brings apples **so that** the kids will be healthy."

- :isv[Ona prinosila jabloka, da by děti byli zdrave.]
  "She brought apples **so that** the kids would be healthy."

The tense of the main clause (present or past) does not affect the form of the purpose clause: the conditional :isv[by] + L-participle is used in both cases.

## 6.1.8. The *da* + Present Tense Construction

### 6.1.8.1. *Da* as an Alternative to the Infinitive

The construction **da + present tense** is attested in all South Slavic languages and is available in Interslavic as an **optional** alternative to the infinitive after modal and other auxiliary verbs.

> This construction is particularly appropriate when communicating with speakers of Bulgarian and Macedonian, which lack an infinitive entirely. Writers targeting a pan-Slavic audience MAY prefer this construction to increase intelligibility for South Slavic readers.

The verb following :isv[da] stands in the **present tense** and agrees with its own subject, regardless of the tense of the governing verb.

| Person/Number | *da* construction | Infinitive construction |
|---|---|---|
| 1sg | :isv[ja moram da idem] | :isv[ja moram idti] |
| 2sg | :isv[ty moraš da ideš] | :isv[ty moraš idti] |
| 3sg | :isv[on mora da ide] | :isv[on mora idti] |
| 1pl | :isv[my moramo da idemo] | :isv[my moramo idti] |
| 2pl | :isv[vy morate da idete] | :isv[vy morate idti] |
| 3pl | :isv[oni morajųt da idųt] | :isv[oni morajųt idti] |

The reflexive particle :isv[sę] in these constructions is placed **after** :isv[da]:

- :isv[Ja moram da sę vråtim.] — "I must return."

This construction may replace the infinitive in virtually any syntactic environment:

- :isv[To mora da sę zna.] — "This must be known."
- :isv[Htěl jesm da kažem.] — "I wanted to say."
- :isv[My bųdemo da čitamo.] — "We will read."
- :isv[Ne mogl by da prijědeš?] — "Couldn't you come?"

### 6.1.8.2. *Da* as a Purposive/Volitional Conjunction

In South Slavic usage, :isv[da] also functions as a conjunction meaning "so that / in order that / that (with volitional force)". In this role it introduces a finite subordinate clause in the **present tense** or **conditional**, without a following infinitive:

- :isv[Prišel sem da ti kažem.] — "I came **so that** I might tell you." / "I came **to tell** you."
- :isv[Želim da mi otkryješ istinų.] — "I want **you** to reveal the truth to me."
- :isv[Naši prědki sųt sę boroli da bysmo žyvěli ščęstlivo.] — "Our ancestors fought **so that** we might live happily."

This usage overlaps functionally with the purpose clause construction described in §6.1.7 (using :isv[že]/:isv[da] + conditional). Both patterns are grammatically acceptable; the :isv[da] + present tense variant is more characteristic of South Slavic style, while :isv[že]/:isv[da] + conditional is more pan-Slavic in distribution.
