# 6.6. Relative Clauses

:::caution[Unverified Content]

The source material covers relative pronoun forms (ktory, koj, iže) and their inflection (in the pronouns section), but does not address relative clause syntax, gap vs. resumptive pronoun strategies, long-distance extraction, or free relative clause structure. This section specifies the structure, pronoun selection, inflection, and extraction strategies for relative clauses in Interslavic, including headed relative clauses, the gap vs. resumptive pronoun alternation, animacy effects, and free (headless) relative clauses.

---

## 6.6.1. Headed Relative Clauses with :isv[ktory]

The primary relative pronoun in Interslavic is :isv[ktory] ("who, which, that"). It introduces **headed relative clauses** — clauses that modify an explicit antecedent noun phrase.

:isv[Ktory] **agrees** with the antecedent in **gender** and **number**, and takes the **case** required by its grammatical function within the relative clause (subject, direct object, indirect object, etc.). It is inflected exactly like an ordinary hard-stem adjective.

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The verb form :isv[dah] in the table below and in section 6.6.2 is a South Slavic aorist form. Standard ISV past tense of :isv[dati] would be :isv[dal].
:::

| Function in relative clause | Example |
|---|---|
| Subject | :isv[žena, ktora čita] — "a woman who reads" |
| Direct object | :isv[děte, ktore ja viděl] — "the child that I saw" |
| Indirect object (dative) | :isv[muž, ktoremu dah knigu] — "the man to whom I gave the book" |
| Genitive (possession) | :isv[žena, ktoroj muž je lekar] — "the woman whose husband is a doctor" |
| Locative (place) | :isv[město, v ktorom on žive] — "the city in which he lives" |

**Full example with internal structure:**

- :isv[Ja viděl muža, ktory prišel včera.]
  "I saw the man who came yesterday."
  (antecedent :isv[muž] is masculine singular; :isv[ktory] = nominative masculine singular, functioning as subject of the relative clause)

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The form :isv[kturų] below appears to be a typo for :isv[ktorų]. The parenthetical explanation correctly says :isv[ktorų] but the example sentence has :isv[kturų].
:::

- :isv[Kniga, kturų on čital, jest interesna.]
  "The book that he was reading is interesting."
  (antecedent :isv[kniga] is feminine singular; :isv[ktorų] = accusative feminine singular, functioning as direct object)

The relative clause immediately follows the antecedent NP. Separation of the relative clause from its antecedent by intervening material is possible but stylistically marked.

## 6.6.2. The :isv[koj] Alternative (Southern Flavorization)

The pronoun :isv[koj] ("which") is the **South Slavic variant** of the relative pronoun. It is inflected like the possessive pronoun :isv[moj] (soft-stem adjective paradigm) and is used in precisely the same syntactic construction as :isv[ktory].

:isv[Koj] and :isv[ktory] are **fully interchangeable** as relative pronouns; their meanings are identical. The choice between them is a matter of **stylistic register** or **dialect orientation**:

- :isv[ktory] — pan-Slavic, appropriate in all contexts and registers
- :isv[koj] — South Slavic flavor; appropriate when targeting a South Slavic readership or for stylistic variation

**Examples with :isv[koj]:**

- :isv[žena, koja čita] — "a woman who reads"
- :isv[děte, koje ja viděl] — "the child that I saw"
- :isv[muž, kojemu dah knigu] — "the man to whom I gave the book"

In all cases where :isv[koj] appears, :isv[ktory] may be substituted, and vice versa.

---

## 6.6.3. The Archaic Pronoun :isv[iže]

A third, more archaic relative pronoun :isv[iže] is also available, primarily in elevated or literary style. Its declension is irregular:

- **Nominative** (all genders, both numbers): :isv[iže]
- **Other cases**: inflected like the corresponding forms of the personal pronoun :isv[on] / :isv[ona] / :isv[ono] with the suffix :isv[-že] appended:
  - Masculine genitive singular: :isv[jegože]
  - Masculine dative singular: :isv[jemuže]
  - Feminine genitive singular: :isv[jejže]
  - etc.

:isv[Iže] is not recommended for ordinary prose; it is retained here for recognition purposes and for writers adopting a Church Slavonic-influenced register.

## 6.6.4. Gap Strategy (No Resumptive Pronoun)

Interslavic relative clauses normally employ the **gap strategy**: the position from which the relative pronoun has been extracted is left **empty** (a syntactic gap). No resumptive pronoun is inserted in the extraction site.

The relative pronoun itself, appearing at the head of the relative clause, bears the case that corresponds to its grammatical role in the embedded clause. This is what makes the case inflection of :isv[ktory]/:isv[koj] syntactically crucial.

**Correct (gap strategy):**

- :isv[Muž, ktorogo [∅] ja ljubim, jest doktor.]
  "The man whom I love is a doctor."
  (:isv[ktorogo] = genitive/accusative of animate masculine; gap in object position)

**Incorrect (resumptive pronoun — not used in standard Interslavic):**

- ~~:isv[Muž, ktory ja jeho ljubim, jest doktor.]~~ (non-standard)

The absence of resumptive pronouns in canonical relative clauses is a structural requirement of standard Interslavic, not merely a preference.

---

## 6.6.5. Resumptive Pronoun in Long-Distance Extraction

In cases of **deeply embedded extraction** — where the gap would be separated from the relative pronoun by one or more clause boundaries — a **resumptive pronoun MAY appear** in the extraction site. This is a licensed exception to the gap strategy specified in §6.6.4.

Long-distance extraction arises when the antecedent is relativized out of an embedded complement clause:

- :isv[Muž, ktory ona skazala, čto ona jemu věrit, jest tamo.]
  "The man whom she said she trusts is there."
  (resumptive :isv[jemu] in the dative position within the embedded clause)

The use of a resumptive pronoun in such contexts reduces processing difficulty and is grammatically acceptable. Writers SHOULD use a resumptive pronoun when the extraction site is more than one clause boundary away from the relative pronoun.

## 6.6.6. Animacy Effects in Relative Pronoun Selection

The accusative form of the relative pronoun :isv[ktory] follows the **animacy distinction** that applies to all masculine adjective-like forms in Interslavic:

- **Inanimate masculine singular accusative** = same as nominative: :isv[ktory]
- **Animate masculine singular accusative** = same as genitive: :isv[ktorogo]

| Animacy | Antecedent | Relative pronoun (accusative) | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inanimate | :isv[stol] "table" (m.) | :isv[ktory] | :isv[stol, ktory ja viděl] — "the table that I saw" |
| Animate | :isv[muž] "man" (m.) | :isv[ktorogo] | :isv[muž, ktorogo ja viděl] — "the man whom I saw" |
| Feminine | :isv[žena] "woman" (f.) | :isv[ktorų] | :isv[žena, ktorų ja viděl] — "the woman whom I saw" |
| Neuter | :isv[děte] "child" (n.) | :isv[ktore] | :isv[děte, ktore ja viděl] — "the child that I saw" |

The same animacy rule applies to :isv[koj] in its accusative forms.

## 6.6.7. Free (Headless) Relative Clauses

A **free relative clause** (also called a headless relative clause) is a relative clause that lacks an explicit antecedent noun. The relative pronoun itself incorporates both the role of "head" and "relativizer". Interslavic forms free relative clauses with the pronouns :isv[kto] ("whoever"), :isv[čto] ("whatever"), and :isv[ktory] / :isv[koj] ("whichever") used without an overt antecedent NP:

- :isv[Kto žela, može priti.]
  "Whoever wishes may come."

- :isv[Čto on skazal, jest pravda.]
  "What he said is true." / "Whatever he said is true."

- :isv[Ktory pridet pervym, dostanet nagradu.]
  "Whichever (one) comes first will receive a prize."

In free relative clauses with :isv[kto] and :isv[čto], the pronoun takes the case required by its function in the **matrix** clause (since there is no antecedent NP to whose case it must agree). When :isv[kto] or :isv[čto] also has a syntactic role within the embedded clause, the case of the embedded function takes precedence, and the matrix function is supplied by the clause as a whole.

- :isv[Ja dam knigu tomu, komu ona nužna jest.]
  "I will give the book to whoever needs it."
  (demonstrative :isv[tomu] serves as matrix indirect object; :isv[komu] = dative in embedded clause)
