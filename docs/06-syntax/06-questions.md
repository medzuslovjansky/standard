# 6.7. Question Formation

:::caution[Unverified Content]
This file has no dedicated source document for question syntax. The core examples in §6.7.1 and §6.7.3 can be partially verified against Jan van Steenbergen's syntax page (`grammar-syntax.md`), but the detailed rules and additional sections (§6.7.2 li-placement, §6.7.4 multiple wh-questions, §6.7.5 embedded questions) are elaborations without direct source backing.
:::

This section specifies the syntactic mechanisms for forming questions in Interslavic: yes/no questions (polar questions) using intonation, the :isv[či] particle, or the :isv[li] clitic; and constituent (wh-) questions involving fronting of interrogative pronouns and adverbs. It also covers multiple wh-questions and embedded (indirect) questions.

---

## 6.7.1. Yes/No Questions: Three Strategies

Interslavic provides three grammatically equivalent strategies for forming polar (yes/no) questions. All three are acceptable; the choice is stylistic or register-based.

### 6.7.1.1. Intonation Only

The simplest strategy is to use a declarative sentence with **rising intonation**. No structural change is required; the question is signaled solely by pitch contour.

> :isv[Otec kupil knigu?]
> "Has father bought a book?"

This strategy is fully grammatical in both written and spoken registers. In written text it is marked by the question mark.

### 6.7.1.2. The :isv[Či] Particle

The particle :isv[či] is placed at the very beginning of the clause, preceding the subject. The rest of the clause retains declarative word order.

> :isv[Či otec kupil knigu?]
> "Has father bought a book?"

The particle :isv[či] is borrowed from the South and West Slavic interrogative tradition. It is neutral with respect to which element of the proposition is being questioned: the entire proposition is put in question.

### 6.7.1.3. The :isv[Li] Particle

The particle :isv[li] is placed **immediately after** the constituent that is the focus of the question. Most commonly this is the finite verb, but it may follow any focused constituent. The focused constituent is typically moved to clause-initial position to precede :isv[li].

> :isv[Kupil li otec knigu?]
> "Has father bought a book?" (focus: the buying)

> :isv[Otec li kupil knigu?]
> "Was it **father** who bought a book?" (focus: the subject)

> :isv[Knigu li kupil otec?]
> "Was it **a book** that father bought?" (focus: the object)

The :isv[li] construction is the most explicit means of indicating which constituent is being questioned and is therefore the most information-structurally precise of the three strategies.

---

## 6.7.2. *Li*-Particle Placement and Focus

The particle :isv[li] is a **post-positive clitic**: it must attach to the word or phrase immediately to its left and cannot appear clause-initially or clause-finally on its own.

**Placement rules:**

1. :isv[Li] is placed **directly after** the constituent that bears the question focus.
2. The focused constituent is typically **moved to the front** of the clause, so that :isv[li] appears early in the sentence.
3. The canonical pattern is: **[Focused constituent + :isv[li]] + [rest of clause]**.

| Focus constituent | Example | Translation |
|---|---|---|
| Verb | :isv[Kupil li otec knigu?] | "Did father buy a book?" |
| Subject | :isv[Otec li kupil knigu?] | "Was it father who bought a book?" |
| Object | :isv[Knigu li kupil otec?] | "Was it a book that father bought?" |

When the verb is the default focus (i.e., the entire proposition is questioned without special constituent focus), the verb-:isv[li] construction functions equivalently to the :isv[či]-initial and intonation-only strategies.

## 6.7.3. Wh-Questions: Fronting of the Interrogative

Content questions (wh-questions) are formed by moving an **interrogative pronoun or adverb** to the **clause-initial** position. The rest of the clause retains normal word order.

Interslavic interrogative words include:

| Interrogative | Gloss |
|---|---|
| :isv[kto] | who |
| :isv[čto] / :isv[što] | what |
| :isv[koj] / :isv[koji] / :isv[ktory] | which |
| :isv[kaky] | what kind of |
| :isv[koliko] | how many / how much |
| :isv[kde] | where |
| :isv[kamo] | whither (to where) |
| :isv[kųda] | whence (from where) |
| :isv[kogda] | when |
| :isv[kako] | how |
| :isv[začto] / :isv[počto] | why |

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The gloss of :isv[kųda] as "whence (from where)" may be incorrect. In most Slavic languages, cognates of *kuda* mean "whither (to where)", not "whence (from where)". "Whence" is typically expressed with a prefixed form such as :isv[odkųda]. This gloss could not be verified against source material.
:::

:::caution[Unverified Content]
The forms :isv[začto] and :isv[počto] for "why" could not be verified in the source material. The source correlatives table distinguishes "why (purpose)" from "why (reason)" but does not confirm these specific lexical forms. :isv[počto] in particular resembles Russian :isv[почто] rather than a pan-Slavic compromise form.
:::

**The interrogative pronoun or adverb MUST move to clause-initial position.** The case marking on the interrogative reflects its **grammatical role** in the clause, not its surface position. This means that the moved interrogative bears the same case it would bear in its base position within the clause.

> :isv[Koju knigu kupil otec?]
> "Which book did father buy?" (:isv[koju] = accusative, direct object)

> :isv[Kde otec kupil tu knigu?]
> "Where did father buy that book?"

> :isv[Kto kupil knigu?]
> "Who bought the book?" (:isv[kto] = nominative, subject)

> :isv[Komu dal otec knigu?]
> "To whom did father give the book?" (:isv[komu] = dative)

Because case endings mark grammatical roles independently of word order, wh-movement does not create ambiguity:

> :isv[Koja žena ljubi togo muža?]
> "Which woman loves that man?" (woman = nominative subject, man = accusative object)

> :isv[Koju ženu ljubi toj muž?]
> "Which woman does that man love?" (man = nominative subject, woman = accusative object)

Where disambiguation is needed beyond what case marking provides, a passive construction makes the roles explicit:

> :isv[Koja žena jest ljubjena od togo muža?]
> "Which woman is loved by that man?"

## 6.7.4. Multiple Wh-Questions

When a clause contains more than one questioned constituent (multiple wh-question), the following rule applies:

- **One** interrogative pronoun or adverb is moved to clause-initial position.
- The **remaining** interrogative(s) stay in their base (in-situ) position within the clause.

> :isv[Kto komu dal knigu?]
> "Who gave a book to whom?"

Fronting of **all** interrogatives simultaneously (pied-piping of multiple wh-words) is also attested and is grammatically permissible, but this is a **marked** construction characteristic of more formal or rhetorical registers:

> :isv[Kto čto jěl?]
> "Who ate what?" (both interrogatives fronted — permissible but marked)

## 6.7.5. Embedded Questions

Embedded (indirect) questions use the **same interrogative pronouns and adverbs** as direct questions. The embedded clause **does not undergo inversion** of any kind; it retains the word order of a declarative clause with the interrogative element in front.

> :isv[Ne znam, kto kupil knigu.]
> "I don't know who bought the book."

> :isv[Ona spytala, kde jest bolnica.]
> "She asked where the hospital is."

> :isv[Skažite mi, koliko to stoit.]
> "Tell me how much that costs."

The embedded question is introduced by the interrogative word alone; no additional complementizer (:isv[že], :isv[čto]) is inserted. The verb of the embedded clause carries a tense appropriate to its own temporal reference, without obligatory sequence-of-tense adjustment (see §6.5.3).

Embedded yes/no questions are introduced by :isv[li] (retained from the direct question) or by :isv[či]:

> :isv[Ne znam, kupil li otec knigu.]
> "I don't know whether father bought a book."

> :isv[Ona spytala, či on pridot.]
> "She asked whether he would come."
