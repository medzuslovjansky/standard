Pragmatics

Unverified Content

This file has no direct source material for fact-checking. The pragmatic rules, ISV example forms, and phrasebook entries below have not been verified against an authoritative reference and require expert review.

This section specifies the pragmatic rules of Interslavic: how speakers and writers choose among grammatically equivalent forms to achieve communicative goals, how social relationships are encoded in address forms, how information is structured within and across sentences, and how language is used to perform social actions.

Register and Politeness​

T/V Distinction: ty vs. vy​

Interslavic maintains the T/V distinction inherited from all Slavic languages: the second-person singular pronoun ty (T-form) and the second-person plural pronoun vy (V-form) are used differently depending on social context.

FormPrimary use
tyInformal address to one person; family members, close friends, children, peers in informal settings
vyFormal address to one person; also the standard second-person plural regardless of formality

When vy is used as a polite singular address (the V-form), verbs and predicates MUST agree in the second-person plural. The L-participle in compound tenses, however, agrees with the actual (singular) referent in gender.

  • Formal address to one man: Vy govorite dobro, gospodine. — "You speak well, sir." (verb: 2pl; referent: sg. masc.)
  • Formal address to one woman: Vy ste skazala, čto... — "You said that..." (L-participle: sg. fem.)

The choice between ty and vy when addressing an individual is a pragmatic decision, not a grammatical one. The following general conventions apply:

SituationExpected form
Addressing a childty
Addressing a close friend or family memberty
First contact with an adult strangervy (default safe choice)
Professional or official contextvy
Informal online or community contextty (increasingly common across Slavic languages)
Explicit mutual agreement to use informal addressty

Writers of Interslavic texts SHOULD use vy as the default when the audience is unknown or mixed, and SHOULD use ty only when the communicative context clearly establishes informality.

Register Vocabulary: Formal vs. Colloquial Synonyms​

Certain synonym pairs in Interslavic are register-differentiated: one form is appropriate for formal writing, the other for informal speech. The dictionary SHOULD mark register for all such pairs.

Writers SHOULD use formal vocabulary in:

  • official documents, academic texts, news reporting, public announcements
  • correspondence with unknown or senior addressees
  • any context where the Interslavic text represents a community or institution

Writers MAY use colloquial vocabulary in:

  • informal personal correspondence
  • creative fiction representing spoken dialogue
  • community forums where an informal tone is the established norm

Deixis​

Deixis is the encoding of spatial, temporal, and personal reference relative to the speech situation.

Person Deixis​

Personal pronouns are the primary person-deictic system of Interslavic. The full paradigm is specified in the relevant section The basic deictic values are:

PronounPerson deictic value
jaSpeaker
tyAddressee (singular informal, or singular formal V-form replaced by vy)
on / ona / onoThird person (absent referent), masculine / feminine / neuter
mySpeaker + others
vyAddressees (plural); or polite singular (V-form)
oni / oneThird persons (plural)

Spatial Deixis: toj / ta / to vs. sej / sa / se​

Interslavic has two demonstrative series expressing spatial distance from the speaker:

SeriesDeictic valueNotes
toj / ta / toDistal (that, those) — entity not in the immediate vicinity of the speakerThe neutral default demonstrative in modern Interslavic
sej / sa / seProximal (this, these) — entity in the immediate vicinity of the speakerMore archaic in character; use is stylistically optional

Both demonstratives decline for gender, number, and case in the same pattern as adjectives (see the relevant section).

The proximal series sej / sa / se is optional: a writer MAY always use toj to mean "this" when pointing to nearby objects, relying on context to supply the proximal reading. The proximal series SHOULD be used when a proximal–distal contrast needs to be made explicit within a single text segment.

  • Toj čelovek je moj prijatelj. — "That man is my friend." (distal)
  • Sej čelovek je moj prijatelj. — "This man is my friend." (proximal, explicit contrast)

Temporal Deixis​

Temporal deictic expressions locate events relative to the moment of speech:

These expressions are deictic anchors: their reference shifts with the time of the utterance. In reported speech or narrative, the deictic center may shift to a past reference point (narrative present), in which case temporal adverbs are reinterpreted relative to that past anchor.

Information Structure​

Topic–Comment Structure​

Interslavic discourse organizes clauses into topic (what the sentence is about; typically given, already-known information) and comment (what is said about the topic; typically new information).

The default position for the topic is sentence-initial. The default position for the comment (especially the most informative, rhematically prominent element) is post-verbal or sentence-final.

  • [Moja sestra [topic] kupila knigu [comment].] — "My sister bought a book." (neutral: sister is topic, book is new info)
  • [Knigu [topic] kupila moja sestra [comment].] — "The book was bought by my sister." (book is topicalized; sister is the new/contrasted info)

This principle interacts with pro-drop : when the topic-subject is recoverable from context, it is typically omitted.

Focus and Contrast​

Focused constituents — those bearing the most informationally prominent content — are marked primarily by prosodic prominence (stress in speech). In writing, focus may be conveyed by:

  1. Fronting the focused constituent to a pre-verbal or sentence-initial position.
  2. Using a cleft-like structure (To jest on, ktory... — "It is he who...").
  3. Italics or underlining in informal writing (a typographic convention, not a grammatical rule).

Contrastive focus signals that the focused constituent is being distinguished from an explicit or implicit alternative:

  • Ja kupil KNIGU, ne žurnal. — "I bought a BOOK, not a magazine." (contrastive focus on knigu)

See the relevant section for cross-reference to questions and information structure in the syntax chapter.

Theme–Rheme Progression in Discourse​

In coherent discourse, successive sentences maintain cohesion through theme–rheme chaining: the rheme (new information) of one sentence typically becomes the theme (given information) of the next.

Ona kupila knigu. — "She bought a book." Kniga jest o istoriji. — "The book is about history." Istorija — eto ejo ljubimaja tema. — "History is her favorite topic."

Each sentence takes the rheme of the preceding sentence as its theme, creating a chain of shared reference that signals discourse coherence. Writers SHOULD maintain this pattern in formal expository prose.

Discourse Markers​

Discourse markers are words and phrases that signal logical, temporal, or rhetorical relationships between clauses, sentences, or larger discourse units. They do not contribute propositional content but guide the reader's interpretation of how segments connect.

Content Transitions​

Beyond logical connectors, discourse cohesion is also maintained through content transitions — devices that carry the same topic or reference forward from one sentence or paragraph to the next. Merunka identifies four main techniques:

1. Pronouns. A noun subject is followed by a personal pronoun to maintain reference to the same entity, and by a possessive pronoun to shift to something associated with that entity. See the relevant section for the full pronoun paradigm.

  • Personal pronouns: ja, ty, on, ona, ono, my, vy, oni
  • Possessive pronouns: moj, tvoj, naš, vaš; the genitive-possessive forms of on/ona/ono/oni are jego/jej/jego/jih

Example:

Fido spi. On jest dobry pes. Jego dom jest pod jablonjeju. — "Fido is sleeping. He is a good dog. His house is under the apple tree."

2. Keyword repetition. Repeating the words that designate the key concepts of the discourse maintains topical continuity.

Example:

These monuments are old houses, bridges, theatres and churches. The oldest Prague monument is from the Romanesque era."

3. Synonyms. Using synonyms for the same referent confirms the partner's understanding and avoids repetition.

Example:

Vaše auto jest lěpo. Ale myslim, že taky voz jest mnogo skupy. — "Your car looks nice. But I think such a vehicle is very expensive."

4. Demonstrative pronouns. Demonstrative pronouns (toj, ta, to, ti, te and their extended forms tutoj, tamtoj) refer back to what has already been mentioned. See the relevant section and for the full declension.

Example:

Moj brat imaje veliky dom. Kako stary jest toj dom? — "My brother has a big house. How old is that house?"

Speech Acts​

Speech acts are communicative actions performed through language. The following subsections specify the standard Interslavic forms associated with major speech act types.

Assertions​

Assertions (declarative speech acts) are performed using declarative sentence form: subject–verb–object order with falling intonation (or period in writing).

  • Interslavsky jest slavjansky jezyk. — "Interslavic is a Slavic language."

No special marking is required for an assertion. The indicative mood is the default for assertions; the conditional is used for hypothetical assertions (see the relevant section).

Questions and Requests for Information​

Yes/no questions and content questions are specified in the relevant section Briefly:

  • Yes/no questions are formed with rising intonation, with the particle či sentence-initially, or with li after the verb.
  • Content questions are formed with an interrogative word (čto, kto, kde, kogda, kako, počemu, etc.) in sentence-initial position.

See the relevant section for full specification.

Requests and Commands​

Direct commands use the imperative mood (see the relevant section):

  • Daj mně knigu! — "Give me the book!" (2sg imperative)
  • Pojdemo! — "Let's go!" (1pl imperative)

Polite requests SHOULD use one of the following softening strategies:

  1. Conditional: Mogli by vy mně pomoći? — "Could you help me?"
  2. Interrogative + conditional: Prosim, možete li mně rěčti, kdě jest...? — "Could you please tell me where...?"
  3. Mitigating adverb: Prosim, dajte mně... — "Please give me..."

The bare imperative without a politeness marker is appropriate in informal contexts (with ty addressees) but SHOULD NOT be used in formal address.

Offers and Invitations​

Offers and invitations use hortative or interrogative structures:

  • Hortative (1pl imperative): Pojdemo v restoran! — "Let's go to a restaurant!"
  • Interrogative offer: Hoćete li čaj ili kafu? — "Would you like tea or coffee?"
  • Declarative offer: Ja mogu pomoći. — "I can help." (implicit offer)
  • Modal interrogative: Mogu li vam što predložiti? — "May I offer you something?"
