Phonological Alternations and Morphophonology

Inflection in Interslavic is kept as regular as possible. Nevertheless, alternations such as palatalization and iotation of consonants are pervasive across all Slavic languages. They play a crucial role in both inflection and word formation, and cannot be avoided even in the most simplified register of Interslavic — to do otherwise would produce forms that are heavily artificial and unnatural to every Slavic ear.

This section specifies all morphophonological alternations that are normative in Standard Interslavic. Rules stated with SHALL or MUST are obligatory; rules stated with SHOULD are the strong default; rules stated with MAY represent acceptable variation.

Second (Progressive) Palatalization: Lexically Preserved Forms​

The historical second (progressive) palatalization — whereby velars following a front vowel shifted to /c z s/ — is not a productive synchronic rule in Interslavic. However, its results are preserved in a number of lexicalized forms and SHOULD be recognized by learners.

Iotation​

Iotation is the conditioned change of a hard consonant when it is followed by the palatal glide /j/. The result differs from simple softening: the /j/ is absorbed into the consonant and does not appear in the surface form. Iotation is distinct from palatalization and from simple palatal softening .

Note on labials: In East Slavic and South Slavic languages, labial consonants + /j/ historically produced an epenthetic /l/ (e.g., Russian куплю, земля). In Interslavic this epenthesis SHALL NOT apply; the labial is simply written with j following it: kupjų, zemja, lovja, zatopjen. This approach is consistent with West Slavic usage and maximizes regularity.

Note on t/d iotation: The etymological outputs ć and đ (IPA [t͡ɕ] and [d͡ʑ]) are the results found in Serbo-Croatian, which Interslavic adopts as its reference for this class. In standard orthography these are written as č and dž (Cyrillic ч and дж). The distinction between ć/đ and č/dž is therefore of etymological significance only and does not affect standard spelling.

Note on st/zd iotation and the clusters šć and žđ: The iotation of st → šć and zd → žđ constitutes a distinct alternation pattern whose reflexes across Slavic languages differ substantially from those of simple t/​d iotation. For example: čistiti → čišćeńje/​čišćeny ("cleaning/cleaned"), jezditi → ježđeńje/​ježđeny ("riding/ridden").

There are two approaches to pronouncing these clusters in MS Plus. In the first approach (influenced by East Slavic, Polish), the preceding hard sibilant assimilates the softness of the following affricate: šć is pronounced [ɕt͡ɕ] (with š becoming [ɕ]) and žđ is pronounced [ʑd͡ʑ] (with ž becoming [ʑ]). This approach is reflected in IPA transcriptions in the Dynamic Dictionary. In the second approach (as in Serbo-Croatian, Czech, Slovak), each consonant retains its independent pronunciation: šć is [ʂt͡ɕ] and žđ is [ʐd͡ʑ], with the sibilant remaining hard. Both approaches are acceptable; the second is simpler in that each letter has a fixed pronunciation regardless of context.

Interaction of Soft Consonants with /j/: Palatalization Without Iotation​

When a consonant that is already soft is followed by /j/, the consonant and glide remain unchanged; no further iotation occurs. The softener blocks the change.

In the etymological register, sequences such as ljj, njj are written as ľj, ńj to avoid gemination of j; similarly ŕj, t́j, d́j, śj, źj, šj, žj, čj. In standard orthography these are written simply as lj, nj, rj, tj, dj, sj, zj, šj, žj, čj.

Where a stem ends in -j, the following suffix-initial j- is absorbed: e.g., dvoj-iti → dvoj-jų → dvojų.

This pattern applies in:

Practical consequence: The suffixes -je, -ji, and the instrumental ending -jų trigger iotation only of velars (k g h) and c; they do NOT trigger iotation of dentals (t d s z) or other consonants in standard Interslavic.

The o/e Alternation After Soft Consonants​

Old Slavic did not permit /o/ after a soft consonant. Whenever such a sequence arose through inflection or suffixation, the /o/ was replaced by /e/. This rule is maintained as a productive alternation in Interslavic.

Rule Statement​

After a soft consonant, the following ending and suffix vowels SHALL be changed as shown:

Fleeting Vowels (Jer Reflexes)​

A characteristic feature of Slavic morphophonology is the existence of "fleeting" (movable) vowels — vowels that appear in some forms of a word but disappear in others. These reflect the Common Slavic reduced vowels (jers) ъ and ь, which were lost in "weak" syllabic positions and vocalized to /o/ and /e/ in "strong" positions.

In most cases, a fleeting vowel appears when its absence would produce an impermissible final consonant cluster, and disappears when a vowel-initial ending follows.

Fleeting Vowel Marking in Citation Forms​

In standard orthography, fleeting vowels are not distinguished from regular vowels in spelling; the word is written in its full (strong) form with o or e. In the etymological register, fleeting vowels MAY be marked with the dot diacritic: ȯ for fleeting /o/ and ė for fleeting /e/. Dictionaries SHOULD mark fleeting vowels to signal the alternation to learners and NLP tools.

The cross-Slavic divergence of the hard jer *ъ (producing o in Russian, e in Czech/Slovak, a in Serbo-Croatian, ъ [ɤ/ɐ] in Bulgarian) is the main phonological reason the ȯ diacritic was introduced: it allows consistent etymological spelling where the standard letters would differ across national conventions.

Voicing Assimilation​

Regressive (Anticipatory) Voicing Assimilation​

Within obstruent clusters, voicing assimilates regressively: each obstruent in the cluster takes on the voicing of the rightmost obstruent. This rule applies within words and SHOULD be reflected in pronunciation; it is not indicated in standard orthography.

RuleEnvironmentExamplePronunciationGloss
Voiceless + voiced → voiced + voicedcluster-internalprosba[ˈprɔzba]request
Voiced + voiceless → voiceless + voicelesscluster-internalvod+ka[ˈvɔtka]vodka (loanword)

Transparent Segments: /v/ and /j/​

The consonants /v/ and /j/ SHALL NOT trigger devoicing of a preceding obstruent. They are phonologically transparent with respect to voicing assimilation: an obstruent before /v/ or /j/ retains its underlying voicing specification.

Final Devoicing: Optional​

Final devoicing (the neutralization of voiced/voiceless obstruent contrasts in word-final position) is optional in Standard Interslavic. All major Slavic languages apply final devoicing except Ukrainian and Shtokavian (Serbian/Croatian/Bosnian), where word-final obstruents retain their voicing. Speakers MAY apply final devoicing or not, according to their native habits — neither approach significantly affects intelligibility.

Vowel Reduction​

Vowel Reduction is Not Normative​

Unstressed vowel reduction — the process by which unstressed vowels merge into a central or mid vowel (as in Russian аканье / akan'je, where unstressed /o/ and /a/ merge to [ɐ] or [ə]) — is NOT prescribed in Standard Interslavic.

All vowels SHALL maintain their full phonemic quality regardless of stress position. This ensures maximum intelligibility across the Slavic language area, since vowel quality carries lexical and morphological information that would otherwise be lost.

Permitted Speaker-Based Reduction​

Speakers from languages with strong vowel reduction (Russian, Ukrainian, to a lesser extent Bulgarian) MAY apply their native reduction patterns in casual or spontaneous speech. This is an acceptable allophonic variation and does not render the speech non-standard, provided the phonemic distinctions remain recoverable from context. In formal, written, or normative contexts, full vowel quality SHALL be maintained.
