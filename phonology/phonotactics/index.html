Phonotactics

Interslavic orthography is based on etymology rather than pronunciation, so consonant clusters arise that may appear unpronounceable to speakers of non-Slavic languages — for example, vozvršenje. The language does not impose phonotactic constraints on consonant clusters beyond what is inherited from the attested Slavic lexicon. Constraints do exist, however, in the co-occurrence of certain vowels and consonants.

Syllable Structure​

The source material does not discuss syllable structure, syllable templates, or maximal syllable forms. The source states only that "every syllable contains one of the following vowels" and lists them. The formal syllable-template analysis in this subsection appears to be editorial elaboration.

Syllable Template​

The maximal syllable template in Interslavic is:

(C)(C)(C) V (C)(C)(C)

where V (the syllabic nucleus) is any of the following:

  • a vowel: a, å, e, ę, ė, ě, i, o, ȯ, u, ų, y
  • syllabic r or ŕ (preceded by a consonant and not followed by a vowel)

Every syllable SHALL contain exactly one syllabic nucleus. Syllabic r and ŕ are pronounced with a preceding schwa: /tərg/ (trg), /ʦukər/ (cukr), /mjərtvɪ/ (mŕtvy).

Unverified Content

The source material does not discuss pronunciation advice for difficult clusters, epenthesis, or cluster reduction. This guidance is editorial.

Because Interslavic orthography is etymological, the written form may produce onset and coda clusters that exceed the phonotactic norms of any individual Slavic language. Such clusters SHOULD be pronounced as they are in the speaker's native Slavic language, with epenthesis or cluster reduction permitted as natural adaptations.

Onset Constraints​

Unverified Content

The source material does not discuss onset constraints, a "positive-attestation criterion," or epenthetic vowel insertion. The source states only that orthography is etymological and clusters may appear unpronounceable. This subsection is editorial elaboration.

Interslavic permits any consonant onset cluster that is attested in at least one standard Slavic language. There are no additional restrictions on onset complexity beyond this positive-attestation criterion.

Speakers whose native language does not permit a given cluster MAY insert an epenthetic vowel to aid pronunciation without this being considered an error in spoken Interslavic.

Coda Constraints​

Unverified Content

The source material does not discuss coda constraints, the "positive-attestation criterion" for codas, or obstruent voicing assimilation rules. This subsection is editorial elaboration.

Coda clusters are likewise governed by the positive-attestation criterion: any coda sequence attested in at least one Slavic language is permitted.

Obstruent voicing assimilation applies across syllable and morpheme boundaries (see , Morphophonemic Alternations): a voiced obstruent in coda position assimilates to the voicing of a following obstruent, and word-finally obstruents MAY devoice in accordance with the speaker's native-language phonology.

The preferred cluster structure follows the sonority hierarchy:

In the preferred (canonical) onset, sonority SHOULD rise toward the nucleus; in the preferred coda, sonority SHOULD fall away from the nucleus. Departures from the sonority hierarchy are well-attested in the Slavic lexicon (e.g., sv-, zv-, čr-) and are fully permitted in Interslavic as inherited clusters.

Vowel-Consonant Co-occurrence Restrictions​

The following rules govern which vowels may follow which consonants.

Unverified Content

The source does not reference a "Proto-Slavic law of syllabic harmony" or the principle that "hard and soft sounds within a syllable must be congruent." The source simply states that there are limitations on combining certain vowels with certain consonants.

These restrictions reflect the Proto-Slavic law of syllabic harmony — the principle that hard and soft sounds within a syllable must be congruent — and its modern reflexes in the Slavic languages.

Distribution of /y/​

The phoneme /y/ (Latin y, Cyrillic ы) is subject to strict distributional constraints:

  1. /y/ MUST NOT follow a soft consonant. The soft consonants that prohibit /y/ are: š /ʃ/, ž /ʒ/, č /t͡ʃ/, dž /d͡ʒ/, c /t͡s/, lj /lʲ/, nj /nʲ/, j /j/ — and in the etymological register: ŕ, ť, ď, ś, ź.
  2. /y/ MUST NOT appear word-initially.
  3. /y/ MUST NOT appear immediately after a vowel.

In morphological contexts where a soft-consonant stem would ordinarily take an ending containing /y/, the /y/ SHALL be replaced:

  • In adjective and pronoun declension: /y/ → /i/ (e.g., dobr-y but svěž-i).
  • In noun declension: /y/ → /e/ (e.g., dom-y but kraj-e; žen-y but zemj-e).

Because several Slavic languages do not distinguish /i/ from /y/, substituting all occurrences of y with i is an acceptable simplification in written Interslavic.

after hard consonant (correct)dobrygood (adj., m.sg.)
after soft consonant (correct)svěžifresh (adj., m.sg.)
after soft consonant (incorrect)svěžy—

Distribution of /ě/ and /ŕ/​

The phonemes /ě/ (Latin ě, Cyrillic ѣ) and /ŕ/ (Latin ŕ, Cyrillic рь) MUST follow a hard consonant or the affricate c /t͡s/ in their base (lexical) forms.

In morphological contexts where a soft-consonant stem would ordinarily take /ě/, the /ě/ SHALL be replaced by /i/:

  • Dative and locative singular of feminine nouns: žen-ě (woman) but zemj-i (earth).
Unverified Content

The source describes these cases differently: it says ě and ŕ are "redundant" after j and č, and that they "can be pronounced in such way that they soften it." The source does not call them "exceptions" or say they are "lexicalized." The SHALL requirement to treat them as "listed forms rather than rule violations" is an editorial interpretation.

Apparent exceptions such as jěsti (to eat) and čŕny (black) arise because j and č are soft consonants. These exceptions are lexicalized and SHALL be treated as listed forms rather than rule violations.

Distribution of /o/ and /ȯ/ after Soft Consonants​

Unverified Content

The source says o and ȯ "rarely follow a soft consonant," which is weaker than the SHOULD NOT normative requirement stated here.

The vowels /o/ (Latin o, Cyrillic о) and /ȯ/ (Latin ȯ, Cyrillic ȯ) SHOULD NOT follow a soft consonant in native Slavic vocabulary. This restriction reflects the Proto-Slavic o→e rule (see , Morphophonemic Alternations), by which /o/ was historically replaced by /e/ after a soft consonant in grammatical endings and suffixes.

Exceptions are permitted in:

  • International loanwords: majonez (mayonnaise), čokolada (chocolate).
  • Proper nouns and internationalisms not subject to Slavic morphophonology.

In native Slavic morphology, endings containing /o/ become /e/ after soft consonants:

Distribution of /å/​

The optional vowel /å/ (Latin å, Cyrillic а̊) is subject to two positional constraints:

  1. /å/ MUST be preceded by a liquid consonant — either r /r/ or l /l/.
  2. /å/ MUST be followed by a consonant; it does not appear in word-final position or before a vowel.

Additionally, /å/ MUST NOT appear word-initially or after a vowel (see the relevant section).

Unverified Content

The etymological explanation referring to Proto-Slavic *ā development is not present in the source material. The source confirms the distributional constraint but does not provide this historical derivation.

This distribution is inherited from Proto-Slavic, where *ā following a liquid consonant in a closed syllable developed differently from *ā in other environments. In flavourised Interslavic, /å/ is written and pronounced as o.

Word-Initial Restrictions on Optional Vowels​

The following vowels and syllabic consonants MUST NOT appear word-initially or immediately after another vowel:

å, ę, ė, ě, ȯ, ų, y, syllabic r, syllabic ŕ

Unverified Content

The source confirms the word-initial restriction but does not mention "standard and etymological registers" applying uniformly, nor does it provide the Proto-Slavic historical explanation given here.

This restriction applies uniformly in both the standard and etymological registers. These phonemes are historically conditioned: they developed from Proto-Slavic phonemes in specific syllabic environments and have no attested word-initial or post-vocalic distribution across the Slavic languages.

Geminate Consonants​

Unverified Content

The source confirms that geminates arise only from prefixing, suffixing, or rare loanwords, but does not prescribe how geminates should be pronounced. The SHALL requirement to pronounce them as "single (lengthened or unreleased) consonants" and the characterization as "surface phenomenon" vs "underlying phonological representations" are editorial additions.

Geminate (double) consonants DO NOT occur in native Interslavic vocabulary as underlying phonological representations. Geminates arise only at morpheme boundaries as a surface phenomenon and SHALL be pronounced as single (lengthened or unreleased) consonants in natural speech; pronounced as two distinct consonants at morpheme boundaries is also acceptable depending on the speaker's native phonological habits.

Contexts in which geminates arise:

  1. Prefixation, when a prefix ending in a consonant is attached to a stem beginning with the same consonant:

    • od- + dati → oddati (to give away)
    • v- + voz → vvoz (import)

  2. Suffixation, when a stem-final consonant is followed by a suffix beginning with the same consonant:

    • kon + -ny → konny (equestrian)
    • rus + -sky → russky (Russian, adj.)

  3. Loanwords, where the geminate is part of the source-language form:

    • motto

In all cases, the standard orthography retains the spelling that reflects the morphological structure (od-dati, kon-ny), even when pronunciation reduces the geminate.
