Phonemic Inventory

Interslavic is neither a national language, nor does it attempt to emulate one. Instead, it is based on twelve national languages, each with its own phonology and orthography tailored to fit its particular characteristics. In general, the further South one goes in the Slavic world, the smaller the phoneme inventory becomes. Both spoken and written Interslavic SHALL aim to occupy the middle ground among these languages as much as possible.

A high number of phonemes makes intuitive writing and pronunciation harder for speakers accustomed to fewer phonemes. A low number, conversely, makes it harder to map a character or sound to an equivalent in one's own language, which has a negative effect on intelligibility. For example, the ideal pronunciation of the word for "five" would be something like [pjætʲ]. If written as pęt́, a Russian speaker can easily recognize their own пять, a Serb their own pet, and a Pole their own pięć; but a Serb has no way of knowing when their own e and t correspond to ę and t́ without consulting a dictionary. On the other hand, the simplified form pet could be misread as Russian петь "to sing" or Polish pet "cigarette butt."

To resolve this dilemma, Interslavic has a basic set of phonemes present in all or the vast majority of Slavic languages, more or less with the same phonetic values. In addition, Interslavic has a set of optional (etymological) phonemes that connect directly to Old Church Slavonic and reflect particular phonetic distinctions between individual languages. These optional phonemes MAY be written by means of the Interslavic etymological alphabet (formerly known as Naučny Medžuslovjansky).

Vowels​

Core Vowel Phonemes (7)​

Basic Interslavic has seven vowel phonemes. Five of them — /a/, /e/, /i/, /o/, /u/ — have a relatively uniform pronunciation across speakers and are mandatory in all registers. The remaining two — /ě/ and /y/ — have a pronunciation that may differ between speakers.

The phoneme y never softens the preceding consonant, which is significant for morphology (e.g., the hard/soft declension distinction: dobry → dobrogo vs. svěži → svěžego). In South Slavic and Ukrainian, /i/ and /y/ have merged into one vowel; substituting all occurrences of y with i is an acceptable simplification in written Interslavic, though this obscures certain morphological patterns. The phoneme /y/ MUST NOT follow a soft consonant, appear word-initially, or appear after a vowel (see the relevant section).

Optional (Etymological) Vowel Phonemes (5)​

In addition to the seven core vowels, Interslavic has five optional vowels that belong to the etymological register. Their diacritical marks are not written in the standard (basic) orthography; they SHALL only appear in etymological-register texts.

Allophonic Variation of Core Vowels​

Interslavic permits the following allophonic variation in natural speech:

  • /a/: realized uniformly as [a] across all positions.
  • /e/: MAY be realized as [e] or [ɛ]; the more open realization [ɛ] is characteristic of South Slavic speakers. The pronunciation of e is always hard — the preceding consonant is never softened. This contrasts with some northern Slavic languages (notably Russian and Polish) where the native reflex of Proto-Slavic *e softens the preceding consonant in inherited vocabulary while remaining hard in loanwords. Interslavic makes no such distinction: e is uniformly hard in all words regardless of origin.
  • /i/: realized uniformly as [i], similar to the vowel in English "knee" or "beach"; in unstressed syllables MAY reduce toward [ɪ]. Czech speakers should note that the Interslavic i is always the close front [i], not the more centralized [ɪ] that Czech uses for short i and y. Regarding softening of preceding consonants before i, standard Interslavic imposes no prescriptive rules — each speaker MAY soften or not according to their native habits.
  • /o/: the standard realization is [ɔ] (open-mid back rounded vowel), regardless of position in the word. Pronouncing it as a close [o] is not recommended, as it sounds unnatural in a Slavic context.
  • /u/: realized uniformly as [u].
  • /ě/: the core realization is [jɛ], which matches the Ukrainian letter є that represents this phoneme in Cyrillic. The phoneme softens the preceding consonant, but the degree of softening is left to the speaker's preference. For example, sědy (grey-haired) may be pronounced [sjɛdɨ], [sʲɛdɨ], or [ɕɛdɨ]; tělo (body) may be [tjɛlɔ], [tʲɛlɔ], or [t͡sʲɛlɔ]; dělo (affair) may be [djɛlɔ], [dʲɛlɔ], or [dzʲɛlɔ]. However, the extreme Polish-style double palatalization (e.g., [t͡ɕ] for t before ě) is not recommended, as it may be confused with entirely different phonemes and impair intelligibility.
  • /y/: the ideal realization is [ɨ] (as in Russian ы, Polish y, or Ukrainian и). The realization [ɪ] is acceptable but somewhat too close to [i], while [ʏ] (sometimes attributed to certain Russian speakers) is too deep. The German [y] (as in über) is not acceptable — no Slavic language uses this sound. The phoneme y never softens the preceding consonant, which has morphological significance: e.g., dobry → dobrogo (hard declension), but svěži → svěžego (soft declension) — when the orthography is simplified by writing only i, this declension-class distinction is lost. Speakers whose native language does not distinguish /i/ from /y/ MAY merge both to [i].

Notes on usage of etymological consonants:

The core digraphs lj and nj are used where the softness is present in both standard and etymological Interslavic (i.e., recognized across most Slavic languages). The letters ľ, ń are used only where the softness appears exclusively in the etymological register.

Specifically:

  • nj (standard) is used in most positions: e.g., konj (horse), banja (bath);
  • ń (etymological) is used primarily before j: e.g., hođeńje (going), iměńje (estate).
  • rj (standard) appears before vowels: e.g., morje (sea), burja (storm);
  • ŕ (etymological) appears before consonants and word-finally, and also before vowels in inflected forms where the stem-final soft /r/ is preserved: e.g., lěkaŕ (doctor, nom.) → lěkaŕa (gen.).
  • ľ (etymological) appears before j and at morpheme boundaries when a hard stem-final /l/ is softened by a suffix: e.g., siľny (strong) from sila (force); nasiľje (violence).

The etymological consonants ť, ď, ś, ź are written in all positions where the corresponding soft sounds occur (word-finally, before consonants, before j, and before vowels): e.g., gosť (guest), ryś (lynx), gvozď (nail), ťma (darkness).

The acute-accent letters (ť, ď, ś, ź, ŕ) in the etymological Latin orthography replace the Cyrillic soft sign ь, which was originally an ultrashort vowel [ĭ] before becoming a softening marker.

Hard vs. Soft Consonant Distinction​

Like all Slavic languages, Interslavic MUST distinguish between hard (plain) and soft (palatalised) consonants. This distinction is binary and phonologically significant.

Two Sources of Consonant Softness​

The soft consonants lj, nj, and (in the etymological register) ŕ can arise from two historically distinct processes, which have different reflexes across modern Slavic languages:

  1. Iotation-derived softness (Proto-Slavic *lj, *nj, *rj — consonant + *j). This softness is reflected in all modern Slavic languages, both Northern and Southern. Examples: kralj "king" (< *korljь), polje "field" (< *polje), konj "horse" (< *konjь), morje "sea" (< *morje). In standard orthography these are written with the digraphs lj, nj, rj.

  2. Yer-derived softness (Proto-Slavic *lь, *nь, *rь — consonant + front yer). This softness is reflected in Northern Slavic languages (Russian, Polish, Czech, Slovak, etc.) but not in Southern Slavic (Serbian, Croatian, Slovene, etc.), where the consonant remains hard after the yer was lost. Examples: velmi/​veĺmi "very" (< *velьmi; cf. Croatian veoma with hard L), den/​dėń "day" (< *dьnь; cf. Croatian dan with hard N), kamen/​kameń "stone" (< *kamy, gen. *kamene; cf. Croatian kamen with hard N).

In the etymological register (MS Plus), these two types are distinguished orthographically: iotation-derived softness uses the digraphs lj, nj, while yer-derived softness uses the háček letters ĺ (or ľ), ń, ŕ. In standard orthography, yer-derived softness is simply dropped (the consonant is written hard), since Southern Slavic speakers pronounce it hard.

Unresolved: Iotation vs. Yer-Derived Softness

The current Interslavic orthography and dictionary are not yet fully consistent in distinguishing iotation-derived softness (present in all Slavic languages) from yer-derived softness (present only in Northern Slavic). Some entries conflate the two: for instance, bolj appears with the iotation digraph lj, but the word derives from *bolь (yer-softened), and Croatian/Serbian have hard L (bol). The standard form should arguably be bol (hard), with boĺ in MS Plus. Similarly, velmi should be veĺmi in MS Plus rather than veljmi. The athematic masculine nouns (kamenj, korenj, etc.) present the same issue — the final softness is yer-derived, not iotated, so the standard form could end in a hard consonant (kamen) with the MS Plus form using ń (kameń).

The status of rj is additionally ambiguous: unlike lj and nj, the sequence rj is not officially recognized as a phoneme pair by the Commission, yet it functions as one in practice (nearly all occurrences correspond to Proto-Slavic soft *r). Whether rj should be permitted word-finally (e.g., rybarj vs. rybaŕ) remains undecided.

The theoretical framework for this distinction can be stated precisely in terms of Proto-Slavic etymology:

  • Iotated consonants (Proto-Slavic *lj, *nj, *rj — consonant fused with the glide *j) were inherently soft from the earliest reconstructable stage. The softness is independent of any adjacent yer. Examples: *korljь → kralj, *konjь → konj, *morje → morje. Crucially, even in the South Slavic languages — which lost yer-derived softness entirely — iotated softness is preserved: Croatian kralj, konj, more all retain the palatalized consonant.

  • Yer-softened consonants (Proto-Slavic *lь, *nь, *rь, *tь, *dь, *sь, *zь — consonant adjacent to the front yer) acquired their softness from the yer. When the yer was lost in the "fall of the yers" (c. 10th–12th century), the softness persisted in Northern Slavic languages but was lost in most Southern Slavic languages. Examples: *silьnъ → Russian сильный (soft L) but Croatian silan (hard L); *kostь → Russian кость (soft T) but Croatian kost (hard T); *dьnь → Russian день (soft N) but Croatian dan (hard N, with vowel change).

This asymmetry explains a key design property of Interslavic: the standard orthography writes iotated softness explicitly (with digraphs lj, nj, rj) because it is universal across Slavic, but drops yer-derived softness entirely because it would be alien to South Slavic readers. The MS Plus register restores yer-derived softness for the benefit of Northern Slavic readers.

Known dictionary errors arising from conflation of the two types include dėnj (the word for "day"), where the Proto-Slavic form *dьnь indicates yer-softening, not iotation — the correct standard form should be den (hard) with MS Plus dėń, not dėnj. Similarly, myslj ("thought," from *myslь) uses the iotation digraph despite yer-derived softness — cf. Croatian misao with no trace of softness, confirming that the correct standard form should be mysl with MS Plus mysĺ.

Resolving this systematically would require auditing the dictionary to classify each instance of lj, nj, rj by its Proto-Slavic origin and adjusting the standard and MS Plus forms accordingly.

  • Palatal approximant: j /j/

Special status of c /t͡s/: The affricate c [t͡s] is pronounced hard phonetically, but it behaves like a soft consonant morphologically — i.e., it conditions the same morphophonemic alternations that soft consonants trigger. Its voiced counterpart [d͡z] does not occur in Interslavic.

Softening: Softening is the process of adding the feature [+palatal] to a consonant, resulting in a more palatal pronunciation (marked in IPA as [ʲ]). In Interslavic, only lj and nj are mandatory soft consonants; the etymological set adds ť, ď, ś, ź, ŕ.

East Slavic speakers are likely to pronounce soft consonants as softened (palatalised) dental or alveolar consonants [Cʲ]; West Slavic speakers more often as fully palatal consonants [C̟]. Both pronunciations are equally correct, although the East Slavic realization is probably easier to understand for South Slavic speakers.

Soft consonants before vowels: Before i, ě, ę, ė, and ŕ, a hard consonant MAY be softened or palatalised. For example, buditi (to wake) may be pronounced [buditi], [budʲitʲi], or [buɟici].

The affricate pair ć /t͡ɕ/ and đ /d͡ʑ/ deserve special mention: these are the iotated counterparts of t and d respectively, and are of etymological origin (Proto-Slavic *tj and *dj). In standard orthography they are written and pronounced as č and dž; the distinction between ć/č and đ/dž is etymological only.

The MS Plus pronunciation of ć and đ was chosen as the midpoint of Slavic reflexes: [t͡ɕ] and [d͡ʑ], identical to the Serbo-Croatian (Shtokavian) pronunciation. These sounds are close to English "ch" and "j" respectively, but softer — produced with maximally relaxed, lowered jaw position. Unlike the other iotated consonants (*lj, *nj, *rj, whose reflexes are relatively uniform across Slavic), the reflexes of Proto-Slavic *tj and *dj have diverged dramatically: Russian щ/ч and жд/ж, Ukrainian/Belarusian ч and дж, Czech c and z, Polish c and dz, Slovak c/ť and dz/ď, Serbo-Croatian ć and đ, Slovene č/t and j/d, Macedonian ќ/ш/т and ѓ/д, Bulgarian щ/т and жд/д. The standard Interslavic forms č and dž match the Ukrainian and Belarusian reflexes, while ć and đ in MS Plus match the Serbo-Croatian reflexes.

ć and đ form a voiceless–voiced pair and exhibit a high degree of symmetry in their morphophonological behaviour.

The tj and dj Sequences and the Absence of Soft T/D​

It is important to distinguish the etymological soft consonants ť /tʲ/ and ď /dʲ/ — which belong exclusively to the MS Plus register — from the letter sequences tj and dj that appear in certain dictionary words (e.g., bratja, kostjum, tjulenj, adjutant, gospodja, Madjar). Standard Interslavic has no soft /tʲ/ or /dʲ/ phoneme. The core consonant inventory includes only hard /t/ and /d/; their iotated counterparts are the postalveolar affricates č /t͡ʃ/ and dž /d͡ʒ/ (or ć /t͡ɕ/ and đ /d͡ʑ/ in MS Plus), not soft stops.

The sequences tj and dj in the standard orthography are therefore intended as clusters of two separate hard sounds — [t] + [j] and [d] + [j] — not as soft consonants. However, this conflicts with the visual expectation of any Slavic reader, for whom tj and dj naturally suggest softened [tʲ] and [dʲ]. The result is a gap in the phonemic system: the orthography implies sounds that the phonology does not recognise.

Unresolved: Phonemic Status of Soft T/D in the Consonant Inventory

Standard Interslavic lacks soft /tʲ/ and /dʲ/ as phonemes, yet the dictionary contains words with tj and dj sequences that imply them — creating ambiguity about pronunciation and transliteration. Several of these words have natural alternatives that avoid the problem entirely: tjulenj → tulenj (cf. Czech tuleň), tjutjun → tutun or tytun, gospodja → gospodža (cf. Russian госпожа, Serbian госпођа), Madjar → Madžar. Words like adjutant and konjugacija contain morpheme boundaries (ad-jutant, kon-jugacija) where the pronunciation is genuinely [d.j] and [n.j] — two separate sounds — and should be retained as-is. For the MS Plus register, the existing letters ť and ď could be extended to cover borrowed soft /tʲ/ and /dʲ/ in words where no better alternative exists.

See the relevant sectiona (Standard Latin Orthography) and (Etymological Alphabet) for a full discussion of this issue.

Allophonic Variation of Consonants​

The following notes describe the permitted allophonic variation for consonants in standard Interslavic:

  • c /t͡s/: Uniform across Slavic languages. Non-Slavic speakers should note this is always [t͡s], never [k] or [s]. Morphologically, c behaves as a soft consonant — words ending in c follow the soft declension pattern (e.g., Amerikancev, not *Amerikancov).

  • č /t͡ʃ/: MAY be realized anywhere in the range from hard [ʈ͡ʂ] (as in Polish, Slovak, Belarusian, and most South Slavic languages) to softer [t͡ʃ] (as in Czech). Both are acceptable. However, the very soft [t͡ɕ] realization is not recommended in standard Interslavic, as it is reserved for the distinct phoneme ć in the MS Plus register.

  • dž /d͡ʒ/: As the voiced counterpart of č, permitted realizations range from hard [d͡ʐ] to softer [d͡ʒ]. The very soft [d͡ʑ] is not recommended, as it corresponds to đ in MS Plus. In loanwords (e.g., džaz, džungla, džentlemen), speakers should use the harder realization regardless of register.

  • g /g/: Interslavic is a G-Slavic language, meaning the letter g corresponds to h in the H-Slavic languages (Czech, Slovak, Upper Sorbian, Ukrainian). The standard pronunciation is [g]. As a compromise toward H-Slavic speakers, the Belarusian-style voiced velar fricative [ɣ] is also acceptable. However, [h] is NOT acceptable — speakers should not substitute a voiceless glottal or pharyngeal fricative.

Unresolved: The G/H Simplification

Interslavic collapses a complex four-way distinction in the Slavic consonant system into a simple two-phoneme system (g /g/ and h /x/). In reality, the Slavic languages exhibit up to four distinct categories:

  1. Common Slavic g — the inherited velar stop that remains [g] in "G-Slavic" languages (Russian, Polish, Serbian, Bulgarian, Macedonian) but shifted to [ɦ] or [h] in "H-Slavic" languages (Czech, Slovak, Ukrainian, Belarusian, Upper Sorbian). This is the source of the famous doublet Bogdan (G-Slavic) / Bohdan (H-Slavic).
  2. Common Slavic h — the inherited voiceless fricative corresponding to Cyrillic Х. It surfaces as [x] in East and South Slavic but as the digraph ch [x] in West Slavic. In standard Interslavic, this merges with h /x/.
  3. Foreign g — the [g] in loanwords from Latin, Greek, English, etc. This is realized as [g] even in H-Slavic languages: Czech logika, Slovak gravitácia, Ukrainian ґравітація (with the special letter Ґ). Interslavic does not distinguish this from native g.
  4. Foreign h — the [h] in loanwords from Greek, Germanic, and other languages. Its reflexes vary: Latin-script languages write h, East Slavic typically uses Г (with Russian [g] pronunciation), and Cyrillic South Slavic uses Х. Polish further distinguishes h from ch in spelling. Interslavic does not distinguish this from native h.

The two-phoneme simplification is adequate for everyday communication and is consistent with the Serbian model (which also has just [g] and [x]). However, it means that flavorization to H-Slavic languages cannot be fully automated: an algorithm cannot determine whether a given g should become Czech h (native) or remain g (foreign) without access to etymological information not encoded in the standard or MS Plus orthography.

  • l /l/ and lj /lʲ/: These form one of only two mandatory hard–soft consonant pairs in standard Interslavic. Slavic languages use four main realizations of l: "lazy l" [w] (modern Polish ł, also found in Slovenian and Bulgarian), dark [ɫ], neutral [l], and palatal [ʎ]. The [w] realization is acceptable for hard l but not recommended, as it impairs intelligibility. Each speaker should choose a consistent pair of hard and soft variants from the following options: (a) [ɫ] (hard) / [l] (soft) — the historical Polish pattern; (b) [l] (hard) / [ʎ] (soft) — the Shtokavian pattern; (c) [ɫ] (hard) / [ʎ] (soft) — the East Slavic pattern. Whatever pair is chosen, consistency is important: a speaker's hard and soft l must always be distinguishable. Before i, the neutral [l] is natural for most Slavic speakers (e.g., list = [list], not [ɫist]).

  • n /n/ and nj /nʲ/: The second mandatory hard–soft pair. Pronounced [n] and [ɲ] respectively by all Slavic speakers. Before ě, softening of n is always obligatory (e.g., někto is always [ɲɛktɔ]). Before i, softening is optional (e.g., nikto may be [niktɔ] or [ɲiktɔ]). When mandatory softening occurs, the orthography marks it explicitly with j (Latin) or њ (Cyrillic) — e.g., njih is always pronounced [ɲix].

  • r /r/: Always a trilled [r], as in all Slavic languages. The sequence rj, although not recognized by the Commission as a separate phoneme, in practice functions as the equivalent of historical soft /ŕ/ — nearly all occurrences of rj in the lexicon correspond to Proto-Slavic soft /r/ rather than two separate phonemes. This sequence may be pronounced either as two sounds [rja] or as a single softened [rʲa] (e.g., burja = [burja] or [burʲa]). In the standard Cyrillic orthography, this is written рј (not рь, since the standalone soft sign is not part of the standard Cyrillic letter inventory).

  • š /ʃ/: MAY be realized from hard [ʂ] (as in most Slavic languages) to softer [ʃ] (as in Czech). Both are acceptable.

  • v /v/: The standard realization is [v]. The labiodental approximant [ʋ] is also acceptable. The semivowel [w] is not recommended. Importantly, v does NOT lose its voicing in non-final positions — unlike in Polish, where v devoices to [f] after voiceless consonants (e.g., krvav- is [krvav-], not *[krfav-]; tvoj is [tvɔj], not *[tfɔj]).

  • ž /ʒ/: MAY be realized from hard [ʐ] (the majority Slavic pronunciation) to softer [ʒ] (as in Czech). Both are acceptable.
