Basic Clause Structure

This section specifies the structural principles governing simple clauses in Interslavic: canonical constituent order, pragmatic deviations from that order, subject-verb agreement, pro-drop, and the syntax of existential sentences.

Canonical Word Order: SVO​

Interslavic employs Subject – Verb – Object (SVO) as its neutral, pragmatically unmarked word order. This is the preferred order for declarative sentences where no constituent bears special emphasis or discourse prominence.

Otec kupil knigu. — "Father bought a book."

Other orderings (SOV, VSO, OVS, etc.) are syntactically grammatical but pragmatically marked: they signal topic–focus structuring, contrast, or stylistic register. Writers and speakers SHOULD use SVO as the default unless a specific pragmatic effect is intended.

Modifiers (adjectives, demonstratives, genitival attributes) normally precede the noun they modify. This is not an absolute requirement, but pre-nominal placement is the most neutral and unambiguous order.

Word Order and Pragmatic Function​

Because Interslavic uses inflectional case endings to signal grammatical roles, constituent order is available to convey information structure rather than grammatical function.

  • Focus on subject (subject postposed):

Subject-Verb Agreement​

Finite verbs in Interslavic MUST agree with their subject in person and number.

  • Ja čitam. — "I read." (1sg)
  • On čita. — "He reads." (3sg)
  • My čitamo. — "We read." (1pl)

In compound tenses formed with the L-participle (past/conditional), the L-participle MUST agree with the subject in gender and number. The auxiliary byti carries person–number agreement.

SubjectL-participle formExample
Masculine singular-lon kupil
Feminine singular-laona kupila
Neuter singular-loono kupilo
Plural (all genders)-lioni kupili

When the subject is a coordinated NP with heads of mixed gender, the plural L-participle defaults to the masculine plural form -li.

Null/Pro-Drop Subject​

Interslavic is a pro-drop language: an overt subject pronoun is OPTIONAL when the grammatical person and number of the subject are recoverable from the verb's inflectional ending.

  • Čitam knigu. — "I am reading a book." (subject ja omitted)
  • Kupili smo hljěb. — "We bought bread." (subject my omitted)

Overt subject pronouns SHOULD be used:

  • for emphasis: Ja to znam, ne ty. — "I know that, not you."
  • for contrast: On pišet, ona čita. — "He writes, she reads."
  • when disambiguation is needed and the inflectional form is ambiguous (e.g., some archaic or colloquial 1sg/3sg syncretic forms).

Subject pronouns are NOT required in any position; their absence is the stylistically neutral choice in running prose when the referent is clear.

Existential and Presentational Sentences​

Affirmative Existential Sentences​

Existential sentences ("there is / there are") are formed with the copula jest (3sg) or sųt (3pl) followed by a nominative subject NP, typically accompanied by a locative or circumstantial phrase.

  • V městě jest bolnica. — "There is a hospital in the city."
  • Na stolu sųt knigi. — "There are books on the table."

The existential subject is typically indefinite or newly introduced into the discourse (presentational use).

Negative Existential Sentences: Genitive of Negation​

Under sentential negation, the subject (or direct object) of an existential/presentational clause SHOULD shift from nominative (accusative) to genitive. This is the genitive of negation.

  • Affirmative: Jest voda. — "There is water." (nominative)
  • Negative: Nět vody. — "There is no water." (genitive)
  • Affirmative: Sųt studenti. — "There are students."
  • Negative: Nět studentov. — "There are no students."

The negative existential particle nět ("there is no") is regularly used in this construction and does not require a separate form of byti.

Omission of jest in Third-Person Copular Sentences​

The copula jest is frequently omitted in 3rd person present tense copular and existential sentences, particularly in informal or neutral register. This omission is grammatical and common:

  • On (jest) doktor. — "He is a doctor."
  • Ona (jest) zdrava. — "She is healthy."

The omission of jest is not permissible in other persons (1sg jesm, 2sg jesi, etc.) in copular sentences.

Passive Constructions and Alternatives​

The passive voice is grammatically available in Interslavic but SHOULD be used with caution, as its naturalness varies across the Slavic readership.

Analytic Passive​

The analytic passive is formed with byti + past passive participle (PPP, suffix -n-/-t-):

  • Pica je dělana. — "The pizza is being made." (present passive)
  • Pica była dělana. — "The pizza was made." (past passive)

The specific mention of Bulgarian and Macedonian as languages that do not use PPP in present passive constructions is not in the source material. The source states more generally that "for others the past passive participle cannot be used for a present tense construction at all" without naming specific languages.

While grammatically correct, this construction can sound unnatural to Slavic speakers unaccustomed to frequent use of byti in the present tense, or to those whose native languages (e.g., Bulgarian, Macedonian) do not use PPP in present passive constructions in the same way.

Preferred Alternatives to the Passive​

When the agent is unknown or indefinite, two constructions are preferred over the analytic passive:

  1. Third-person plural with null subject ("impersonal active"):
    • Dělajut picu. — "They make pizza / One makes pizza / Pizza is being made."
Unverified Content

The reflexive particle is written as sę here, but the source material (grammar-syntax.md) uses se in the same example: "Pica dělaje se". Verify whether sę or se is the correct standard form.

  1. Reflexive-passive with sę:
    • Pica dělaje sę. — Literally "Pizza makes itself"; translated as "One makes pizza / Pizza is being made."

When the agent is known, a normal active sentence SHOULD be used in preference to the passive.

Purpose (Final) Clauses​

Purpose clauses express the goal or intention of the action in the main clause. They are introduced by the conjunctions že or da followed by the conditional (by + L-participle).

  • Turisti posětet Niderlandiju, že by fotografovali tulipany. "Tourists visit the Netherlands to take photos of the tulips."

  • Turisti posětili Niderlandiju, že by fotografovali tulipany. "Tourists visited the Netherlands to take photos of the tulips."

  • Ona prinosi jabloka, da by děti byli zdrave. "She brings apples so that the kids will be healthy."

  • Ona prinosila jabloka, da by děti byli zdrave. "She brought apples so that the kids would be healthy."

The tense of the main clause (present or past) does not affect the form of the purpose clause: the conditional by + L-participle is used in both cases.

The da + Present Tense Construction​

Da as an Alternative to the Infinitive​

The construction da + present tense is attested in all South Slavic languages and is available in Interslavic as an optional alternative to the infinitive after modal and other auxiliary verbs.

This construction is particularly appropriate when communicating with speakers of Bulgarian and Macedonian, which lack an infinitive entirely. Writers targeting a pan-Slavic audience MAY prefer this construction to increase intelligibility for South Slavic readers.

The verb following da stands in the present tense and agrees with its own subject, regardless of the tense of the governing verb.

Person/Numberda constructionInfinitive construction
1sgja moram da idemja moram idti
2sgty moraš da idešty moraš idti
3sgon mora da ideon mora idti
1plmy moramo da idemomy moramo idti
2plvy morate da idetevy morate idti
3ploni morajųt da idųtoni morajųt idti

The reflexive particle sę in these constructions is placed after da:

  • Ja moram da sę vråtim. — "I must return."

This construction may replace the infinitive in virtually any syntactic environment:

  • To mora da sę zna. — "This must be known."
  • Htěl jesm da kažem. — "I wanted to say."
  • My bųdemo da čitamo. — "We will read."
  • Ne mogl by da prijědeš? — "Couldn't you come?"

Da as a Purposive/Volitional Conjunction​

In South Slavic usage, da also functions as a conjunction meaning "so that / in order that / that (with volitional force)". In this role it introduces a finite subordinate clause in the present tense or conditional, without a following infinitive:

  • Prišel sem da ti kažem. — "I came so that I might tell you." / "I came to tell you."
  • Želim da mi otkryješ istinų. — "I want you to reveal the truth to me."
  • Naši prědki sųt sę boroli da bysmo žyvěli ščęstlivo. — "Our ancestors fought so that we might live happily."

This usage overlaps functionally with the purpose clause construction described in the relevant section (using že/da + conditional). Both patterns are grammatically acceptable; the da + present tense variant is more characteristic of South Slavic style, while že/da + conditional is more pan-Slavic in distribution.
