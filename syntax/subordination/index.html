Subordination

Unverified Content

The majority of this document cannot be verified against the provided source material. The primary source (grammar-conjunctions.md) covers prepositions, not subordinating conjunctions or clause types. The secondary source (grammar-syntax.md) covers basic word order, questions, passive voice, and purpose clauses only. Sections on complement clauses, infinitival complements, tense correlation, conditionals, temporal clauses, concessive/adversative clauses, compound sentences, subordination connectors, and punctuation rules have no corresponding source material and may reflect the document author's synthesis rather than established Interslavic norms.

This section specifies the syntactic rules governing subordinate clauses in Interslavic: finite complement clauses, infinitival (and da-periphrastic) complements, tense correlation, conditional, purposive, temporal, and concessive/adversative constructions. It also covers compound sentences (coordination), the three subordination connectors, and the universal punctuation rule for complex sentences.

Finite Complement Clauses (že/čto Clauses)​

Verbs of saying, thinking, knowing, believing, and perception take finite complement clauses introduced by the subordinating conjunction že or its variant čto ("that"). The embedded verb stands in the indicative mood, in whichever tense is appropriate for the content expressed.

  • On věrit, že zemlja jest okrugla. "He believes that the earth is round."
Unverified Content

The verb form pridu is 1st person singular, but the subject is ona (3rd person). Expected form: pridet or pride.

  • Ona skazala, čto ona ne pridu. "She said that she would not come."

  • My znamo, že on byl tamo. "We know that he was there."

Both že and čto are fully interchangeable as complementizers. Že is more characteristic of a North/West Slavic stylistic register; čto is more characteristic of East Slavic. Either form is normatively correct.

The complementizer may be omitted in informal or stylistically elevated registers, as in some natural Slavic languages:

  • Věrim, on pridet. — "I believe he will come." (complementizer omitted)

Omission of the complementizer is optional; the full form with že or čto is always preferred in formal written usage.

Infinitival Complements​

When the subject of the complement clause is coreferent with the subject of the matrix clause (control), Interslavic strongly prefers the infinitive rather than a finite clause:

  • On hoče idti. — "He wants to go." (not on hoče, da on ide)
  • Ona začela čitati. — "She began to read."
  • My moramo rabotati. — "We must work."

Da + Present Tense as Alternative to Infinitive​

As specified in the relevant section, the construction da + present tense is an optional alternative to the infinitive after modal and auxiliary verbs. The verb following da always stands in the present tense regardless of the tense of the governing verb, and agrees with its own subject:

  • Ja moram idti. — "I must go." — alternatively: Ja moram da idem.

The form kašem appears to be a typo for kažem ("I say"), as shown in the alternative form on the next line.

  • Htěl jesm kašem. — "I wanted to say." — alternatively: Htěl jesm da kažem.

This construction is particularly appropriate in texts addressing South Slavic readers (especially speakers of Bulgarian and Macedonian, which have no infinitive).

Tense Correlation in Embedded Clauses​

Interslavic does not have a strict sequence-of-tense rule comparable to that in English or French. The tense in a subordinate clause is interpreted relative to speech time, not relative to the tense of the matrix verb.

  • On skazal, že ona jest bolna. "He said that she is ill." (present in the embedded clause = still true at speech time)

  • On skazal, že ona była bolna. "He said that she was ill." (past in the embedded clause = illness before speech time)

  • On skazal, že ona bųde bolna. "He said that she will be ill." (future in the embedded clause = illness after speech time)

Writers SHOULD select the tense that accurately describes the time of the embedded event relative to the moment of speaking, not relative to the main clause tense. Backshift (as obligatory in English "he said she was ill" even when still ill) is not required and is not part of the Interslavic norm.

Conditional Clauses​

Interslavic distinguishes three types of conditional constructions depending on the degree of factual commitment.

Real Conditional​

A real (open) conditional expresses a condition that is genuinely open — the speaker does not prejudge whether it holds. The protasis (condition) is introduced by jesli or ako ("if") and takes the indicative mood. The apodosis (consequence) also takes the indicative:

  • Jesli ona pridet, my bųdemo radostni. "If she comes, we will be happy."

  • Ako on ima vrěme, on pomože nam. "If he has time, he will help us."

Both jesli and ako (li) are accepted; jesli is more pan-Slavic in distribution.

Hypothetical (Counterfactual) Conditional​

A hypothetical conditional presents the condition as contrary to fact or unlikely. Both protasis and apodosis use the conditional mood (by + L-participle):

  • Ako byh ja věděl o tym, ne pošel byh tamo. "If I had known about that, I would not have gone there."

  • Dětę ne plakalo by, ako by go ničto ne bolělo. "The child would not be crying if nothing were hurting it."

  • Rusi sę ne žalili by na žiťje, ako by imali lěpšų vlådų. "Russians would not complain about life if they had a better government."

Past Counterfactual Conditional​

A past counterfactual specifies that the condition was not met in the past. It is formed with the past conditional (byl by + L-participle) in both clauses:

  • Ako byh byl prišel, ja byh bil viděl to. "If I had come, I would have seen it."

The past conditional is formed with the L-participle of byti (byl / byla / bylo) combined with the conditional particle by: ja byl byh dělal "I would have done (it)".

The Conjunction ako ne ("unless")​

The conjunction ako ne introduces a negative conditional with the meaning "unless":

  • Pridu, ako ne bųde dožd. "I will come unless it rains."

Purposive Clauses​

Purpose clauses express the goal or intended result of the action in the main clause. Interslavic has two overlapping constructions for this function.

Že by / Da by + Conditional​

The primary construction uses že by or da by followed by the conditional (by + L-participle):

  • Turisti posětet Niderlandiju, že by fotografovali tulipany. "Tourists visit the Netherlands in order to photograph the tulips."

  • Ona prinosi jabloka, da by děti byli zdrave. "She brings apples so that the children will be healthy."

The tense of the main clause (present or past) does not affect the conditional form in the purpose clause.

Da + Present Tense (South Slavic Style)​

As an alternative, particularly in South Slavic register, da + present tense expresses purpose with volitional force:

  • Prišel sem da ti kažem. "I came to tell you." / "I came so that I might tell you."

  • Naši prědki sųt sę boroli da bysmo žyvěli ščęstlivo. "Our ancestors fought so that we might live happily."

Unverified Content

The use of aby as a purposive subordinator is not attested in the provided source material. The source only covers že by and da by for purpose clauses.

Aby as a Purposive Subordinator​

The conjunction aby ("in order to") is also available as a purposive subordinator and is common in West Slavic stylistic register:

  • Prišel jesm, aby pomogti. "I came in order to help."

Temporal Clauses​

Temporal subordinate clauses specify the time relation between the embedded event and the main clause event. The following subordinating conjunctions introduce temporal clauses:

Examples:

  • Kogda on pridet, my začnemo. "When he comes, we will begin."

  • Dokolě solnce světit, rabotajmo. "As long as the sun shines, let us work."

  • Čekajte, dopoka ne pridu. "Wait until I come."

Aspect and tense in temporal clauses: When the main clause verb is in the future tense, the temporal clause verb is typically in the perfective present (which expresses a completed future event) rather than in an analytic future form. This mirrors the pattern in natural Slavic languages:

  • Kogda on pride (pf. pres.), my pojdemo. — "When he comes, we will go." (not kogda on bųde priti)

Concessive and Adversative Clauses​

Concessive Clauses​

Concessive clauses express a circumstance that is acknowledged but does not prevent the main clause from being true. The primary concessive conjunctions are:

ConjunctionMeaning
hotě or hoti"although, though, even though"
ače"although, though"
vsěako"nevertheless, all the same" (concessive adverb)

  • Hotě on byl ustalen, prodolžal rabotati. "Although he was tired, he continued working."

  • Ače jest zima, děti igrajut vně. "Although it is winter, the children are playing outside."

The concessive clause typically precedes the main clause, though it may follow. No special verb form is required in either clause: both take the indicative appropriate to their time reference.

Adversative Clauses​

Adversative constructions contrast two propositions. The primary adversative conjunction is no ("but"), which connects clauses of equal weight:

  • On hoče idti, no ona hoče ostati. "He wants to go, but she wants to stay."

The conjunction a expresses a milder contrast or shift of topic ("and, but, while"):

  • On pišet, a ona čita. "He writes, while she reads."

The conjunction jednako ("however, nevertheless") introduces an adversative main clause after a concessive subordinate clause:

  • Hotě jest utomlen, jednako prodolžajet. "Although he is tired, he continues nonetheless."

Compound Sentences (Coordination)​

A sentence is compound (složnosočinjenoje) when its constituent clauses are independent of one another — neither depends on the other for its meaning, and each carries equal informational weight. Compound sentences are formed by placing a coordinating conjunction or particle between the clauses.

The main coordinating conjunctions are:

TypeConjunctionMeaning
Copulativei"and"
Copulative (negative)ni … ni …"neither … nor …"
Disjunctiveili"or"
Disjunctive (logical)i ili"and/or"
Adversativeale, no"but"
Causalbo, zatože"because, for"
Final/volitionalda"so that, let"
Implicativeako … potom … inako …"if … then … otherwise …"

Examples:

  • Včera je byla velika zima, i my jesmo ostali doma. "Yesterday it was very cold, and we stayed at home."

  • Budete li letěti (samoletom), ili hočete idti autom? "Will you fly (by plane), or do you want to go by car?"

  • Jesmo se učili Esperanto, ale no byl to znajemy jezyk. "We studied Esperanto, but it was not a well-known language."

  • Hočemo, da imate dobro vrěme! "We want you to have good weather!"

  • Ako bude dobro vrěme, potom idemo do prirody. "If the weather is good, then we will go to the countryside."

note

In Slavic languages the interrogative particle ako (or its equivalent) is frequently replaced by a verb in interrogative form. For example: bude li dobro vrěme ("will the weather be good?"). This inverted form carries the same conditional meaning as the particle ako.

Complex Sentences: The Three Subordination Connectors​

A sentence is complex (složnopodčinjenoje) when one clause (the subordinate clause) depends semantically on another (the main clause). The subordinate clause cannot stand alone without the elements of the main clause that signal the dependency.

In Interslavic, a subordinate clause within a complex sentence always opens with a special grammatical (referential) element that signals the relationship of the subordinate clause to the corresponding main clause. There are three such connectors:

The Conjunction že ("that")​

The conjunction že introduces a subordinate clause that as a whole refers back to the verbal predicate of the main clause. The entire subordinate clause functions as a complement of that predicate.

  • Govorju, že to ne jest dobro. "I am saying that this is not good." (govorju ⇐ že)

The Relative Pronoun iže and Its Paradigm​

The relative pronoun iže (and its gender/case forms) introduces a subordinate clause that refers back to a noun, numeral, or pronoun in the main clause. It signals that the subordinate clause is a relative (adjectival) clause modifying that nominal element. (For the full paradigm see the Semantics chapter.)

  • Kto jest tamtoj člověk, ktory imaje zeleno auto? "Who is that person who has a green car?" (člověk ⇐ ktory)

  • Ne hoču auto, v ktorom jest slaby motor. "I don't want a car in which there is a weak engine." (auto ⇐ ktorom)

  • Moj prijatelj, jegože mlada žena tamo ide, jest bolestny. "My friend, whose young wife is going there, is ill." (prijatelj ⇐ jegože)

Interrogative Pronouns and Adverbs as Subordinators​

Any interrogative pronoun (ktory, koliko, …), numeral, or adverb (kogda, …) may serve as a subordinating connector. It refers back to a noun, numeral, adverb, or adverbial expression in the main clause.

  • Ne znaju točno, koliko imajemo ljudij. "I don't know exactly how many people we have." (točno ⇐ koliko)

  • Idemo do prirody, kogda je dobro vrěme. "We go to the countryside when the weather is good." (do prirody ⇐ kogda)

Punctuation: The Comma Rule​

Since Interslavic is a constructed language, it has no single rigid punctuation tradition. Writers MAY follow the comma conventions of their native language. However, one simple rule is universally applicable and is RECOMMENDED for use in all Interslavic writing:

Each clause of a complex sentence that has its own predicate — together with its own subject, objects, and other elements — MUST be separated from the adjacent clause by a comma.

This rule applies throughout all examples in Merunka's handbook and is used consistently in formal Interslavic prose.

One comma (two-clause sentence):

  • Idemo do prirody, kogda bude dobro vrěme. "We will go to the countryside when the weather is good."

    PartClause
    First clauseIdemo do prirody.
    Second clauseKogda bude dobro vrěme.

Two commas (relative clause embedded within main clause):

  • Moj prijatelj, jegože mlada žena tamo ide, jest bolestny. "My friend, whose young wife is going there, is ill."

    PartClause
    First clauseMoj prijatelj jest bolestny.
    Embedded clauseJegože mlada žena tamo ide.

The embedded relative clause is surrounded by commas on both sides because it interrupts the main clause.

Cross-reference

This comma rule applies equally to relative clauses , complement clauses , conditional clauses , temporal clauses , purposive clauses , and concessive clauses . It supersedes any more permissive practice from the writer's native-language orthography when the goal is maximal clarity.
