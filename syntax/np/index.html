Noun Phrase Structure

Unverified Content

The primary source material for syntax (Jan van Steenbergen's grammar-syntax) contains only a brief note that "modifiers usually precede the noun" and that this is "the most neutral and the clearest way." The vast majority of this document — including canonical modifier ordering, numeral case government, apposition rules, possessive adjective distribution, agreeing/non-agreeing modifier theory, sentence-to-NP transformations, and capitalization rules — has no basis in the provided source material and appears to be editorial elaboration.

This section specifies the internal structure of noun phrases (NPs) in Interslavic: the canonical ordering of pre-nominal modifiers, the use of demonstratives as quasi-definiteness markers, the case government of numeral phrases, the syntax of appositives, the distribution of genitive possession versus possessive adjectives, and the distinction between agreeing and non-agreeing modifiers, including the rules for transforming full sentences into NP constituents.

Adjective Placement​

Attributive adjectives in Interslavic NORMALLY precede the noun they modify. Pre-nominal placement is the neutral, pragmatically unmarked position.

  • dobry čověk — "a good person"
  • stara kniga — "an old book"
Unverified Content

The form vysoko derevo appears incorrect: vysoko is the adverb form (see the relevant section), while the neuter nominative adjective should be vysoke derevo. This contradicts the agreement rule stated in this section.

  • vysoko derevo — "a tall tree"

Postpositive (post-nominal) adjectives are syntactically grammatical but stylistically marked: they occur in poetic, archaic, or emphatic registers, or in fixed nominal phrases and titles.

  • car slavny — "the glorious tsar" (elevated or poetic register)

Writers SHOULD use pre-nominal placement in neutral prose. Post-nominal placement MAY be used when a deliberate stylistic or emphatic effect is intended.

Canonical Modifier Order within the NP​

Unverified Content

The canonical modifier ordering (Demonstrative > Possessive > Ordinal > Adjective > Noun) and the MUST requirement for full gender/number/case agreement are not addressed in the source material. This ordering appears to be editorial inference from general Slavic typology.

When a noun is modified by more than one pre-nominal element, the elements SHOULD appear in the following canonical order:

Demonstrative > Possessive > Ordinal numeral > Adjective(s) > Noun

SlotCategoryExample
1Demonstrativetoj, ta, to
2Possessivemoj, jeji, našego
3Ordinal numeralpervy, vtory
4Adjective(s)dobry, veliky
5Head nounčověk, kniga

Example with multiple slots filled:

  • toj moj pervy stary drug "that old first friend of mine"

All pre-nominal modifiers MUST agree with the head noun in gender, number, and case.

Demonstratives as Quasi-Definiteness Markers​

Interslavic has no grammatical definite or indefinite article. The demonstrative pronoun toj / ta / to MAY be used to signal definiteness — specifically, anaphoric reference to an entity already established in the discourse, or reference to a uniquely identifiable entity.

  • Anaphoric use: Videl jesm muža. Toj muž był vysok. "I saw a man. The man was tall."

  • Identifying use: Toj gorod jest stolica strani. "That/The city is the capital of the country."

This use of toj / ta / to is pragmatically optional and is not grammatically obligatory. Its use is RECOMMENDED where disambiguation between definite and indefinite reference is communicatively important, but its absence does not produce an ungrammatical sentence.

The demonstrative retains its full morphological agreement (gender, number, case) with the head noun in all uses (see for paradigms).

Numeral Phrases​

Unverified Content

The entire numeral phrases section , including the distinction between numerals 1–4 (agreement) and 5+ (genitive plural government), and the neuter singular verbal agreement rule, has no basis in the provided source material.

Cardinal Numerals 1–4​

Cardinal numerals 1–4 and all compound numerals ending in 1–4 govern agreement: the numeral and the noun agree in case. The noun stands in the nominative (or the case appropriate to its syntactic function), and the numeral precedes the noun.

NumeralExampleGloss
1jedin čověk"one person"
2dva muža"two men" (nom/acc)
3tri ženy"three women"
4četyri dny"four days"

For numerals 2–4, the noun takes the genitive singular when the numeral phrase is in nominative or accusative position.

Cardinal Numerals 5 and Above​

Cardinal numerals 5 and above govern genitive plural of the noun when the numeral phrase occupies nominative or accusative position. The entire NP is treated as a neuter singular for the purposes of verbal agreement in such position.

  • pět čověkov — "five people" (genitive plural)
  • deset knig — "ten books" (genitive plural)
  • Pět studentov prišlo. — "Five students came." (neuter singular verb)

In other syntactic cases (genitive, dative, instrumental, locative), the numeral agrees with the noun in the appropriate case, and the noun stands in the plural of the relevant case.

  • s pětimi čověkami — "with five people" (instrumental plural)

Apposition​

Unverified Content

The apposition rules, including the MUST requirement for case agreement and the SHOULD preference for declining fixed proper names, are not addressed in the source material.

An appositive is a noun phrase that immediately follows and further identifies or renames its head noun. Appositives in Interslavic MUST agree in case with the noun they modify. They also typically agree in number; agreement in gender is not required when the appositive belongs to a different inherent-gender class than the head.

  • gorod Varšava — "the city of Warsaw" (both nominative)
  • v gorode Varšavě — "in the city of Warsaw" (both locative)
  • s profesorom Kovalom — "with Professor Koval" (both instrumental)

Personal titles and professional designations preceding a proper name follow the same rule: both title and name decline in case.

  • Nominative: doktor Novak
  • Genitive: doktora Novaka
  • Dative: doktoru Novaku

Appositives that are fixed proper names (names of streets, institutions, brands) which are morphologically uninflectable in a given language variant MAY be left undeclined; however, declining them is preferred and SHOULD be done whenever the morphological paradigm permits.

Genitive Possession versus Possessive Adjective​

Unverified Content

The distinction between possessive adjective and genitive NP strategies, including the SHOULD preference for possessive adjectives with simple possessors, is not addressed in the source material.

Interslavic offers two strategies for expressing possession within an NP. Their distribution is governed by the morphological status of the possessor.

Possessive Adjective (for simple possessors)​

When the possessor is a single uninflected noun (a personal name or a simple common noun), a possessive adjective derived from that noun SHOULD be used. Possessive adjectives are formed with the suffixes -ov/-ev (masculine/neuter possessors) and -in (feminine possessors), as specified in the relevant section

The possessive adjective precedes the possessed noun and agrees with it in gender, number, and case.

  • bratov dom — "brother's house" (masc. possessor → -ov)
  • materin golos — "mother's voice" (fem. possessor → -in)
  • Ivanovo pismo — "Ivan's letter" (proper name → -ov/-o-)

Genitive NP (for complex or inflected possessors)​

When the possessor is a pronoun, a modified NP, a proper name already bearing a modifier, or any structure that cannot felicitously form a possessive adjective, the possessor is expressed as a genitive NP following the possessed noun.

  • kniga togo čověka — "that man's book" (modified NP)
Unverified Content

The form mogo does not match the standard ISV possessive pronoun paradigm. The genitive masculine form is mojego (see pronoun paradigms). This may be an error.

  • dom mogo starogo brata — "my old brother's house"
  • stolica Francii — "the capital of France" (geographical proper name)

The genitive possessor follows the possessed head noun. It does not agree with the head but stands in the genitive case regardless of the case of the head noun phrase as a whole.

Competing Constructions​

Both strategies are available for simple personal names; the possessive adjective is preferred in neutral prose for personal names.

  • Preferred: Mariin syn — "Maria's son"
  • Also grammatical: syn Marii — "the son of Maria"

Agreeing and Non-Agreeing Modifiers​

Interslavic distinguishes two structural types of nominal modifier: agreeing modifiers (soglasoványje oprědělenja) and non-agreeing modifiers (nesoglasoványje oprědělenja). Both types can co-occur within the same NP.

Agreeing Modifiers​

An agreeing modifier corresponds in gender, number, and case to the noun (or pronoun, or numeral) it modifies, and inflects together with it as the NP changes case. Agreeing modifiers are expressed by adjectives and adjectival pronouns. They are normally placed before the head noun (see the relevant section).

The modifier inflects in lockstep with the head:

CaseExampleGloss
Nominativedobry čověk"a good person"
Genitivedobrogo čověka"of a good person"
Dativedobromu čověku"to a good person"

Non-Agreeing Modifiers​

A non-agreeing modifier does not change its form when the head noun changes gender, number, or case. It retains its own fixed grammatical form regardless of the case of the NP it belongs to. Non-agreeing modifiers are typically expressed by:

  • a noun (or pronoun) in the genitive case, often with a preposition — positioned after the head noun;
  • less commonly, by another prepositional phrase.

The canonical example is a noun phrase with the preposition iz ("from"):

Case of NPExampleGloss
Nominativečověk (N.) iz Ameriky (G.)"a person from America"
Genitivečověka (G.) iz Ameriky (G.)"of a person from America"
Dativečověku (D.) iz Ameriky (G.)"to a person from America"

The modifier iz Ameriky remains invariantly in the genitive throughout all cases of the head NP. The head noun čověk inflects normally; the non-agreeing modifier does not.

Combining Agreeing and Non-Agreeing Modifiers​

A single head noun may carry both an agreeing and a non-agreeing modifier simultaneously. In such constructions, the agreeing modifier inflects with the head while the non-agreeing modifier remains fixed:

  • dobromu čověku iz Ameriky "to a good person from America" — dobromu (D.) agrees with čověku (D.); iz Ameriky remains in the genitive throughout.

Sentence-to-NP Transformations​

Unverified Content

The entire sentence-to-NP transformation section , including the three transformation strategies (byti-predicate, active participle, and gerund), is not addressed in the source material.

In complex noun phrases, a full sentence can be condensed into a single NP constituent. Interslavic inherits from its Slavic base three productive transformation strategies. All three rest on the agreeing/non-agreeing distinction described in the relevant section

Transformation of Subject Sentences with byti​

When a sentence whose predicate is byti ("to be") is compressed into a modifier, the components undergo the following mapping:

  • The subject (nominative) → non-agreeing modifier: preposition od + genitive (ablative/separative construction).
  • Other non-nominative constituents → non-agreeing modifiers in their original case or with a preposition.

Passive sentences follow the same rule. The sole difference is that the agent (expressed by the instrumental in the passive clause) maps to a non-agreeing modifier with od + genitive.

Example (passive):

Slovo jest pisano Bogom (I.) → Slovo od Boga (G.) "The word is written by God" → "the word of God"

Example (active byti sentences):

Three source sentences:

  1. Muž jest dobry. — "The man is good."
  2. Toj muž jest iz Ameriky. — "That man is from America."
  3. Vidimo tu togo muža. — "We can see that man here."

Sentences 1 and 2 compress into modifiers of sentence 3:

Vidimo tu dobrogo muža iz Ameriky. "We can see the good man from America here."

The nominative subject muž of sentence 1 supplied the agreeing adjective dobry → dobrogo; the predicate iz Ameriky of sentence 2 supplied the non-agreeing modifier iz Ameriky.

Transformation Using the Active Participle​

When a sentence with a non-byti verb predicate is compressed into a modifier of another sentence, the verbal predicate is transformed into an active present participle (agreeing modifier), while the remaining clause constituents become non-agreeing modifiers.

Example:

Source sentences:

  1. Žena piše pismo. — "The woman is writing a letter."
  2. Ta žena sedi doma. — "That woman is sitting at home."

Sentence 1 is compressed into a modifier of sentence 2:

Žena pišuča pismo sedi doma. "The woman writing a letter is sitting at home."

The verb piše → active participle pišuča (agreeing with žena); the object pismo remains as a non-agreeing accusative constituent within the expanded NP.

Gerund Transformation (Full Sentence → NP)​

When an entire sentence must be nominalized into a single NP (for use as subject, object, or in another nominal position), the verbal predicate is transformed into a gerund (verbal noun), and all remaining constituents become non-agreeing modifiers. The procedure is:

  1. The subject (nominative) → non-agreeing modifier: od + genitive.
  2. The direct object (accusative) → non-agreeing modifier: genitive (without preposition).
  3. Adverbs → agreeing adjectives modifying the gerund.

Example:

Source sentence: Moj brat dobro rysuje naš dom. "My brother draws our house well."

Source constituentCase→ Transformed form
moj brat (subject, N.)Nominative→ od mojego brata (G.)
dobro (adverb)—→ dobro (adj. modifying gerund)
rysuje (verb)—→ rysovanje (gerund, N.)
naš dom (object, A.)Accusative→ našego doma (G.)

Result:

Dobro rysovanje našego doma od mojego brata (lit. "Good drawing of our house by my brother")

Multiple Modifiers​

A single head noun may carry more than one modifier — agreeing or non-agreeing — simultaneously. This is not a separate syntactic construction but a direct consequence of the relevant section and : each modifier independently satisfies its own agreement or non-agreement requirements while all depend on the same head.

Example:

Kto jest toj filosof učitelj naš? "Who is that philosopher and our teacher?"

Here the single head has multiple nominal predicates; the same principle allows multiple pre- and post-nominal modifiers to stack on one noun.

Capitalization of Proper Names​

Unverified Content

The capitalization rules, including the MUST requirement for unique entities, are not addressed in the source material.

Since Interslavic is a constructed language, it has no single rigid orthographic tradition for capitalization. Writers MAY follow the conventions of their own native language. However, one simple and universally applicable rule is RECOMMENDED:

The name of any unique or one-of-a-kind entity MUST be written with a capital letter.

Examples:

ExampleGloss
Slovačska republika"Slovak Republic"
Europska unija"European Union"
Naum Ohridsky"Naum of Ohrid" (Ohrid being a unique proper name)

This rule ensures that references to geographically, institutionally, or personally unique entities are consistently distinguished in writing.
