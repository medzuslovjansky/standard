Syntax

This section specifies the syntactic rules of Interslavic: the principles that govern how words combine into phrases, clauses, and sentences. The rules defined here apply to all standard written and spoken Interslavic regardless of flavorizational register.

Core Structural Properties​

Interslavic exhibits the following typological properties, which together determine the overall shape of its syntax:

  1. Morphologically rich inflection. Grammatical relations (subject, object, indirect object, etc.) are expressed by case endings on nouns, pronouns, adjectives, and numerals. Word order is therefore available to express discourse-level functions (topic, focus, contrast) rather than grammatical functions.

  2. Neutral word order: SVO. Subject–Verb–Object is the pragmatically unmarked default. Other orderings are grammatical but discourse-marked (see the relevant section).

  3. Pro-drop. Overt subject pronouns are optional when recoverable from verb morphology (see the relevant section).

  4. Prepositional phrases and case government. Prepositions govern specific cases. The case selected by a preposition depends on the preposition's lexical identity and, for spatial prepositions, on whether motion or static location is expressed (see the relevant section).

  5. No articles. Interslavic has no grammatical articles. Definiteness MAY be indicated pragmatically by the demonstrative toj / та / то (тoj / та / то), but this is not obligatory (see the relevant section).

Relationship Between Syntax and Morphology​

The syntactic rules in this chapter presuppose the morphological forms specified in the Morphology chapter. In particular:

  • Case assignment rules reference the seven-case system described in the relevant section
  • Verbal agreement rules reference the person/number paradigms in the relevant section
  • Coordination agreement references the gender system in the relevant section

Normative Status​

All rules in this chapter that use the modal verbs MUST, SHALL, SHOULD, MAY, and SHOULD NOT carry the following normative force:

  • MUST / SHALL — an absolute grammatical requirement; violation produces an ungrammatical sentence.
  • SHOULD — the strongly recommended default; deviation is grammatical but stylistically marked.
  • MAY — an explicitly permitted option; neither preferred nor dispreferred.
  • SHOULD NOT — a usage that is grammatical but actively discouraged as stylistically poor or cross-dialectally opaque.
