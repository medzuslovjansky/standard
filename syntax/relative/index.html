Relative Clauses

The source material covers relative pronoun forms (ktory, koj, iže) and their inflection (in the pronouns section), but does not address relative clause syntax, gap vs. resumptive pronoun strategies, long-distance extraction, or free relative clause structure. Most of the syntactic analysis in this file is likely AI-generated and should be reviewed by the committee.

This section specifies the structure, pronoun selection, inflection, and extraction strategies for relative clauses in Interslavic, including headed relative clauses, the gap vs. resumptive pronoun alternation, animacy effects, and free (headless) relative clauses.

Headed Relative Clauses with ktory​

The primary relative pronoun in Interslavic is ktory ("who, which, that"). It introduces headed relative clauses — clauses that modify an explicit antecedent noun phrase.

Ktory agrees with the antecedent in gender and number, and takes the case required by its grammatical function within the relative clause (subject, direct object, indirect object, etc.). It is inflected exactly like an ordinary hard-stem adjective.

The koj Alternative (Southern Flavorization)​

The pronoun koj ("which") is the South Slavic variant of the relative pronoun. It is inflected like the possessive pronoun moj (soft-stem adjective paradigm) and is used in precisely the same syntactic construction as ktory.

Koj and ktory are fully interchangeable as relative pronouns; their meanings are identical. The choice between them is a matter of stylistic register or dialect orientation:

  • ktory — pan-Slavic, appropriate in all contexts and registers
  • koj — South Slavic flavor; appropriate when targeting a South Slavic readership or for stylistic variation

Examples with koj:

  • žena, koja čita — "a woman who reads"
  • děte, koje ja viděl — "the child that I saw"
  • muž, kojemu dah knigu — "the man to whom I gave the book"

In all cases where koj appears, ktory may be substituted, and vice versa.

The Archaic Pronoun iže​

A third, more archaic relative pronoun iže is also available, primarily in elevated or literary style. Its declension is irregular:

  • Nominative (all genders, both numbers): iže
  • Other cases: inflected like the corresponding forms of the personal pronoun on / ona / ono with the suffix -že appended:
    • Masculine genitive singular: jegože
    • Masculine dative singular: jemuže
    • Feminine genitive singular: jejže
    • etc.

Iže is not recommended for ordinary prose; it is retained here for recognition purposes and for writers adopting a Church Slavonic-influenced register.

Gap Strategy (No Resumptive Pronoun)​

Interslavic relative clauses normally employ the gap strategy: the position from which the relative pronoun has been extracted is left empty (a syntactic gap). No resumptive pronoun is inserted in the extraction site.

The relative pronoun itself, appearing at the head of the relative clause, bears the case that corresponds to its grammatical role in the embedded clause. This is what makes the case inflection of ktory/koj syntactically crucial.

Correct (gap strategy):

  • Muž, ktorogo [∅] ja ljubim, jest doktor. "The man whom I love is a doctor." (ktorogo = genitive/accusative of animate masculine; gap in object position)

Incorrect (resumptive pronoun — not used in standard Interslavic):

  • Muž, ktory ja jeho ljubim, jest doktor. (non-standard)

The absence of resumptive pronouns in canonical relative clauses is a structural requirement of standard Interslavic, not merely a preference.

Resumptive Pronoun in Long-Distance Extraction​

In cases of deeply embedded extraction — where the gap would be separated from the relative pronoun by one or more clause boundaries — a resumptive pronoun MAY appear in the extraction site. This is a licensed exception to the gap strategy specified in the relevant section

Long-distance extraction arises when the antecedent is relativized out of an embedded complement clause:

  • Muž, ktory ona skazala, čto ona jemu věrit, jest tamo. "The man whom she said she trusts is there." (resumptive jemu in the dative position within the embedded clause)

The use of a resumptive pronoun in such contexts reduces processing difficulty and is grammatically acceptable. Writers SHOULD use a resumptive pronoun when the extraction site is more than one clause boundary away from the relative pronoun.

Animacy Effects in Relative Pronoun Selection​

The accusative form of the relative pronoun ktory follows the animacy distinction that applies to all masculine adjective-like forms in Interslavic:

  • Inanimate masculine singular accusative = same as nominative: ktory
  • Animate masculine singular accusative = same as genitive: ktorogo
AnimacyAntecedentRelative pronoun (accusative)Example
Inanimatestol "table" (m.)ktorystol, ktory ja viděl — "the table that I saw"
Animatemuž "man" (m.)ktorogomuž, ktorogo ja viděl — "the man whom I saw"
Femininežena "woman" (f.)ktorųžena, ktorų ja viděl — "the woman whom I saw"
Neuterděte "child" (n.)ktoreděte, ktore ja viděl — "the child that I saw"

The same animacy rule applies to koj in its accusative forms.

Free (Headless) Relative Clauses​

A free relative clause (also called a headless relative clause) is a relative clause that lacks an explicit antecedent noun. The relative pronoun itself incorporates both the role of "head" and "relativizer". Interslavic forms free relative clauses with the pronouns kto ("whoever"), čto ("whatever"), and ktory / koj ("whichever") used without an overt antecedent NP:

  • Kto žela, može priti. "Whoever wishes may come."

  • Čto on skazal, jest pravda. "What he said is true." / "Whatever he said is true."

  • Ktory pridet pervym, dostanet nagradu. "Whichever (one) comes first will receive a prize."

In free relative clauses with kto and čto, the pronoun takes the case required by its function in the matrix clause (since there is no antecedent NP to whose case it must agree). When kto or čto also has a syntactic role within the embedded clause, the case of the embedded function takes precedence, and the matrix function is supplied by the clause as a whole.

  • Ja dam knigu tomu, komu ona nužna jest. "I will give the book to whoever needs it." (demonstrative tomu serves as matrix indirect object; komu = dative in embedded clause)
