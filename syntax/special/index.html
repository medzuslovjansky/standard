Special Clause Types

This section specifies the syntax of special clause types in Interslavic that have distinctive structural properties: imperatives (direct commands, hortatives, and optatives); impersonal constructions (meteorological predicates, dative-experiencer constructions, modal impersonals); reflexive constructions and their three functions; and complement control (subject-control and object-control verbs).

Imperatives​

Unverified Content

The entire (Imperatives) section — including imperative formation rules, paradigm tables, consonant alternation rules, irregular imperatives, hortative, and optative constructions — has no basis in the primary source material (grammar-syntax.md), which does not address imperative morphology or syntax.

The imperative mood is used to express direct commands, requests, invitations, and prohibitions. Interslavic imperatives are morphologically formed for three person–number combinations and syntactically may be supplemented by a third-person optative construction using nehaj.

Direct 2nd-Person Imperative​

The 2nd-person imperative is the canonical form of the direct command, addressed to the person(s) the speaker is speaking to directly.

Formation: The imperative stem is derived from the 3rd person singular present tense form (in the traditional/non-contracted conjugation). If the stem ends in the glide -j-, the imperative endings attach directly; otherwise, the linking vowel -i- is inserted.

dělajeтdělaj-dělajdo!
umějeтuměj-umějknow how!
věrujeтvěruj-věrujbelieve!
neseтnes-nesicarry!
postaviтpostavi-postaviput!
pijeтpij-pijdrink!
myjeтmyj-myjwash!

Consonant alternations before the linking vowel -i-: When the linking vowel -i- is added to a stem ending in a velar consonant, First Palatalization applies: k → č, g → ž, h → š. (These alternations apply because the imperative stem is taken from the 3sg, which has already undergone the vowel -e- alternation, but the palatal glide trigger in the 2sg ending conditions palatalization of the preceding consonant.)

pomožeт → pomoži — "help!" izrěčeт → izrěči — "pronounce! utter!"

Verbs in -avati: Verbs with the suffix -va- in the infinitive form their imperative from the infinitive stem with the linking glide -j- rather than from the present stem:

davati → davaj — "give! (habitual)" sòzdavati → sòzdavaj — "create! (habitual)"

Full paradigm of the imperative (three forms):

The Interslavic imperative has three forms: 2nd person singular, 1st person plural (hortative), and 2nd person plural (plural command or polite singular command). The plural and 1st-plural forms are built by adding -mo and -te respectively to the 2sg stem:

datibytidavativěritivěrovati
2sgdajbųďdavajvěrivěruj
1pldajmobųďmodavajmověrimověrujmo
2pldajtebųďtedavajtevěritevěrujte

Negation of the imperative: Negative commands (prohibitions) are formed by placing ne before the imperative form:

Ne dělaj tego! — "Don't do that!" Ne bųďte glupi! — "Don't be foolish!"

Pragmatic note: The direct 2nd-person imperative is used for direct, unhedged commands and requests. For requests that require politeness, speakers may prefer the conditional (by + L-participle) or phrases with modal verbs such as možete li "could you".

Hortative 1st Plural​

The 1st-person plural imperative (hortative) expresses an inclusive invitation or proposal — "Let's (all) do X" — addressing both the speaker and the interlocutor(s).

The form is constructed by adding -mo to the 2sg imperative stem:

Idemo! — "Let's go!" Dělajmo! — "Let's do (it)!" Bųďmo veseli! — "Let's be merry!" Pijmo za zdorovje! — "Let's drink to health!"

The hortative is an inclusive form: it necessarily includes the speaker. For an exclusive directive to a group not including the speaker, the 2nd-person plural imperative (-te form) is used instead.

3rd-Person Optative: Nehaj + Indicative​

For 3rd-person commands, wishes, and optative expressions ("let him/her/it/them do X"), Interslavic uses the construction nehaj followed by the verb in the 3rd-person indicative (present or past as appropriate).

Nehaj on pride! — "Let him come!" Nehaj ona čita. — "Let her read." Nehaj bųde světlo! — "Let there be light!" Nehaj oni sami rěšajut. — "Let them decide for themselves."

The particle nehaj is invariable. It may also be used with 1st and 2nd person verbs for stronger optative or permissive nuance, though the imperative forms are more usual in those persons.

Impersonal Constructions​

Unverified Content

The entire (Impersonal Constructions) section — including weather verbs, dative-experiencer constructions, and modal impersonals — has no basis in the primary source material (grammar-syntax.md), which does not discuss impersonal constructions.

Impersonal constructions are clauses that lack a nominative subject. In Interslavic, impersonal constructions appear in several structural types.

Weather Verbs and Meteorological Predicates​

Meteorological events and natural phenomena are expressed by 3rd-person singular verbs with no overt subject:

Sněži. — "It snows / It is snowing." Doždi. — "It rains / It is raining." Grmi. — "It thunders." Světaje sę. — "It is dawning."

No dummy subject pronoun is inserted. The verb appears in the default 3sg form.

Dative-Experiencer Constructions​

Many psychological states and physical sensations are expressed impersonally with a dative experiencer (the person who undergoes the state, in the dative case) and no nominative subject:

Mně jest holodno. — "I am cold." (lit. "To me it is cold.")

Unverified Content

The form Tebu is suspect. The expected ISV dative of ty is tebě (or tebi). The form Tebu does not follow standard ISV pronoun declension patterns.

Tebu jest žarko. — "You are hot." (lit. "To you it is hot.") Jemu jest skučno. — "He is bored." Nam jest veselo. — "We are merry." Mně sę hočet spati. — "I feel like sleeping." (lit. "To me it wants to sleep.")

The predicate in these constructions is an invariable adverbial/predicative form (often ending in -o) or an impersonal verb. The copula jest may be omitted in present-tense constructions (see the relevant section).

Modal Impersonals​

Impersonal modal predicates express necessity, possibility, or permission without a nominative subject. The logical subject of the modal appears in the dative case, and the modal predicate is invariable:

Možno jest idti. — "It is possible to go / One may go." Trěba jest dělati. — "It is necessary to do / One must do." Nělza idti. — "It is not permitted to go / One must not go." Nam trěba idti. — "We need to go." (dative logical subject nam)

The copula jest is frequently omitted: Možno idti.

Reflexive Constructions​

The reflexive particle sę (accusative reflexive pronoun, enclitic) serves three distinct grammatical functions in Interslavic. All three use the same form of the particle; the specific function is determined by the verb class and context.

Unverified Content

The (Reflexive Proper) and (Reciprocal) subsections are not supported by the primary source material, which only discusses the reflexive passive/impersonal use of sę (e.g., Pica dělaje se).

Reflexive Proper​

In the reflexive proper, sę signals that the action of the verb is directed back onto the subject — the subject acts on itself:

On myje sę. — "He washes himself." Ona oblěkajet sę. — "She dresses herself." Děti igrajut sę. — "The children play (occupy themselves)."

Reciprocal​

With a plural subject, sę may express a reciprocal meaning — the members of the group act on each other:

Oni ljubjat sę. — "They love each other."

Unverified Content

The form viděliь contains a Cyrillic soft sign (ь) mixed into an otherwise Latin-script example. This appears to be a character encoding error; the correct form would be viděli.

My viděliь sę. — "We saw each other." Oni sę borjut. — "They are fighting each other."

The reciprocal and reflexive readings may be ambiguous with plural subjects; context and the semantics of the verb typically disambiguate. An explicit reciprocal phrase jedin drugogo ("one another") may be used when disambiguation is needed.

Reflexive Passive (Impersonal Passive)​

Unverified Content

The claim that the reflexive passive is "one of the two preferred alternatives to the analytic passive" is partially supported by the source, which describes the reflexive construction as "even more common" than the 3pl impersonal. However, the source does not frame it as one of exactly two alternatives, and the claim about it being the preferred construction when the agent is "unknown or irrelevant" (in the paragraph below the examples) overstates what the source says.

The construction verb + sę with an inanimate object as the surface subject (or with no overt subject at all) expresses a reflexive passive or general impersonal meaning. This is one of the two preferred alternatives to the analytic passive (see the relevant section):

Pica dělaje sę. — "Pizza is being made." (lit. "Pizza makes itself.") To prodaje sę. — "That is sold / on sale." Kak to dělaje sę? — "How is that done?" Tuto govori sę po-russki. — "Russian is spoken here."

In this construction, the nominal participant (if present) may appear as nominative (grammatical subject) or remain implicit. The verb takes the 3sg form in either case. This construction is preferred over the full analytic passive when the agent is unknown or irrelevant.

Complement Control​

Unverified Content

The entire (Complement Control) section — including subject control, object control, and raising constructions — has no basis in the primary source material (grammar-syntax.md), which does not discuss control or raising constructions.

Control constructions involve a main (matrix) verb that selects an infinitive complement. The implicit (phonologically null) subject of the infinitive is obligatorily coreferent with a specific argument of the main verb — the controller. Interslavic has two types of control: subject control and object control.

Subject Control​

In subject-control constructions, the implicit subject of the infinitive is coreferent with the grammatical subject of the matrix verb. The controller is the nominative argument of the matrix verb.

Typical subject-control verbs include modal and volitional verbs:

VerbGloss
htětito want
moćito be able to, can
umětito know how to
načetito begin
prestatito stop, cease
staraje sęto try

On htěl idti. "He wanted to go." (he = subject of htěti = implicit subject of idti)

Ona načela čitati knigu. "She began to read a book." (she = subject of načeti = implicit subject of čitati)

My možemo pomoći. "We are able to help." (we = subject of moći = implicit subject of pomoći)

Object Control​

In object-control constructions, the implicit subject of the infinitive is coreferent with the direct or indirect object of the matrix verb. The controller is an accusative or dative argument of the matrix verb.

Typical object-control verbs include verbs of requesting, compelling, and permitting:

VerbGlossCase of controller
prositito ask, requestaccusative
zastavitito force, compelaccusative
pozvolitito allow, permitdative
povelětito command, orderdative

On prosil ju idti. "He asked her to go." (her = object of prositi = implicit subject of idti)

Oni zastavili nas rabotati. "They forced us to work." (us = object of zastaviti = implicit subject of rabotati)

Ona pozvolila dětem igrati sę. "She allowed the children to play." (children = dative object of pozvoliti = implicit subject of igrati)

Control vs. Raising​

Interslavic also has raising constructions, in which a nominal argument is syntactically the subject of the matrix verb but receives its thematic role from the embedded predicate. The most common raising predicate is the copula byti in its predicative function with adjectives:

On jest gotov idti. "He is ready to go." (on is thematically the subject of idti, not only of jest)

The distinction between raising and control is relevant for correctly analyzing agreement patterns and the interpretation of implicit arguments.
