Morphological Flavorization

Morphological flavorization selects among documented inflectional and derivational variants of Interslavic to increase familiarity for speakers of a particular dialect area. Unlike phonological flavorization, which operates on sounds, morphological flavorization operates on grammatical forms: endings, pronouns, prefixes, and prepositions.

All morphological variants specified in this chapter are optional. Standard Interslavic forms are always acceptable regardless of target audience.

The MUST requirement for intelligibility and the cross-reference to are not stated in the source material. The source discusses intelligibility as a general goal but does not formalize it as a normative constraint with a section reference.

A morphologically flavorized text MUST remain fully intelligible to any speaker of standard Interslavic .

Infinitive Ending: -ti vs. -ť​

Standard Interslavic uses the infinitive ending -ti, which is the form found across South Slavic, Czech, Slovak, and Ukrainian.

Rule for Northern flavor: The infinitive ending SHOULD be shortened to -ť.

Unverified Content

The characterization of Belarusian as -ць and the claim about Polish/Sorbian "tendency toward reduced endings" are not in the source material. The source simply states that Northern infinitives end in -ť.

This ending reflects the East Slavic pattern (Russian -ть, Belarusian -ць) and aligns with the Polish/Sorbian tendency toward reduced endings.

Adjectival Nominative/Accusative Neuter Singular: -o vs. -e​

Standard Interslavic uses the neuter singular adjective ending -o (e.g., dobro), which is the South Slavic dominant form.

Rule for Northern flavor: The neuter singular nominative/accusative adjectival ending SHOULD be changed to -e (e.g., dobre).

StandardNorthernGloss
dobro dětedobre detjaa good child
novo slovonove slovoa new word
staro městostare městoan old city

The ending change applies to all adjectival forms in the neuter nominative/accusative singular, including possessive, demonstrative, and interrogative pronouns used adjectivally.

Regional reference:

PolishUkrainianBulgarianCzechSerbian
-e-е-о-é-о
Unresolved: The Neuter -e/-o Tension

The neuter singular ending -o vs. -e is one of the most visible North–South splits in the Slavic world. The standard Interslavic choice of -o (following the South Slavic and East Slavic pattern for most adjective classes) means that Polish, Czech, and Slovak speakers encounter an unfamiliar ending in high-frequency words like dobro (where they expect dobre, dobré, dobré). The reverse choice would alienate South Slavic and some East Slavic speakers.

This tension extends beyond adjective endings to neuter noun forms and affects the overall "feel" of the language for different speaker groups. Some community members have proposed encoding both values with a special letter (analogous to the å compromise for the a/o alternation), which would allow each reader to interpret the vowel according to their own language's pattern. However, no concrete notation has been adopted, and the standard currently resolves the tension by choosing one form (-o) and documenting -e as a Northern flavorization option.

Relative Pronoun: ktory (North) vs. koj (South)​

Interslavic provides two relative pronouns:

  • ktory — primarily North Slavic in orientation (cf. Polish który, Russian который).
  • koj — primarily South Slavic in orientation (cf. Serbian/Croatian koji, Macedonian кој).

Both forms are standard and fully grammatical. Their choice is the primary relative-pronoun marker of Northern vs. Southern flavor.

Rule for Northern flavor: ktory SHOULD be preferred. Rule for Southern flavor: koj SHOULD be preferred.

Fem. sg. nom.ktorakoja

Directional Prefix: vy- (North) vs. iz- (South)​

Interslavic provides two competing prefixes expressing outward direction or origin:

  • vy- — North Slavic orientation (cf. Polish wy-, Russian вы-, Czech vy-).
  • iz- — South Slavic orientation (cf. Serbian/Croatian iz-, Slovene iz-).

Rule for Northern flavor: The prefix vy- SHOULD be used. Rule for Southern flavor: The prefix iz- SHOULD be used.

MeaningNorthernSouthern
to go outvyjtiizjti
to speak outvygovoritiizgovoriti
to write outvypisatiizpisati
to learnvyučitiizučiti

Both prefixes may co-occur in the standard dictionary as synonyms with the same meaning; the regional marking governs which is preferred in a given flavor register.

Purposive Preposition: dlja (North) vs. za (South) 'for'​

The preposition 'for' with purposive meaning is expressed by two competing forms:

  • dlja — North Slavic orientation (cf. Russian для, Polish dla, Ukrainian для).
  • za — South Slavic orientation (cf. Serbian/Croatian za, Slovene za, Bulgarian за).

Rule for Northern flavor: dlja SHOULD be used for purposive 'for'. Rule for Southern flavor: za SHOULD be used.

ContextNorthernSouthernGloss
for the peopledlja ljudejza ljudej—
for this reasondlja togoza to—
for a long timedlja dolgogo vremeneza dolgo vrěme—

Note: za has other meanings in standard Interslavic (behind, after, during) that are unaffected by this flavorization rule. Only the purposive 'for' sense is subject to Northern/Southern alternation.

dlaдлязаpro / proза

Avoiding Infinitives and Cases: Bulgarian/Macedonian Flavor​

Unverified Content

The source material only briefly mentions "avoiding the use of infinitives and noun cases when addressing Bulgarians" as an example of grammatical-level flavourisation. The detailed rules in this section — the da + finite verb substitution patterns, the constraint on accusative retention, the prohibition on postposed articles, and the MUST/MUST NOT normative requirements — are elaborations not present in the source.

Bulgarian and Macedonian have eliminated the inherited Slavic case system and the infinitive, replacing them with analytical constructions. Standard Interslavic uses full case morphology and the infinitive; these are obligatory in the standard, and MUST be used unless a Bulgarian/Macedonian-specific accommodation is explicitly applied.

Accommodation rule (Bulgarian/Macedonian flavor):

  1. Infinitive: Replace infinitive constructions with a finite verb introduced by the particle da + present tense.

    StandardBulgarian/Macedonian flavorGloss
    Hoču pisati.Hoču da pišu.I want to write.
    Trěba govoriti.Trěba da govoriš.You need to speak.
    Mogu čitati.Mogu da čitam.I can read.

  2. Case morphology: Where context permits, analytical prepositional constructions MAY substitute for synthetic case endings. For the accusative (direct object), no preposition is available in Interslavic; the accusative form is retained even in Bulgarian/Macedonian flavor.

  3. Definiteness: Bulgarian and Macedonian mark definiteness by a postposed article. Interslavic has no postposed article; this feature MUST NOT be introduced into flavorized Interslavic. Definiteness, where necessary, MAY be indicated by a demonstrative pronoun in prenominal position.

Critical constraint: This accommodation is intended strictly for texts directed at Bulgarian or Macedonian speakers who lack familiarity with case morphology. It MUST NOT be applied as a general simplification in other contexts; doing so would reduce the morphological integrity of the text without communicative justification.

Worked Morphological Flavorization Examples​

Unverified Content

These worked examples are not present in the source material. While the individual rules they demonstrate (infinitive shortening, prefix alternation, dlja/za substitution) are sourced, the specific sentences and especially the Bulgarian/Macedonian accommodation example (with da piše, može da) are original compositions that cannot be verified against the source.

The following parallel texts illustrate morphological flavorization. The base sentence is given in standard Interslavic; Northern and Southern variants follow with individual changes annotated.

Base sentence (standard Interslavic):

Dobry člověk hoče pisati dlǎgu knigu, da by ju izučiti.

Northern morphological flavorization:

Dobry člověk hoče pisať dljagu knigu, da by ju vyučiť.

Changes applied:

  • Infinitive -ti → -ť: pisati → pisať; izučiti → vyučiť
  • Prefix iz- → vy-: izučiti → vyučiť
  • Preposition dlja retained (Northern)

Southern morphological flavorization:

Dobri člověk hoče pisati za dugu knigu, da by ju izučiti.

Changes applied:

  • Neuter/masc. adjectival ending: dobry → dobri (South Slavic preference)
  • Preposition dlja → za (Southern)
  • Prefix iz- retained (Southern)
  • Infinitive -ti retained (Southern)

Bulgarian/Macedonian accommodation:

Dobri člověk hoče da piše za duga kniga, da može da ja izuči.

Changes applied:

  • Infinitive replaced by da + finite present: pisati → da piše
  • Infinitive replaced by da + finite present: izučiti → da izuči
  • da by modal replaced by može da (analytic modal)
