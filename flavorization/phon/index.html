Phonological Flavorization

Phonological flavorization makes use of the etymological alphabet — the set of optional letters whose standard pronunciation is fixed but whose realization varies historically across the Slavic dialect continuum. By writing and/or pronouncing these letters according to the reflexes of a particular dialect area, a text can be made more immediately familiar to speakers of that area without altering its standard Interslavic meaning.

All vowel and consonant substitutions specified in this chapter are optional. Their application is governed by the target audience, not by any intrinsic requirement of the language. Standard Interslavic remains correct regardless of whether flavorization is applied.

Vowel Substitutions​

y → i (Southern/Ukrainian Flavor)​

The phoneme /y/ (y in Latin) is distinct from /i/ in Russian, Belarusian, Polish, and Sorbian. In South Slavic languages, in Ukrainian, and in colloquial Czech and Slovak, /y/ and /i/ have merged into a single vowel.

Rule: When addressing an audience of South Slavic or Ukrainian speakers, all occurrences of y SHOULD be replaced by i.

This substitution has no effect on intelligibility for North Slavic speakers, since both y and i are already distinct in their languages and the standard form remains recoverable.

ě Realizations by Target Language​

The etymological vowel ě has excellent compromise qualities precisely because it has developed into different vowels in different Slavic languages. No single realization is optimal for all audiences simultaneously.

This contrast is why ě has compromise value: a Serb reads rěč as their own reč, a Croat as their own riječ, a Ukrainian as their own rič, all without any ambiguity.

ę → ja / a (Northern Flavor)​

In standard Interslavic, the etymological vowel ę is written and pronounced as e as a default simplification, which is a feature characteristic of South Slavic.

Rule for Northern flavor: The etymological ę SHOULD be realized as ja after a hard consonant, or as a after a soft consonant. This approximates the reflexes of North Slavic languages (cf. Russian я [ja], Polish ię/ą).

å → o (Northern Flavor)​

The etymological letter å does not occur as a separate phoneme in any modern Slavic language. Standard Interslavic uses a in this position, as do Old Church Slavonic, South Slavic, Czech, Slovak, and many Russian words.

Rule for Northern flavor: å SHOULD be replaced by o, reflecting the North Slavic (especially Russian and Polish) reflex of this etymological vowel.

Standard (å → a)Northern (å → o)Gloss
travatrovagrass
razumětirozumětito understand
kravakrovacow
glavaglovahead

Note: å occurs only between a liquid consonant (r or l) and a following consonant . The substitution å → o is therefore restricted to this specific phonotactic environment.

Czech and Slovak typically show ra/rá and la/lá reflexes in this position (e.g., Czech kráva, hlava, mráz), closely matching the standard Interslavic forms. In Russian, many words have both a native East Slavic form with polnoglasije (голова, борода, молоко) and a South Slavic borrowing via Old Church Slavonic (глава, блато, враг); in some cases one form has displaced the other, while in others both coexist with differentiated meanings (e.g., голова "head (anatomical)" vs. глава "chapter; leader").

Unverified Content

Slovene is a South Slavic language, not West Slavic as implied by "West Slavic (Slovene, Czech) character." The source correctly groups Slovene with "the remaining West Slavic languages" for the e reflex, but labeling the combined flavor as "West Slavic" while including Slovene is misleading.

Rule for West Slavic flavor: ȯ SHOULD be replaced by e, giving the text a West Slavic (Slovene, Czech) character. Rule for South Slavic / Croatian flavor: ȯ SHOULD be replaced by a.

Standard Interslavic uses o as the default realization of ȯ, which best serves East Slavic and most Slavic audiences.

Syllabic r → or / er (Northern/Eastern Flavor)​

In Czech, Slovak, Slovene, Serbo-Croatian, and Macedonian, the syllabic consonant /r/ is retained as a syllabic nucleus . In Russian, Polish, Ukrainian, and Belarusian, syllabic /r/ was historically resolved by inserting a vowel.

Rule for Northern/Eastern flavor:

  • Syllabic hard r SHOULD be written and pronounced as or.
  • Syllabic soft ŕ SHOULD be written and pronounced as er.

Consonant Substitutions​

g → h / ɦ (Ukrainian, Belarusian, Czech, Slovak Flavor)​

In Ukrainian, Belarusian, Czech, Slovak, and Upper Sorbian, the velar stop /g/ has shifted to a voiced velar or glottal fricative ɣ or ɦ. Standard Interslavic uses /g/ as the conservative form.

Rule: In texts oriented toward Ukrainian, Belarusian, Czech, or Slovak speakers, g MAY be pronounced as an aspirated or fricative h (ɣ or ɦ).

Unverified Content

The claim that this is a "pronunciation-level adaptation only" with the written form remaining g is not stated in the source material. The source simply notes the pronunciation difference without specifying whether the adaptation is limited to pronunciation or also applies to writing.

This is a pronunciation-level adaptation only; the written form remains g in standard and Northern Latin, unless a specific orthographic flavorization is also applied.

Optional Palatalized Consonants for Northern Flavor​

Standard Interslavic has two obligatory soft consonants: lj /lʲ/ and nj /nʲ/. The etymological alphabet provides five additional optional soft consonants: ĺ (ľ), ń, ŕ, t́ (ť), d́ (ď), ś, ź.

Rule for Northern flavor: All seven etymological soft consonants SHOULD be retained and written explicitly. This gives the text a distinctly North Slavic (Polish, Russian) character and maximizes intelligibility for readers accustomed to richly marked palatalization.

The five additional etymological consonants (ť, ď, ś, ź, ŕ) are largely absent from South Slavic; ś and ź are also absent from standard Czech and Slovak; ŕ is absent from Slovak and Belarusian. Their use is therefore a Northern-oriented choice.

Orthographic note: When marking all soft consonants consistently:

  • In Latin: use the acute accent or háček consistently: ť, ď, ś, ź, ŕ, ĺ (or ľ), ń.

An alternative Latin convention, used in Polish, Sorbian, and certain forms of Belarusian Łacinka and Ukrainian Latynka, is to use l for the soft [ʎ] and ł for the hard [l]. This convention MAY be used in place of ĺ / ľ.

ć đ Retention (Southern Flavor)​

The etymological affricates ć /t͡ɕ/ and đ /d͡ʑ/ are, in standard Interslavic, written and pronounced as č and dž respectively . Unlike other iotated consonants (*lj, *nj, *rj), the reflexes of Proto-Slavic *tj and *dj have diverged dramatically across Slavic languages, making these among the most variable correspondences in the family. In South Slavic languages — particularly Serbian and Croatian — ć and đ are distinct phonemes.

The following table shows the reflexes of Proto-Slavic *tj (= ISV ć) and *dj (= ISV đ) in the major Slavic languages:

Rule for Southern flavor: The etymological ć and đ MAY be retained in both writing and pronunciation.

Critical constraint: ć and đ MUST NOT be used for the sequences tj and dj (soft t and soft d). These are distinct etymological entities. Confusing them constitutes an error, not a flavor variant.

Epenthetic L (Southern vs. Northern/Western Flavor)​

In a number of Slavic words, an epenthetic (inserted) l appears between a labial or dental consonant and the glide j. This epenthetic l is present in East Slavic (Russian, Ukrainian, Belarusian), Shtokavian (Serbian/Croatian/Bosnian), and Slovenian, but absent in West Slavic (Polish, Czech, Slovak) and in Bulgarian and Macedonian.

The phenomenon is most visible in words like zemlja "earth/land" (with epenthetic L) vs. the West Slavic forms ziemia (Polish), země (Czech), zem (Slovak), and the Bulgarian/Macedonian земя/земја. Other common examples include zdravlje "health" and verb forms like dobavljati "to supply."

Standard Interslavic includes the epenthetic l in these words (following the majority pattern), which means that West Slavic and Bulgarian/Macedonian speakers encounter a consonant cluster absent from their own languages. This is one of several points where the "middle ground" of Interslavic necessarily leans toward one group of languages at the expense of another.

Unresolved: Epenthetic L Encoding

The epenthetic l in words like zemlja, zdravlje, dobavljati is present in East Slavic, Shtokavian, and Slovenian, but absent in West Slavic, Bulgarian, and Macedonian. Standard Interslavic includes it (following the numerical majority), but this choice is not encoded in the etymological alphabet — there is no diacritic or special notation to flag the l as epenthetic rather than original. This means automated flavorization cannot determine whether a given lj sequence should be preserved (for East Slavic / South-West Slavic audiences) or stripped (for West Slavic / Bulgarian / Macedonian audiences) without external etymological data. Encoding this distinction — for example, with a marked variant of l in the scientific register — would improve flavorization accuracy but would add complexity to the already large etymological inventory.

šč → št (Southern Flavor)​

The consonant cluster šč occurs in standard Interslavic. In South Slavic, this cluster has merged to št.

Rule for Southern flavor: The sequence šč SHOULD be replaced by št.
