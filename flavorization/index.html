Flavorization

Interslavic is designed to occupy the centre of the Slavic linguistic space, offering solutions that serve the broadest possible range of Slavic speakers. Those solutions are not always equally optimal for every subgroup. What is maximally intelligible in Bulgaria or Croatia is not necessarily maximally intelligible in Poland or Russia. Flavorization is the systematic mechanism by which Interslavic is adapted — on one or more linguistic dimensions — to increase intelligibility or familiarity for a specific audience, without abandoning the language's identity as Interslavic.

The term flavorization (flavorizacija) denotes the deliberate addition of regional colour to a text or utterance. A flavorized text remains Interslavic: it is not a translation into a national language, and it MUST remain fully intelligible to any speaker of standard Interslavic.

Definition of Flavorization​

Flavorization is the systematic adaptation of Interslavic on phonological, morphological, lexical, or orthographic levels to increase intelligibility or familiarity for a specific subgroup of Slavic speakers.

The following normative constraints apply:

  • Flavorization is optional in all registers; standard Interslavic is always acceptable.
  • Flavorization MAY be applied on any single dimension or on multiple dimensions simultaneously.
  • A flavorized text MUST remain mutually intelligible with standard Interslavic (see the relevant section).
  • Flavorization is not the creation of a separate language or dialect in the sociolinguistic sense; it is a stylistic and communicative register within Interslavic.

The process is bidirectional in practice: a speaker's native language inevitably influences the Interslavic they produce, even without deliberate flavorization. The normative framework in this chapter concerns deliberate, rule-governed adaptation; incidental native-language influence is not regulated.

Flavorization Levels: Four Dimensions​

Flavorization MAY be applied independently on any of the following four dimensions:

DimensionDescriptionExample
PhonologicalSubstitution of phonemes or sound patterns according to regional reflexesy → i for South Slavic audiences
MorphologicalSelection of inflectional or derivational variants that favor particular dialect areasinfinitive -ť (Northern) vs. -ti (standard)
LexicalChoice among regional synonyms documented in the Interslavic dictionaryktory (Northern) vs. koj (Southern)
OrthographicAdaptation of spelling conventions to resemble a familiar national orthographysz, cz conventions for Polish readers

The four dimensions are fully independent: a text MAY apply phonological and lexical flavorization without any orthographic or morphological adjustment, and vice versa. However, canonical flavor models specify coordinated settings across multiple dimensions.

Mutual Intelligibility Threshold​

The normative constraint on flavorization is intelligibility.

A flavorized Interslavic text MUST remain mutually intelligible with standard Interslavic.

The operational threshold is: a speaker of standard Interslavic SHOULD be able to understand a fully flavorized text with no more difficulty than the difficulty of understanding a regional variety of their own language.

This constraint implies:

  1. Phonological substitutions MUST be systematic and reversible; no substitution MAY introduce phonemic ambiguity that destroys lexical contrast.
  2. Morphological variants MUST remain within the paradigm space documented in the relevant section; ad-hoc morphological inventions are not flavorization.
  3. Lexical choices MUST draw from the documented Interslavic dictionary; they MUST NOT introduce words that are borrowed exclusively from a single national language without Slavic etymological grounding.
  4. Orthographic variants MUST map to standard Interslavic in a one-to-one or rule-governed fashion; the mapping MUST be recoverable by any reader of the standard.

Flavorized texts that violate these constraints are not flavorized Interslavic; they are national-language texts or code-switching.

Two Canonical Flavor Models: Northern and Southern​

For practical use, Interslavic defines two canonical flavor models. These models represent coordinated settings across all four dimensions and are intended to cover the most common communicative scenarios.

PropertyNorthern ModelSouthern Model
Primary target audienceRussians, Belarusians, Poles, SorbsSerbs, Croats, Slovenes, Bulgarians, Macedonians
Secondary target audienceCzechs, Slovaks, UkrainiansCzechs, Slovaks, Ukrainians
Phonological center of gravityEast and West Slavic reflexesSouth Slavic reflexes
Morphological featuresInfinitive -ť; neuter adj. -e; relative pronoun ktoryInfinitive -ti; neuter adj. -o; relative pronoun koj
Key lexical alternationsPrefix vy-; preposition dljaPrefix iz-; preposition za
Orthographic conventionsEast Slavic Cyrillic or Polish-based LatinCroatian/Slovene Latin or South Slavic Cyrillic

These two models are not exhaustive. The full combinatorial space of phonological, morphological, lexical, and orthographic variants is much larger, and a text oriented toward Czech readers, for instance, may apply a distinct combination that does not map cleanly to either canonical model. The canonical models serve as reference points and as a practical shorthand in this specification.
